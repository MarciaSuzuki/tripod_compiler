# J02 — Jonah 1:4–2:1

## 1. Metadata
- **Pericope title:** The storm finds Jonah, and the sea is stilled
- **BCV:** Jonah 1:4–2:1
- **Genre Group:** NARRATIVE
- **Genre:** HISTORICAL_NARRATIVE
- **Register:** INFORMAL_CASUAL

*(Versification note: English Jonah 1:17–2:1 is Hebrew 2:1–2. The verse references below are English.)*

**Multi-level register tagging:**
The whole passage sits in INFORMAL_CASUAL, the plain default for the biblical narrator's voice. Three moment-level shifts fall inside the speech: the captain's rebuke and charge in v.6 ("get up, cry out to your god") lifts into ELDER_AUTHORITY — a superior aboard giving an order, and his words echo the command Jonah fled in 1:2. The sailors' deliberations among themselves in v.7 ("come, let us cast lots") and toward Jonah in v.11 ("what shall we do to you?") sit in CONSULTATIVE — equals working a problem together; CONSULTATIVE is never used for the narrator's own voice. The crew's prayer to YHWH in v.14 ("we beg, YHWH, do not let us perish") lifts into RELIGIOUS_WORSHIP — a petition addressed directly to God.

## 2. Level 1 — Whole-Passage Movement

### 2.1 Prose Arc / Shape / Argument / Burden / Concern
YHWH hurls a great wind at the sea, and the storm that follows hunts one man through a whole crew. The sailors do everything — pray, jettison, cast lots, interrogate, row — while the runaway sleeps below; the lot finds Jonah, his own mouth confesses the God he is fleeing, and his only counsel is "hurl me in." Hurled at last into the sea, the storm stops dead, the pagan crew worships YHWH, and YHWH appoints a great fish to swallow the man. What weighs on the passage is the reach of the God Jonah is running from: the sea, the lot, and the fish all answer to him, and the flight ends exactly where he was first told to get up — only now it is the sea's bottom, not Nineveh.

### 2.2 Context
This continues J01 directly: Jonah is aboard the ship he paid for, running for Tarshish, away from before YHWH. The crew are working seamen of no stated nation, each with his own god — ordinary pagan sailors, not Israelites. Casting lots was a recognized way to ask the gods who was to blame for a calamity. To "fear YHWH" is the standard way of saying one worships him; the storm makes the word literal. Innocent blood — killing a man who has done you no wrong — is a guilt the crew begs YHWH not to lay on them before they throw him in.

### 2.3 Emotion / Tone / Pace
Alarm builds in waves and the irony rides on top of it. The sailors fear, then fear a great fear, then fear YHWH a great fear — three steps up; twice the narrator says the sea "kept growing stormier." Against that swell, the prophet of YHWH is fast asleep below while every pagan aboard is praying, and the man who says "I fear YHWH, who made the sea" is the one fleeing him across it. The pace is fast and crowded — verbs tumble over each other — until v.15: Jonah goes over the side and the sea stands still from its raging, a sudden flat calm. The close is quiet awe: sacrifice, vows, and then the great fish, three days and three nights of silence.

### 2.4 Communicative Function
The passage carries the flight of J01 into its consequence: the runaway is found, named, and surrendered to the sea, and the God he fled is feared and worshiped by pagans because of it. It hands the story its central irony — the heathen crew prays, rows, and sacrifices while YHWH's prophet sleeps, deflects, and asks to die rather than turn back. It sets up the psalm that follows: the fish is rescue wearing the shape of judgment, and the prayer Jonah never prayed on deck begins in the fish's belly. To speak this passage is to keep the storm, the lot, the confession, the hurling, the calm, the crew's worship, and the swallowing in their order — cause pressing on effect until the sea goes still.

**3C — Objects and Concepts**

[[O1-Ship]] — אֳנִיָּה / the ship
- What it is: the seagoing vessel Jonah boarded in J01
- Function in scene: caught in the storm and near breaking; Jonah sleeps in its far hold

[[O3-Wind]] — רוּחַ גְּדוֹלָה / a great wind

[[O4-Storm]] — סַעַר גָּדוֹל / a great storm

- Function in scene: the danger the whole scene answers; it nearly breaks the ship

[[O5-Cargo]] — הַכֵּלִים / the goods in the ship
- What it is: the ship's freight and gear
- Function in scene: hurled into the sea to lighten the ship — the voyage's worth thrown away to survive

**3D — Times**
- None: no distinct time-setting; the storm breaks in on the voyage already underway.

**3E — What Happens**
[[B2-YHWH]] YHWH hurls a great [[O3-Wind]] wind at the [[PL4-Sea]] sea, and a great [[O4-Storm]] storm rises — the [[O1-Ship]] ship is about to break apart. The [[B4-Ship-Crew]] sailors are afraid; each cries to his own god, and they hurl the [[O5-Cargo]] cargo into the sea to lighten the ship. [[B1-Jonah]] Jonah, meanwhile, has gone down into the far hold, lain down, and fallen fast asleep. The [[B6-Captain]] captain comes to him: "What are you doing, sleeper? Get up, cry out to your god — maybe that god will give us a thought and we will not perish."

**3F — Communicative Purpose**
Opens the consequence: the storm is YHWH's answer to the flight, and it lands on everyone aboard. Sets the irony — pagans praying, the prophet asleep — and puts the command Jonah fled ("get up, cry out") back in his ears from a pagan captain's mouth.

**Significant Absence**
Jonah does not pray — in a scene where every other man aboard is crying to a god, the prophet alone is silent, and he stays prayerless through the whole storm. The narrator never says why he sleeps.

### Scene 2 — The lot finds him, and he confesses (1:7–10)

**3A — Beings**
[[B4-Ship-Crew]] — הָאֲנָשִׁים / the men
- Role: the ones who cast lots, question Jonah, and fear the great fear
- Relationship: the same crew, now dealing with the man the lot found
- Presence: PRESENT
- Referential form: from v.10 the narrator calls them "the men" — no longer "the sailors"

[[B1-Jonah]] — יוֹנָה / Jonah
- Role: the one the lot falls on; the questioned man; the confessor
- Relationship: the cause of the calamity, now exposed
- Presence: PRESENT
- Referential form: he names himself "a Hebrew" — his people, not his calling

[[B2-YHWH]] — יְהוָה / YHWH
- Role: the God Jonah confesses — maker of the sea and the dry land
- Relationship: the God of heaven Jonah fears yet flees
- Presence: REFERENCED

**3B — Places**
[[PL4-Sea]] — הַיָּם / the sea
- Role: what Jonah's God is confessed to have made
- Type: open water, still raging
- Effect on scene: the confession names its maker while it rages — the threat and the theology are the same water

[[PL5-Dry-Land]] — הַיַּבָּשָׁה / the dry land
- Role: the other half of the confession — YHWH made the sea and the dry land
- Type: the safe ground, far off
- Effect on scene: named only as what YHWH made; the men will soon row for it and fail

**3C — Objects and Concepts**

[[O6-Lots]] — גּוֹרָלוֹת / lots
- What it is: the casting pieces used to ask who is to blame
- Function in scene: the means of exposure — the lot falls on Jonah

[[CB_0053-Fear-of-YHWH]] — יִרְאָה / fear of YHWH
- What it is: the worship-fear of YHWH — taking him seriously
- Function in scene: what Jonah claims in his confession while fleeing; the men's great fear answers it

[[CB_0052-Before-the-Face-of-YHWH]] — מִלִּפְנֵי יְהוָה / from before YHWH
- What it is: the presence of YHWH — the same presence Jonah fled in J01
- Function in scene: what the men learn Jonah is running from; the knowledge behind their great fear

**3D — Times**
- None: no distinct time-setting; the scene runs straight on inside the storm.

**3E — What Happens**
The [[B4-Ship-Crew]] men say to one another: come, let us cast [[O6-Lots]] lots, to know on whose account this evil is on us. They cast, and the lot falls on [[B1-Jonah]] Jonah. They press him: tell us — whose fault is this? what is your work? where do you come from? what is your land? from which people are you? He answers: "I am a Hebrew, and I fear [[B2-YHWH]] YHWH, God of heaven, who made the [[PL4-Sea]] sea and the [[PL5-Dry-Land]] dry land." The men fear a great fear and say, "What is this you have done!" — for they know he is fleeing from before YHWH, because he had told them.

**3F — Communicative Purpose**
Exposes the runaway by his own God's means (the lot) and his own mouth (the confession). The pagans take YHWH more seriously than his prophet does — their fear is great while his flight stands.

**Significant Absence**
Asked plainly "what is your work?", Jonah never answers it — he gives his people and his God, but not his calling. The narrator lets the word "prophet" go entirely unsaid. Jonah confesses that he fears YHWH, yet the narrator records fear in everyone aboard except him.

### Scene 3 — "Hurl me in" — and they row instead (1:11–13)

**3A — Beings**
[[B4-Ship-Crew]] — הָאֲנָשִׁים / the men
- Role: the ones asking what to do, then rowing hard for shore to spare him
- Relationship: the crew, now holding Jonah's life in the question
- Presence: PRESENT

[[B1-Jonah]] — יוֹנָה / Jonah
- Role: the one who says: lift me and hurl me into the sea
- Relationship: the owner of the storm — "I know this great storm is on you on my account"
- Presence: PRESENT

**3B — Places**
[[PL4-Sea]] — הַיָּם / the sea
- Role: the place Jonah says he must be hurled into
- Type: open water, growing stormier
- Effect on scene: it presses the choice — twice the narrator says it "kept growing stormier"

[[PL5-Dry-Land]] — הַיַּבָּשָׁה / the dry land
- Role: where the men row for and cannot reach
- Type: the safe ground
- Effect on scene: the rowing fails against the sea — there is no way back that spares the man

**3C — Objects and Concepts**

[[O4-Storm]] — הַסַּעַר הַגָּדוֹל / this great storm
- What it is: the same tempest, now named by Jonah as his
- Function in scene: what his words own — "on my account this great storm is on you"

**3D — Times**
- None: no distinct time-setting; the sea is still rising.

**3E — What Happens**
The [[B4-Ship-Crew]] men ask [[B1-Jonah]] Jonah: what shall we do to you, so the [[PL4-Sea]] sea will quiet down for us? — for the sea keeps growing stormier. He answers: lift me and hurl me into the sea, and the sea will quiet for you; I know this great [[O4-Storm]] storm is on you on my account. But the men dig their oars in to get back to the [[PL5-Dry-Land]] dry land — and they cannot, for the sea keeps growing stormier against them.

**3F — Communicative Purpose**
Puts the cost in the open: the storm wants its man. The pagans try to save the runaway at their own risk before they will throw him; his counsel is to be thrown, not to turn back.

**Significant Absence**
Jonah never offers to pray, repent, or be taken back to his errand — the one thing that is never said is "take me to Nineveh." He owns the storm ("on my account") but never names the flight as wrong; there is no word of sin and no word of sorrow.

### Scene 4 — Overboard: the sea stands still, and the men worship (1:14–16)

**3A — Beings**
[[B4-Ship-Crew]] — הָאֲנָשִׁים / the men
- Role: the ones who pray, hurl Jonah in, and then fear and worship YHWH
- Relationship: pagan crew turned, by the storm's end, toward Jonah's God
- Presence: PRESENT

[[B2-YHWH]] — יְהוָה / YHWH
- Role: the one prayed to; the one whose pleasure the storm carried out; the one feared and worshiped at the end
- Relationship: no longer only Jonah's God — the crew cries to him by name
- Presence: PRESENT

[[B1-Jonah]] — יוֹנָה / Jonah
- Role: the man lifted and hurled into the sea
- Relationship: "this man" in the crew's prayer — a life they beg not to be charged with
- Presence: PRESENT
- Referential form: in the prayer he is "this man," a soul that might be charged as innocent blood

**3B — Places**
[[PL4-Sea]] — הַיָּם / the sea
- Role: where Jonah is hurled; the water that stands still from its raging
- Type: open water
- Effect on scene: its sudden calm is the verdict — the storm wanted this one man

**3C — Objects and Concepts**

[[CB_0054-Innocent-Blood]] — דָּם נָקִיא / innocent blood
- What it is: the guilt of taking a blameless life
- Function in scene: what the men beg YHWH not to lay on them before they hurl Jonah in

[[CB_0053-Fear-of-YHWH]] — יִרְאָה גְדוֹלָה / a great fear of YHWH
- What it is: the worship-fear of YHWH, now arrived in full
- Function in scene: what the calm produces in the men — fear of YHWH, sacrifice, and vows

**3D — Times**
- None: no distinct time-setting; prayer, hurling, and calm run as one motion.

**3E — What Happens**
The [[B4-Ship-Crew]] men cry to [[B2-YHWH]] YHWH: we beg, YHWH — do not let us perish for this man's life, and do not lay innocent blood on us; for you, YHWH, have done as you pleased. Then they lift [[B1-Jonah]] Jonah and hurl him into the [[PL4-Sea]] sea — and the sea stands still from its raging. And the men fear YHWH with a great fear, and they offer a sacrifice to YHWH, and they vow vows.

**3F — Communicative Purpose**
Closes the storm with its point made: the sea answers to YHWH, and the pagan crew ends the scene doing everything Israel's prophet would not — praying to YHWH, fearing him, sacrificing, vowing. The flight has made worshipers of strangers.

**Significant Absence**
Jonah says nothing from the moment the men row for his life to the moment the water closes over him — no protest, no thanks, no prayer, no farewell. The crew's own fate after the calm is never told; the story leaves them mid-sea, vows in hand, and follows the man overboard.

### Scene 5 — The fish: swallowed, three days, and a prayer begins (1:17–2:1; Heb 2:1–2)

**3A — Beings**
[[B2-YHWH]] — יְהוָה / YHWH
- Role: the one who appoints the great fish
- Relationship: the same hand behind wind, storm, lot, and calm — now behind the rescue
- Presence: PRESENT

[[B7-Great-Fish]] — דָּג גָּדוֹל / the great fish
- Role: the appointed swallower; Jonah's holder for three days and three nights
- Relationship: YHWH's creature, doing YHWH's errand without argument
- Presence: PRESENT

[[B1-Jonah]] — יוֹנָה / Jonah
- Role: the swallowed man; at last, the one who prays
- Relationship: the runaway carried, alive, inside the fish
- Presence: PRESENT

**3B — Places**
[[PL4-Sea]] — הַיָּם / the sea
- Role: the deep the fish swims; where Jonah now is
- Type: open water, now calm above him
- Effect on scene: the flight's end — the runaway is below the sea he was hurled into

**3C — Objects and Concepts**

[[TH_THREE_DAYS_AND_THREE_NIGHTS]] — שְׁלֹשָׁה יָמִים וּשְׁלֹשָׁה לֵילוֹת / three days and three nights
- What it is: the span Jonah is inside the fish
- Function in scene: the story's held breath — the time between the swallowing and the prayer

**3D — Times**
- None: the three days and three nights are content the story refers to (listed above), not a time-setting for the scene; the scene has no distinct temporal frame.

**3E — What Happens**
[[B2-YHWH]] YHWH appoints a [[B7-Great-Fish]] great fish to swallow [[B1-Jonah]] Jonah, and the fish swallows him. Jonah is in the belly of the fish three days and three nights. Then Jonah prays to YHWH his God from the belly of the fish.

**3F — Communicative Purpose**
Turns judgment into keeping: the man given to the sea is not drowned but held. Ends the pericope at the hinge — the prayer the prophet never prayed on deck begins in the fish, and the psalm (J03) is that prayer.

**Significant Absence**
No rescue is announced — the narrator never says "YHWH saved him"; there is only a fish, appointed. Nothing is said of the three days — no word, no thought, no fear is recorded inside the fish until the prayer breaks the silence.
