# P11 — Ruth 4:1-8

## 1. Metadata
- **Pericope title:** The gate: the non-name, the field before Ruth, and the sandal
- **BCV:** Ruth 4:1-8
- **Genre Group:** NARRATIVE
- **Genre:** HISTORICAL_NARRATIVE
- **Register:** INFORMAL_CASUAL

*(Versification note: Ruth's chapter and verse numbers are the same in English and Hebrew — no divergence in this pericope.)*

**Multi-level register tagging:**
The whole passage sits in INFORMAL_CASUAL. Scenes 2 through 4 shift to FORMAL_OFFICIAL at scene level: from the moment the ten elders are seated, the talk is a legal proceeding at the town gate — offer, terms, declination, and the attestation act, all spoken before witnesses (the register the royal decree carried in Jonah's Nineveh). Scene 1 stays in the plain narrative key: the convening — Boaz sitting down, the redeemer passing, "turn aside, sit here" — is brisk staging, not yet the proceeding. The narrator's aside at v.7, explaining the old custom to a later audience, stays the narrator's own plain voice inside the formal scene.

## 2. Level 1 — Whole-Passage Movement

### 2.1 Prose Arc / Shape / Argument / Burden / Concern
The passage is the morning Naomi promised: the man does not rest. Boaz goes up to the gate and sits down — and behold, the redeemer of whom he spoke is passing. He calls him to turn aside and sit, naming him with a non-name — friend So-and-so — and seats ten men of the town's elders. Then the matter, laid out in two stages. First the field: the portion of our brother Elimelech, which Naomi, back from the fields of Moab, is selling — buy it before those seated and before the elders of my people; if you will redeem, redeem; if not, tell me, for there is no one besides you, and I am after you. The man says: I will redeem. Then the second stage: on the day you buy the field from Naomi's hand, you also acquire Ruth the Moabite, the wife of the dead, to raise up the name of the dead upon his inheritance. And the redeemer turns back: I cannot redeem it for myself, lest I ruin my own inheritance — you take my right of redemption. The narrator pauses to explain the old way in Israel — for redeeming and for exchanging, a man drew off his sandal and gave it to his fellow; this was the attestation. So the redeemer says to Boaz, buy it for yourself — and draws off his sandal. What weighs on the passage is the order of the telling: the field first, Ruth second — the disclosure staged so that the claim that drew the man in is the one he cannot keep. The question it leaves hanging for one more scene is the word no one has yet said over the bargain: a blessing, a name, a future.

### 2.2 Context
This is the scene the whole book has been walking toward, fulfilling Naomi's word from the night before — the man will not rest unless he has finished the matter today. The chance that put Ruth in Boaz's field at 2:3 has its twin here: Boaz sits down at the gate, and behold, the very redeemer passes by. The nearer redeemer Boaz disclosed in the dark (3:12) now sits in daylight — and the narrator, who names everyone in this book with care, gives him no name at all: peloni almoni, friend So-and-so, the non-name of the man who would not preserve a name. Naomi's selling of Elimelech's portion is the first the audience hears of a field to sell — the legal substance under the whole story surfaces only now. Ruth is named "the Moabite, the wife of the dead": her husband still goes unnamed, one scene longer. And through all eight verses, neither woman is present, and the divine name is not spoken — the gate does its work in purely human words; the blessing waits for the next scene.

### 2.3 Emotion / Tone / Pace
Brisk, public, and procedural — the opposite key from the night before. The staging moves in quick commands: turn aside, sit here, sit here. The offer is business-like, the first answer confident — I will redeem. The second stage lands like a held card played: one verse from Boaz, and the confident answer reverses into I cannot. The narrator's antiquarian aside slows the scene deliberately before its one physical act, so that the sandal comes off in full view and unhurried. Underneath the procedure runs the irony the audience carries from chapter 3: the careful, bloodless terms at the gate are deciding the marriage asked for in the dark.

### 2.4 Communicative Function
This passage resolves the redeemer-queue opened at 2:20 and sharpened at 3:12: the nearer man declines, and the right passes to Boaz with a sandal for witness. It stages the disclosure — the field, then Ruth — so the legal drama hinges on the order of the telling. It deepens the book's naming-pattern to its limit: the man who will not raise the dead's name gets no name himself, while Ruth is named again as the Moabite at the exact moment her future is decided. It opens the raise-up-the-name formula the attestation scene will complete, keeps both women offstage and the divine name silent so the next scene's blessing lands with full weight, and explains the sandal-custom so a later audience can read the act. To speak this passage is to keep the procedure's plainness, the staged order, the non-name, and the silence where the women and the blessing will be.
