# E16 — Esther 9:11–19

## 1. Metadata
- **Pericope title:** The Susa toll, Esther's second request, and the two days of feasting
- **BCV:** Esther 9:11–19
- **Genre Group:** NARRATIVE
- **Genre:** HISTORICAL_NARRATIVE
- **Register:** INFORMAL_CASUAL

**Multi-level register tagging:**
The passage sits in INFORMAL_CASUAL throughout — the storyteller's plain voice, reporting a toll, a request, and a feast from the outside. There is a good deal of quoted speech here (the king to Esther, Esther back to the king, the king's word of consent), but it is conversation in a palace, not a written decree, so it stays INFORMAL_CASUAL — the same register the narration is in. One moment in v.14 brushes against the formal key: the king *says* the thing should be done, and "a decree was given out (*dat*) in Susa." That is the narrator reporting that an order issued, not the order's own words being read out; so I have kept it INFORMAL_CASUAL and flagged it as a meaning call below — there is no edict *text* voiced here for a FORMAL_OFFICIAL override to land on. The last verses (17–19) lift slightly toward the book's closing COMMUNITY_MEMORY frame — "this is why the Jews of the open towns keep the fourteenth…" — but that full framing belongs to the next pericope (the formal institution of Purim), so here it is only a first touch, kept INFORMAL_CASUAL.

## 2. Level 1 — Whole-Passage Movement
### 2.1 Prose Arc / Shape / Argument / Burden / Concern
The passage opens with a count being brought in — the number of the dead in the capital — and from that count the whole movement unfolds. The king, almost marvelling, tells the queen what has happened in Susa alone and asks her, once more, what she still wants; and she asks for two hard things: one more day for the Jews of Susa to act, and the bodies of Haman's ten sons hung up on display. Both are granted. Then the killing happens a second time in the capital, and — said twice over, pointedly — the Jews lay no hand on the plunder. The angle then pulls back from the capital to the whole empire, where the provincial Jews had already finished in a single day and killed many times more. And the passage closes not on blood but on the calendar: who rested when, who feasted when — the open-country Jews on the fourteenth, the Susa Jews on the fifteenth — so that the story's burden, by its end, has quietly turned from slaughter to the founding of a yearly day of gladness, eating, and gifts of food between neighbours.

### 2.2 Context
This falls near the very end of the book of Esther, on the days of the Jews' self-defence (the thirteenth of Adar and after), the reversal of the decree Haman had set in motion. By now the danger has turned: the law that was meant to destroy the Jews has been answered by a counter-law letting them stand for their lives, and these verses report the result. The reader already knows Esther the queen, the king Ahasuerus, Haman the fallen enemy and his ten sons, and the capital Susa with its citadel. What this passage adds is the bookkeeping of the deliverance — the toll, the second day, the refusal of plunder — and, most of all, the seed of the festival the next pericope will name and fix: Purim. God is still nowhere named; the rescue runs, as always in this book, through a queen's request and a king's consent and the turning of dates on a calendar.

### 2.3 Emotion / Tone / Pace
The tone is sober and exact, the voice of a record-keeper, but with a current of grim satisfaction running under it. The pace shifts as it goes. It opens slow and close-up — a number carried in, a king's wondering question, a queen's measured answer — almost intimate. Then it speeds and widens: the second day's three hundred in Susa, the seventy-five thousand across the provinces, told fast and flat. Twice the narrator stops the count to note the same restraint — "but on the plunder they did not lay their hand" — and that repeated line carries the moral weight, marking this as defence and not greed. At the close the rhythm settles into something almost liturgical: rest, gladness, feasting, a good day, sending portions to one another — the shape of a festival forming out of the shape of a battle.

### 2.4 Communicative Function
The passage does two things at once. It closes the account of the fighting — tallying the dead, granting the queen's last requests, and twice insisting the Jews took no spoil — and it lays the groundwork for the feast. Its real work is etiological: it explains, by who rested on which day, why there come to be *two* days of Purim — the fourteenth kept by the Jews of the unwalled towns, the fifteenth (as the next pericope will draw out) by the Jews of Susa. To speak this passage is to move the hearer from the last of the killing into the first of the keeping: from a count of bodies to a day of gladness, feasting, and shared food — the hinge on which the book turns toward its festival.

- Signals: the calendar seam that will divide the two Purim dates

**3E — What Happens**
On that day the count of the slain in [[PL1-Susa-the-citadel]] Susa the citadel was brought before [[B4-Ahasuerus]] the king. The king said to [[B2-Esther]] Esther the queen: in Susa the citadel [[B17-the-Jews]] the Jews have killed and destroyed five hundred men, and [[B16-the-ten-sons-of-Haman]] the ten sons of Haman — what, then, have they done in the rest of the king's provinces? Now what is your request? — it will be granted you; what more do you ask? — it will be done. [[B2-Esther]] Esther said: if it please the king, let tomorrow also be granted to the Jews who are in Susa, to act according to today's decree; and let the ten sons of Haman be hung on [[TH_GALLOWS]] the tree. The king said it should be done so; a decree was given out in Susa, and the ten sons of Haman they hung up.

**3F — Communicative Purpose**
Reports the capital's death-toll and stages the king's granting of Esther's last two requests — a second day and the public hanging of the enemy's sons.

**Significant Absence**
No God is named — not in the king's wondering question, not in the queen's measured request, not over the granting. The deliverance is asked for and given entirely in the human key of court speech. And Esther gives no reason for either request — no anger voiced, no vengeance named; the motive behind the second day and the hanging is left unspoken, carried only by the bare asking.

### Scene 2 — The second day in Susa and the provincial reckoning (v.15–16)

- Relationship: the foes of the Jewish people across the provinces

**3B — Places**
[[PL1-Susa-the-citadel]] — שׁוּשָׁן / Susa
- Role: the capital where the second day's three hundred are killed
- Type: royal capital
- Meaning: here the city of the extra, fourteenth-day action — the thing that sets Susa apart

The king's provinces — מְדִינוֹת הַמֶּלֶךְ / the king's provinces *(scene content — the empire-wide setting; cf. [[I5-The-royal-provinces-the-127]])*

- Type: the realm beyond the capital
- Meaning: the 127 provinces, set over against Susa to frame the two timings
- Effect on scene: widens the count from the city to the whole empire

**3C — Objects and Elements**
The plunder not taken — בִּזָּה / spoil *(scene content — the load-bearing restraint, stated twice)*
- What it is: the goods of the slain, which the Jews were permitted to seize but did not
- Function in scene: twice the narrator marks that "on the plunder they did not lay their hand" — in Susa and in the provinces alike
- Signals: defence, not greed — the moral seal on the whole action; repeated, and so weighted

**3D — Times**
"The fourteenth of the month of Adar" — בְּיוֹם אַרְבָּעָה עָשָׂר לְחֹדֶשׁ אֲדָר / on the fourteenth day of the month of Adar *(content time — anchored to registered month [[TM_ADAR]] Adar)*
- What it is: the second day in Susa, when the three hundred are killed
- Function: fixes the capital's extra day on the calendar
- Signals: the date that will become one of the two Purim days

"The thirteenth of the month of Adar" — בְּיוֹם שְׁלֹשָׁה עָשָׂר לְחֹדֶשׁ אֲדָר / on the thirteenth day of the month of Adar *(content time — anchored to registered month [[TM_ADAR]] Adar)*
- What it is: the single day on which the provincial Jews acted
- Function: fixes the provinces' one day of action, against Susa's two
- Signals: the other date — the one the open-country Jews will keep

**3E — What Happens**
[[B17-the-Jews]] the Jews who were in Susa gathered again on the fourteenth day of the month of [[TM_ADAR]] Adar and killed in Susa three hundred men — but on the plunder they did not lay their hand. And the rest of [[B17-the-Jews]] the Jews who were in the king's provinces had gathered and stood for their lives, and had rest from their enemies, killing seventy-five thousand of those who hated them — but on the plunder they did not lay their hand.

**3F — Communicative Purpose**
Records the second day's killing in the capital and the empire-wide toll, twice marking that the Jews took no plunder — and sets up the difference in timing between Susa and the provinces.

**Significant Absence**
No God is named over the deliverance, even at its great summary number. And no spoil is taken — twice the line refuses it. In a world (and a genre) where the victors plunder the fallen, the doubled withholding of the hand from the spoil is the absence the narrator most wants heard: this is not enrichment, it is survival.

### Scene 3 — The rest, the feasting, and the two days (v.17–19)

**3A — Beings**
[[B17-the-Jews]] — הַיְּהוּדִים / the Jews
- Role: those who rest and feast — the provincial Jews on one day, the Susa Jews on another
- Relationship: the whole Jewish people, now turning from fighting to feasting
- Presence: PRESENT

The Jews of Susa — הַיְּהוּדִים אֲשֶׁר בְּשׁוּשָׁן / the Jews who were in Susa *(a referential form of [[B17-the-Jews]] — the capital's community, who rest on the fifteenth)*
- Role: those who acted two days and so rest and feast on the fifteenth
- Relationship: the Jewish community of the capital
- Presence: PRESENT

The Jews of the open towns — הַיְּהוּדִים הַפְּרָזִים / the Jews of the unwalled places *(a referential form of [[B17-the-Jews]] — the country/open-town community, who keep the fourteenth)*
- Role: those who live in the open villages and keep the fourteenth as their day of gladness
- Relationship: the rural Jewish communities, set apart by their one-day timing
- Presence: PRESENT

**3B — Places**
[[PL1-Susa-the-citadel]] — שׁוּשָׁן / Susa
- Role: the capital whose Jews rest on the fifteenth, a day later than the rest
- Type: royal capital
- Meaning: the city that acts two days and so rests later — the root of the second Purim date
- Effect on scene: anchors the later resting-day to the capital

The open towns and villages — עָרֵי הַפְּרָזוֹת / the towns of the open country *(scene content — the unwalled settlements; candidate place)*
- Role: the country settlements whose Jews keep the fourteenth
- Type: unwalled towns and villages
- Meaning: the open, walls-less places — set against the citadel of Susa to explain the two days
- Effect on scene: gives the fourteenth-day keepers their setting, opposite the capital

**3C — Objects and Elements**
The day of feasting and gladness — יוֹם מִשְׁתֶּה וְשִׂמְחָה / a day of feasting and gladness *(scene content — the festal day formed here; the *mishteh*, cf. [[I1-The-royal-banquet-mishteh]])*
- What it is: a day made into one of drinking-feast and joy
- Function in scene: the form the rest-day takes — the festival beginning to crystallize
- Signals: the turn from battle to celebration; the *mishteh* that has run all through this book reappears, now as the people's own feast

The sending of portions — מִשְׁלוֹחַ מָנוֹת / sending of portions *(scene content — the gift-of-food custom; candidate object)*
- What it is: the sending of food-gifts, each person to a neighbour
- Function in scene: marks the day of gladness with shared food between people
- Signals: a communal, neighbourly festival — the practice that will define the keeping of Purim

**3D — Times**
[[TM_ADAR]] — אֲדָר / Adar
- What it is: the twelfth month, in which all these days fall
- Function: the month that holds the thirteenth, fourteenth, and fifteenth — the fighting and the feasting alike
- Signals: the festival month forming around these dates

"The fourteenth of Adar" — אַרְבָּעָה עָשָׂר / the fourteenth *(content time — the day the open-town Jews rest, feast, and keep; anchored to [[TM_ADAR]] Adar)*
- What it is: the day of rest and gladness for the provincial / open-town Jews
- Function: the festival day kept by the unwalled towns
- Signals: one of the two Purim dates

"The fifteenth of Adar" — חֲמִשָּׁה עָשָׂר / the fifteenth *(content time — the day the Susa Jews rest and feast; anchored to [[TM_ADAR]] Adar)*
- What it is: the day of rest and gladness for the Jews of Susa
- Function: the festival day kept by the capital, a day later
- Signals: the other of the two Purim dates — the difference this whole passage exists to explain

**3E — What Happens**
This was on the thirteenth day of the month of [[TM_ADAR]] Adar; and on the fourteenth they rested, and made it a day of feasting and gladness. But [[B17-the-Jews]] the Jews who were in [[PL1-Susa-the-citadel]] Susa had gathered on the thirteenth and on the fourteenth, and rested on the fifteenth, making it a day of feasting and gladness. For this reason [[B17-the-Jews]] the Jews of the open country, who live in the unwalled towns, keep the fourteenth day of the month of Adar as gladness and feasting, a good day, and a day for sending portions of food, each to a neighbour.

**3F — Communicative Purpose**
Explains, by who rested on which day, why there come to be two days of feasting — the fourteenth kept by the open-town Jews, the fifteenth by the Jews of Susa — and shows the festival taking its shape: rest, gladness, feasting, and gifts of food.

**Significant Absence**
No God is named, even as the people turn from rescue to rejoicing — there is no thanksgiving spoken, no prayer, no altar; the gladness is simply kept. And the festival here has no name yet: the word *Purim* does not appear in this passage. The day is described — feasting, gladness, portions sent — but not yet titled or commanded; that naming is held back for the next pericope.
