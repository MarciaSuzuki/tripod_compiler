# E18 — Esther 10:1–3

## 1. Metadata
- **Pericope title:** The book closes: the king's tribute and Mordecai's greatness in the chronicles
- **BCV:** Esther 10:1–3
- **Genre Group:** NARRATIVE
- **Genre:** HISTORICAL_NARRATIVE
- **Register:** INFORMAL_CASUAL

**Multi-level register tagging:**
The whole passage sits in INFORMAL_CASUAL — the storyteller's plain summing-up voice as the book ends. Two things lift the framing without changing the register. First, the closing colophon-formula in v.2, *halo hem ketuvim al sefer divrei hayamim* ("are they not written in the book of the chronicles…"), is the set archival phrase of the Hebrew historians; it raises the voice into the formal key of an old, official record being pointed to — a COMMUNITY_MEMORY echo that closes the book the way *vayhi bimei Achashverosh* opened it (this marks the framing, not the register). Second, the narrator reports the chronicle's content from the outside; nothing is voiced as a decree, so no FORMAL_OFFICIAL override applies — even the tribute of v.1 is described, not enacted in royal words.

## 2. Level 1 — Whole-Passage Movement
### 2.1 Prose Arc / Shape / Argument / Burden / Concern
After the long deliverance and the founding of Purim, the book does not end on a scene but pulls back to the wide imperial angle it opened on — a single king laying tribute across the whole land and the far sea-coasts. Then, almost in the same breath, it turns from the king's power to the man the king raised: Mordecai. The movement is a settling — from the great empire down to one Jew now standing second only to the throne — and a sealing: all of it, the king's might and Mordecai's greatness, is said to be already written in the official chronicles, where the reader could go and check. What weighs on the passage is permanence and vindication: the danger is past, the enemy is gone, and the once-threatened people now has its own man at the empire's center, using that height not for himself but for the good and the peace of his people.

### 2.2 Context
This is the last word of the book of Esther. Behind it lies the whole turned story — the threat of annihilation under Haman, the reversal, the slaughter of the enemies, the feast of Purim now fixed for every year. Ahasuerus (Xerxes) still rules Persia and Media, the great empire of the exile; Mordecai the Jew, Esther's kinsman, who once sat in sackcloth at the gate, is now the king's second. The chronicle-formula here ("are they not written in the book of the chronicles of the kings of Media and Persia") is the same kind of archival back-reference that earlier sent the king to his record-book the night he could not sleep — the book that started the whole reversal. Now it closes the story by filing it away as fact. As everywhere in Esther, God is not named: the people's rescue and Mordecai's rise are stated as plain imperial history, and the One behind them is left unspoken.

### 2.3 Emotion / Tone / Pace
The tone is settled, official, and quietly proud — the calm of a story that has come safely to rest. The pace slows and widens after the action: a single broad sentence on the king's tribute, then the formal chronicle-formula, then a closing portrait of Mordecai that lingers, piling up his titles and his goodness — great, second to the king, accepted by his brothers, seeking his people's good, speaking peace. There is no suspense left; the rhythm is that of an epitaph or a seal pressed at the end of a document, content and final.

### 2.4 Communicative Function
The passage closes the book and fixes its outcome. It re-establishes the empire's scale one last time, certifies the whole account as recorded history (the reader is pointed to the official annals), and crowns the story by setting Mordecai the Jew at the empire's summit — not as a tyrant but as a benefactor of his people. To speak this passage is to seal the story: the threat is over, the people endures, and one of them now stands second to the throne of the world's great king, turning that power toward his kindred's good and peace.

**3E — What Happens**
[[B4-Ahasuerus]] King Ahasuerus laid a tribute on the land and on the far sea-coasts — his power pressing across the whole realm, from the mainland out to the islands.

**3F — Communicative Purpose**
Re-establishes the empire's full reach and the king's standing power as the book closes — the wide frame returning at the end.

**Significant Absence**
No God is named over the empire's power, as nowhere in the book. And nothing here is troubled or threatened — the tribute is stated as settled fact, with no danger, no enemy, and no crisis; the storm of the book is wholly past.

### Scene 3 — Mordecai the Jew, second to the king, for his people's good (v.3)

**3A — Beings**
[[B1-Mordecai]] — מָרְדֳּכַי הַיְּהוּדִי / Mordecai the Jew
- Role: the closing portrait — the Jew now standing second to the throne and turning that height toward his people
- Relationship: second to [[B4-Ahasuerus]] King Ahasuerus; great among [[B17-the-Jews]] the Jews; accepted by his many brothers
- Presence: PRESENT
- Referential form: "the Jew" (*ha-Yehudi*) — the load-bearing label that closes the book: the once-endangered people now has its named man at the empire's center

[[B4-Ahasuerus]] — הַמֶּלֶךְ אֲחַשְׁוֵרֹושׁ / King Ahasuerus
- Role: the king to whom Mordecai is second
- Relationship: the throne Mordecai now serves directly beneath
- Presence: REFERENCED

[[B17-the-Jews]] — הַיְּהוּדִים / the Jews
- Role: the people among whom Mordecai is great, and whose good and peace he seeks
- Relationship: Mordecai's own people; the once-threatened nation, now with its man at the top
- Presence: REFERENCED

His brothers / his kindred — אֶחָיו … זַרְעוֹ / his brothers … his seed (kindred) *(scene content — Mordecai's own kin, the body of the Jewish people; coextensive with [[B17-the-Jews]])*
- Role: those who accept Mordecai and to whom he speaks peace — his people seen as kin
- Relationship: the multitude of his brothers and all his seed (his lineage/kindred)
- Presence: REFERENCED

**3B — Places**
- None: no place is set in v.3; the verse is a standing portrait of Mordecai's status, not a located scene.

**3C — Objects and Elements**
Second to the king — מִשְׁנֶה לַמֶּלֶךְ / second to the king *(scene content — Mordecai's rank, not a registry object)*
- What it is: the office of viceroy — the rank directly below the throne
- Function in scene: states how high the Jew has risen — one step under the king of the empire
- Signals: the full reversal, in a title: the man once condemned now ranks second in the world's great kingdom

Good and peace — טוֹב … שָׁלוֹם / good … peace (shalom) *(scene content — what Mordecai seeks and speaks; not a registry object)*
- What it is: the welfare (tov) he seeks for his people and the peace (shalom) he speaks to his kindred
- Function in scene: defines how Mordecai uses his height — as benefactor, not tyrant
- Signals: power turned to the people's good; the book's last note is one of peace

**3D — Times**
- None: no time-setting; the verse is a timeless closing characterization of Mordecai.

**3E — What Happens**
For [[B1-Mordecai]] Mordecai the Jew was second to King [[B4-Ahasuerus]] Ahasuerus, and great among [[B17-the-Jews]] the Jews, and accepted by the multitude of his brothers — seeking the good of his people and speaking peace to all his kindred.

**3F — Communicative Purpose**
Closes the book with the portrait of Mordecai exalted yet self-giving — the once-endangered Jew now second to the throne, using his power for his people's good and peace.

**Significant Absence**
God is not named even here, at the summit of the deliverance: Mordecai's rise and his people's safety are stated without a word of thanks or attribution to the One the book never names. And nothing is said of any private reward or self-enrichment — his greatness is described only as turned outward, toward his people; the personal is left silent so the communal can stand.
