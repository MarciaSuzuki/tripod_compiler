# P06 — Ruth 2:8-16

## 1. Metadata
- **Pericope title:** Boaz speaks to Ruth in the field: instructions, blessing, meal, and harvester protocol
- **BCV:** Ruth 2:8-16
- **Genre Group:** NARRATIVE
- **Genre:** HISTORICAL_NARRATIVE
- **Register:** INFORMAL_CASUAL

**Multi-level register tagging:**
The whole passage sits in INFORMAL_CASUAL. Scene 2 (vv. 10-13) shifts to INTIMATE: Ruth's bowed-down question, Boaz's account of her loyalty, and Ruth's reply make a close exchange between a foreigner and the field-owner who has just taken notice of her. One spot at v.12 lifts higher, to CEREMONIAL: Boaz's blessing takes a formal shape, naming YHWH the God of Israel and using the picture of taking refuge under his wings. The narrator's framing stays in the plain, everyday telling.

## 2. Level 1 — Whole-Passage Movement

### 2.1 Prose Arc / Shape / Argument / Burden / Concern
The passage moves through four steps. Boaz tells Ruth to stay in his field, where he will keep her safe. Ruth asks why she, a foreigner, has been shown such favor. Boaz tells her he has heard all she did for her mother-in-law, and blesses her, asking God to shelter her under his wings. The two share a meal with plenty to spare. Then Boaz tells his harvesters to make her gleaning easy. The shape is two sets of instructions wrapped around a center of being noticed and blessed. What weighs on the passage is the gap between who Ruth is — a foreigner, a gleaner — and the welcome she is given: favor, food, safety, blessing. The question it leaves hanging is whether all this care points to something more than enough food for one day.

### 2.2 Context
This follows right on Boaz's arrival in his field and the foreman's report that names Ruth as the Moabite woman who came back with Naomi. Naomi's household is empty; Ruth has set out to glean on her own; the audience knows Boaz is a kinsman of the dead husband, though Ruth does not. The Moabite name the foreman has just used is the condition Boaz speaks under: he knows she is a foreigner as he welcomes her. The blessing at v.12, about taking refuge under God's wings, opens a pair across pericopes that will close at 3:9, when Ruth asks Boaz to spread his wing over her.

### 2.3 Emotion / Tone / Pace
The tone is warm and held-in, weighted by how much Ruth's being a foreigner puts at risk. Boaz speaks in orders meant to keep her safe; Ruth speaks from the ground, bowed low. The meal at v.14 brings a slowed, down-to-earth welcome — sit, eat, dip your bread, be handed grain by his own hand. The closing word to the harvesters goes back to keeping her safe. The pace is slow through the speeches and steady through the meal and the closing instructions.

### 2.4 Communicative Function
This passage carries the harvest-provision thread a long way forward: Boaz gives Ruth more than the law requires, letting her gather where gleaners normally may not. It carries the taking-in of the outsider forward by having Boaz, in his own words, honor Ruth's loyalty. It opens the picture of refuge under God's wings, which lands at 3:9. It opens a shift in how Ruth is named — from a lowly servant-girl here to something nearer at 3:9. It spreads the three "do not" commands — do not touch her, do not shame her, do not rebuke her — across the passage. And it plants signs of welcome toward Naomi's household, without yet spelling out to the people in the story that this could be a rescue.

- What it is: the act of gleaning

**3D — Times**
- None: scene-setting time is the continuing harvest day from P05; no distinct temporal frame.

**3E — What Happens**
Boaz tells Ruth to glean only in his field and to stay close to his young women. He tells her the young men have already been commanded not to touch her, and that she may drink from the workers' vessels.

**3F — Communicative Purpose**
Sets up Boaz's protection of Ruth, through his instructions and the order he says he has already given his men.

**Significant Absence**
Boaz never tells Ruth his name. Ruth says nothing; the whole scene is Boaz giving instructions.

**3C — Objects and Elements**
[[CB_0033-Hen-Favor]] — חֵן בְּעֵינֶיךָ / "favor in your eyes"
- What it is: the word hen — favor — that Ruth uses for what she has been given
- Function in scene: names what Ruth was hoping for and now finds; comes back at v.13
- Signals: kindness from someone who has the say over how things turn out

[[CB_0038-Nokhriya-Foreigner]] — נָכְרִיָּה / "foreigner"
- What it is: the word Ruth uses of herself — an outsider
- Function in scene: puts Ruth's foreignness in her own mouth at the very moment she is noticed
- Signals: nokhriya is the strongest word for an outsider — stronger than "resident foreigner," and not the Moabite name

[[CB_0037-Refuge-Under-Wings-Kanaph]] — לַחֲסוֹת תַּחַת כְּנָפָיו / "to take refuge under his wings"
- What it is: the image of finding shelter under God's wings, like a bird sheltering its young
- Function in scene: Boaz names Ruth's coming to Israel as coming to take shelter under God's wings
- Signals: opens the book's central wing image; closes at 3:9

[[CB_0008-Blessing]] — a formal blessing
- What it is: a blessing in set, weighty form
- Function in scene: Boaz's words at v.12 carry the weight of a real blessing, not a casual good-wish
- Signals: lifts to a ceremonial key at this one moment

**3D — Times**
- None: no distinct temporal frame; continuous within the field day.

**3E — What Happens**
Ruth falls on her face and asks why she has found favor and been recognized when she is a foreigner. Boaz answers that he has heard the full account of what she did for her mother-in-law after her husband's death — how she left her parents and her birth-land and came to a people she did not know. He blesses her: may YHWH repay her work; may her wages be full from YHWH the God of Israel, under whose wings she has come for refuge. Ruth answers that she has found favor, that he has comforted her and spoken to her heart, though she is not as one of his servant-girls.

**3F — Communicative Purpose**
The moment Ruth is noticed: Boaz puts her loyalty into words and blesses her in YHWH's name.

**Significant Absence**
Moab is not named by Boaz; he uses "the land of your birth" instead. Ruth's deceased husband is not named by which son; the wife-pairing is withheld. Boaz does not name himself; Ruth still addresses him as "my lord."

- What it is: the bread of the field meal
- Function in scene: plain food Ruth is invited to share
- Signals: closes the bread-thread that ran from a hungry Bethlehem to Boaz's table

[[O9-Vinegar]] — הַחֹמֶץ / "the vinegar"
- What it is: vinegar to dip the bread in
- Function in scene: a plain, bodily welcome at the meal
- Signals: the custom of a shared table

[[O10-Roasted-Grain]] — קָלִי / "roasted grain"
- What it is: roasted grain Boaz holds out to Ruth
- Function in scene: food passed from host to gleaner, hand to hand
- Signals: a small picture of plenty after famine

**3D — Times**

[[TM_MEALTIME-At-Mealtime]] — לְעֵת הָאֹכֶל / mealtime
- What it is: the time of day for the meal
- Function: the moment the meal invitation happens
- Signals: an ordinary harvest-day mealtime, made to matter by Ruth being taken in

**3E — What Happens**
At mealtime Boaz invites Ruth to come, eat from the bread, and dip her morsel in the vinegar. She sits beside the harvesters; he holds out roasted grain to her; she eats and is satisfied and has leftover.

**3F — Communicative Purpose**
Records the plain, bodily welcome of the meal; that she eats, is satisfied, and has some left over sets up the food she will carry back to Naomi.

**Significant Absence**
No conversation is recorded at the meal. Ruth says nothing; the harvesters say nothing.

**3A — Beings**

[[B13-Boaz]] — בֹּעַז / Boaz
- Role: field-owner commanding his harvesters about Ruth's gleaning
- Relationship: employer of the young men
- Presence: PRESENT

[[B17-Young-Men]] — נְעָרָיו / "his young men"
- Role: the ones Boaz gives the instructions to
- Relationship: Boaz's male field-workers
- Presence: PRESENT

[[B9-Ruth]] — implied
- Role: the gleaner the instructions are about; she has risen to glean
- Relationship: Moabite daughter-in-law of [[B3-Naomi]] Naomi
- Presence: PRESENT

**3B — Places**

[[PL5_BOAZ_PORTION-Boazs-Portion-of-the-Field]] — implied
- Role: where the gleaning happens
- Type: an owned harvest portion
- Meaning: the field where Ruth may now gather more than the usual
- Effect on scene: where Boaz's order takes effect

בֵּין הָעֳמָרִים / "among the sheaves"
- Role: the place Ruth is now allowed to glean
- Type: a part of the field gleaners are normally kept out of
- Meaning: letting her gather more than just what is left at the edges
- Effect on scene: gives Ruth more to gather than the law alone would

**3C — Objects and Elements**
[[CB_0034-Leket-Gleaning]] — לְלַקֵּט / "to glean"
- What it is: the act of gleaning
- Function in scene: carries the gleaning on in Boaz's command
- Signals: the same custom, now stretched in Ruth's favor

[[O11-Sheaves-and-Handfuls]] — הָעֳמָרִים / הַצְּבָתִים / sheaves; bundles
- What it is: the bound stalks left from the harvest, and handfuls pulled out on purpose
- Function in scene: what the extra-generous gleaning works among
- Signals: gleaning among the sheaves and pulling grain from the bundles goes well beyond the usual

**3D — Times**
- None: continuous within the field day; the rising to glean follows the meal.

**3E — What Happens**
Ruth rises to glean. Boaz commands his young men to let her glean even among the sheaves and not shame her. He also tells them to pull out for her on purpose from the bundles and leave them for her to gather, and not to rebuke her.

**3F — Communicative Purpose**
Closes the passage with Boaz's order for extra-generous gleaning, which builds his protection of Ruth right into how the harvesters work.

**Significant Absence**
Ruth is not among those Boaz speaks to; the young men are told about her, not her. The narrator never says Ruth hears the order.
