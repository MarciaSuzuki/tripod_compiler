# E10 — Esther 5:1–14

## 1. Metadata
- **Pericope title:** The scepter, the two banquets, and the gallows raised for Mordecai
- **BCV:** Esther 5:1–14
- **Genre Group:** NARRATIVE
- **Genre:** HISTORICAL_NARRATIVE
- **Register:** INFORMAL_CASUAL

**Multi-level register tagging:**
The whole passage sits in INFORMAL_CASUAL — the plain storyteller's voice, even at the dangerous moment when the queen stands unsummoned before the throne and the heavy moment when the gallows is built. The dialogue is quoted throughout (the king to Esther; Esther's two invitations; Haman's boast to his household; Zeresh and the friends' counsel), and all of it stays in the casual, lived register of people speaking to one another — no decree is voiced or written here. The king's grand formula "even to half the kingdom" (v.3, v.6) is courtly hyperbole spoken in conversation, not a published edict, so it is *not* a FORMAL_OFFICIAL moment.

## 2. Level 1 — Whole-Passage Movement
### 2.1 Prose Arc / Shape / Argument / Burden / Concern
The passage turns on two reversals that almost touch but never meet. First the queen risks her life: on the third day Esther dresses in royalty and stands, unsummoned, in the inner court — and instead of death she is met with the golden scepter held out, and a king's open-handed offer of "even to half the kingdom." She uses the opening not to ask for anything yet, but to invite — the king and Haman to a banquet, and there, at the banquet, to a second banquet the next day. The withholding is deliberate; the asking is postponed twice. Then the passage swings to Haman, who walks out of the same day "glad and good of heart," at the very peak of his fortune — and is undone in an instant by the sight of one man, Mordecai, who will not rise or tremble at the gate. The whole second half is Haman's swelling: his riches, his many sons, his promotions, his unique invitation to the queen's table — all of it, he says, worth nothing while Mordecai sits there. The passage closes with his wife Zeresh and his friends giving the counsel that will destroy him: build a gallows fifty cubits high, ask the king in the morning to hang Mordecai on it, and go to the banquet glad. The advice pleases Haman, and he has the gallows made. The burden is dramatic irony at full pitch: the man rising fastest is building the instrument of his own fall, and the queen's quiet, doubled invitation is the trap closing around him.

### 2.2 Context
This is the hinge of the book. Earlier, Haman has bought from the king the right to destroy all the Jews on a fixed day, and Mordecai has sent word to Esther: she must go to the king, though to enter the inner court unsummoned is death unless the king extends the scepter. She has asked her people to fast with her for three days and resolved, "if I perish, I perish." This pericope is the third day — the moment she steps forward. Everything before has built the danger; everything after will spend it. The reader already knows what Haman does not: that the queen he so resents being invited beside is a Jew, that her people are the ones he has condemned, and that the gallows he raises in pride will be the one he hangs on. God is named nowhere — not in Esther's risk, not in the scepter's reach, not in the swing of Haman's fortune — and the reader is left to feel the hand that is never named moving under all of it.

### 2.3 Emotion / Tone / Pace
The pace is taut and held. The first scene is almost silent with tension — a woman standing, a king looking, a scepter held out — and then loosens into courtly back-and-forth as Esther twice defers her request, stretching the suspense across two banquets. The middle turns sharply: Haman's elation curdles to rage in a single glance, and the tone goes hot — "filled with fury." Then comes the long, swelling boast at home, comic in its excess and heavy with irony, every triumph he names tightening the noose. The close is brisk and grim: the counsel given in a few quick lines, the gallows decided, the dimension named (fifty cubits), the advice "good in Haman's eyes," the gallows made. Pleasure and dread run side by side the whole way — Haman keeps being told to go to the banquet "glad," even as he builds his own scaffold.

### 2.4 Communicative Function
The passage moves the whole plot from danger into the mechanism of reversal. It shows the queen surviving the throne-room and setting her trap by invitation rather than by demand; it shows Haman at the exact summit of his pride; and it plants, at its very end, the gallows — the concrete object on which the book's central irony will turn, raised here by the proud man himself and waiting for the next pericope's sleepless night to begin turning everything over. To speak this passage is to feel the trap set and the rope measured, with neither the king nor Haman seeing what the audience already sees.

**3B — Places**
The inner court of the king's house — חֲצַר בֵּית־הַמֶּלֶךְ הַפְּנִימִית / the inner court of the king's house *(scene venue — sub-location of the palace in [[PL1-Susa-the-citadel]] Susa)*
- Role: the forbidden space Esther enters unsummoned, facing the throne
- Type: the palace's inner courtyard, directly before the throne-room door

**3C — Objects and Elements**
[[TH_SCEPTER]] — שַׁרְבִיט הַזָּהָב / the golden scepter
- What it is: the king's golden staff, held in his hand
- Function in scene: extended toward Esther, it grants her life and admits her to the king's presence; she touches its tip in acceptance
- Signals: the single object on which life or death turns here — mercy made visible

The royal throne — כִּסֵּא מַלְכוּתֹו / his royal throne *(scene content — the seat of the enthroned king)*
- What it is: the seat of rule on which the king sits facing the court
- Function in scene: places the king in full royal authority at the moment Esther appears
- Signals: settled, absolute power — the power to kill or to spare

Royal dress — מַלְכוּת / royalty (royal apparel) *(scene content — what Esther puts on)*
- What it is: the royal garb Esther clothes herself in for the approach
- Function in scene: she comes not as a suppliant but arrayed as queen, claiming her standing as she risks her life
- Signals: deliberate self-presentation — dignity worn into danger

**3D — Times**
The third day — בַּיֹּום הַשְּׁלִישִׁי / on the third day *(scene content — the day of the approach, closing the three-day fast)*
- What it is: the day Esther steps forward, completing the three days of fasting begun in the previous pericope
- Function: marks the appointed moment of the risk — "on the third day"
- Signals: a resolved, arrived moment; the end of waiting and the start of acting

**3E — What Happens**
On the third day, [[B2-Esther]] Esther clothed herself in royalty and stood in the inner court of the king's house, facing the throne-room, while [[B4-Ahasuerus]] the king sat on his royal throne, facing the doorway. When the king saw Esther the queen standing in the court, she found favor in his eyes, and he held out to her [[TH_SCEPTER]] the golden scepter that was in his hand; Esther drew near and touched the tip of the scepter. Then the king said to her, "What is it, Esther the queen? What is your request? Even to half the kingdom, it shall be given you."

**3F — Communicative Purpose**
Shows the queen surviving the unsummoned approach by the king's mercy, and the king opening the door wide with his offer — the moment that lets everything follow.

**Significant Absence**
God is named nowhere in the most dangerous moment of the book — the woman steps into a death-space and lives, and the narrator credits no prayer, no providence, no Name; only "she found favor in his eyes." The three-day fast that frames this day is mentioned in the previous pericope without any object of the fasting being named. The deliverance is real and unattributed — the book's signature silence at its sharpest.

**3D — Times**

- What it is: the first banquet held "today," and a second promised for "tomorrow"
- Function: stretches the request across two days, deferring the ask and building suspense
- Signals: deliberate delay — the queen choosing her moment

**3E — What Happens**
[[B2-Esther]] Esther said, "If it is good to the king, let [[B4-Ahasuerus]] the king and [[B3-Haman]] Haman come today to the banquet I have made for him." The king said, "Hurry Haman, to do as Esther has said" — and the king and Haman came to the banquet Esther had made. At the banquet of wine the king said to Esther, "What is your petition? It shall be granted you. And what is your request? Even to half the kingdom, it shall be done." Esther answered, "My petition and my request: if I have found favor in the eyes of the king, and if it is good to the king to grant my petition and do my request — let the king and Haman come to the banquet I will make for them tomorrow, and tomorrow I will do as the king has said."

**3F — Communicative Purpose**
Shows the queen springing her trap by invitation, twice declining to state her request and binding both the king and Haman to a second banquet — drawing Haman in unknowing.

**Significant Absence**
Esther never says what she actually wants — twice asked, twice she withholds it. The narrator gives no reason for the delay, no glimpse of her plan, and no Name behind her patience. The reader is left to feel a deliberate strategy with no stated source — the silence of a withheld purpose.

[[B1-Mordecai]] — מָרְדְּכַי / Mordecai

- Type: the gate of the king's house — the place of officials and petitioners
- Meaning: the open, public spot where the slight is given and seen
- Effect on scene: stages Haman's humiliation in the open, before all, just as he leaves the queen's table at his height

**3C — Objects and Elements**
- None: this scene turns on a man's bearing, not on any object — the absence of an act (Mordecai's not rising) is its whole substance.

**3D — Times**
That day — בַּיֹּום הַהוּא / on that day *(scene content — the same day as the first banquet)*
- What it is: the very day of the first banquet, on which Haman goes out
- Function: ties the elation and the rage to a single day, sharpening the reversal
- Signals: fortune and fury crowded into one day

**3E — What Happens**
[[B3-Haman]] Haman went out that day glad and good of heart. But when Haman saw [[B1-Mordecai]] Mordecai in the king's gate, and that he did not rise or tremble before him, Haman was filled with fury against Mordecai.

**3F — Communicative Purpose**
Shows Haman at his peak undone in an instant by one man's refusal to honor him — the spark that drives the plot to the gallows.

**Significant Absence**
Mordecai says nothing and does nothing — his power here is pure refusal, an absence of the homage everyone else gives. The narrator offers no word from Mordecai, no explanation, and no Name to ground his defiance; the reader knows only that he will not bow. The whole reversal hinges on a man who stays silent and still.

**3E — What Happens**
[[B3-Haman]] Haman held himself in and went home, and sent for his friends and [[B6-Zeresh]] Zeresh his wife. Haman recounted to them the weight of his riches, his many sons, and all the ways the king had promoted him and raised him above the officials and the king's servants. "Even Esther the queen," Haman said, "brought no one with the king to the banquet she made but me — and tomorrow too I am invited to her, with the king. Yet all this is worth nothing to me, every time I see [[B1-Mordecai]] Mordecai the Jew sitting in the king's gate." Then [[B6-Zeresh]] Zeresh his wife and all his friends said to him, "Let a gallows be made, fifty cubits high; and in the morning tell the king to hang Mordecai on it. Then go with the king to the banquet glad." The thing was good in Haman's eyes, and he had [[TH_GALLOWS]] the gallows made.

**3F — Communicative Purpose**
Shows Haman's pride at full height and the counsel that will destroy him — and raises, in his own hand, the gallows the book will turn back on him.

**Significant Absence**
No one in the room foresees the irony the reader sees plainly — that the gallows is Haman's own. The counsel is given with full confidence and no hint of warning; God is not named, no omen is read, no caution is spoken. The silence here is dramatic: the audience knows what the speakers cannot, and the narrator lets the gallows go up without a word of the doom it carries.
