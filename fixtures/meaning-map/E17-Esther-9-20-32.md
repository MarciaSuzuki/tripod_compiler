# E17 — Esther 9:20–32

## 1. Metadata
- **Pericope title:** The institution of Purim: Mordecai's letters, the days bound for every year, and Esther's confirming word
- **BCV:** Esther 9:20–32
- **Genre Group:** NARRATIVE
- **Genre:** HISTORICAL_NARRATIVE
- **Register:** INFORMAL_CASUAL

**Multi-level register tagging:**
The narrator's own voice sits in INFORMAL_CASUAL throughout — the plain telling of how a festival came to be kept. But this pericope is unusual: its content is largely *letters being written and sent out*, and where the letter's own instituting words are voiced — the charge to keep the fourteenth and fifteenth of Adar year by year as days of feasting, gladness, gifts, and portions (v.20–22), and the second letter of "peace and truth" confirming it (v.29–31) — the voice lifts into FORMAL_OFFICIAL, the key of a royal-style edict laid down for all the provinces. This is a real moment-override (not just framing): an obligation is being *enacted in writing*, "to keep upon them … that they should not fail" (v.27). The etiology of the name (v.24–25, recalling Haman's plot and its turning back on his own head) drops back to the narrator's plain INFORMAL_CASUAL report.

## 2. Level 1 — Whole-Passage Movement
### 2.1 Prose Arc / Shape / Argument / Burden / Concern
The fighting is over and the danger past; this passage is about how a one-time rescue is turned into a thing kept forever. It moves from act to memory. Mordecai writes the whole story down and sends letters to every Jew in the empire, near and far, binding them to keep two days each year — the fourteenth and fifteenth of the month of Adar — the very days their sorrow turned to gladness and their mourning to a good day. The keeping is spelled out: feasting and gladness, portions of food sent person to person, and gifts to the poor. The people take the obligation on. Then the passage pauses to explain the festival's odd name — Purim, from *pur*, the lot Haman cast to destroy them, the lot that fell back on his own head. Finally a second letter goes out, this one over the names of Queen Esther and Mordecai together, words of "peace and truth," to fix the days firmly for every generation; and the whole matter is written into the book. What weighs on the passage is permanence: the burden is to make sure this deliverance is never forgotten, that the days "should not pass from among the Jews, nor their memory perish from their seed."

### 2.2 Context
This comes near the very end of the book of Esther, after the Jews throughout the Persian empire have defended themselves and been delivered from Haman's decree of destruction. The story proper is done; what remains is to anchor it. Persia is an empire that runs on the irreversible written decree — the book has turned on that all along — and here the Jewish community uses the same machinery for itself: letters, sent to all 127 provinces, binding an obligation in writing. The festival being founded, Purim, is still kept; this passage is its charter. Behind it stands the whole reversal the book has narrated: a plot to wipe out a people, undone, and the plotter hanged on the gallows he built. God is never named here, as nowhere in the book — yet what is being commanded is, in effect, a perpetual act of remembrance for a rescue no one in the text credits to anyone.

### 2.3 Emotion / Tone / Pace
The tone is settled, deliberate, and forward-looking — the voice of someone founding an institution meant to outlast everyone present. Where the earlier chapters raced, this one is measured and a little formal, weighted with the language of letters and binding: "to keep," "to be doing," "year by year," "generation and generation, clan and clan, province and province, city and city." That piling-up of pairs is the rhythm of permanence, of something deliberately spread to every corner and every age. Under the official cadence runs real warmth — the turn "from grief to gladness, from mourning to a good day," the portions sent friend to friend, the gifts to the poor — the joy of a community that has come through. The pace is unhurried; nothing is at stake in events now, only in fixing them.

### 2.4 Communicative Function
The passage closes the action by converting it into observance: it founds the festival of Purim and hands on its rules, its name, and its reasons. It tells the hearer what to do (keep these two days, every year, with feasting and gifts), why (the sorrow that turned to joy), and what to call it (Purim, after the lot). It establishes the double authority behind the keeping — first Mordecai's letter, then a confirming letter over Esther's and Mordecai's names together — and seals the matter by recording it in the book. To speak this passage is to install a memory: to make sure that what happened is not only remembered but enacted, year after year, by a whole people and their descendants.

**3D — Times**
[[TM_ADAR]] — חֹדֶשׁ אֲדָר / the month of Adar
- What it is: the twelfth month, in which the two festival days fall
- Function: fixes the festival to the fourteenth and fifteenth of Adar, the days the danger ended
- Signals: the calendar anchor of the whole observance — "year by year"

**3E — What Happens**
[[B1-Mordecai]] Mordecai writes down these events and sends letters to all [[B17-the-Jews]] the Jews throughout the provinces of King [[B4-Ahasuerus]] Ahasuerus, near and far, binding them to keep the fourteenth and fifteenth of the month of [[TM_ADAR]] Adar every year — the days their sorrow turned to gladness and their mourning to a good day — as days of feasting and gladness, of sending portions to one another and gifts to the poor; and the Jews take on what they had begun to do and what Mordecai wrote to them.

**3F — Communicative Purpose**
Founds the festival by written charge: which days, how often, and how they are to be kept.

**Significant Absence**
No God is named as the one who turned the sorrow to gladness — the reversal is stated in the passive ("the month that was turned for them"), with no agent. The deliverance being commemorated is credited to no one in the text. And no priest, sanctuary, or sacrifice is named — this is a festival founded by letter, not by altar.

- Function in scene: the thing the festival's name is taken from — "Purim, after the name of the pur"

- What it is: the wooden gallows on which Haman and his sons were hanged

- Signals: the reversal at its sharpest — the device of death falls on the one who planned it

[[TH_EDICT]] — הָאִגֶּרֶת / the letter
- What it is: the written letter whose words ground the naming
- Function in scene: "because of all the words of this letter" — the document that fixes the reason
- Signals: the written charter behind the festival's name and keeping

**3D — Times**
- None: no separate time-setting; this is the narrator stepping back to explain, outside the festival calendar.

**3E — What Happens**
The narrator explains the name: because Haman son of Hammedatha the Agagite, the foe of all [[B17-the-Jews]] the Jews, had plotted to destroy them and had cast [[TH_PUR_LOT]] the *pur* — that is, the lot — to crush and destroy them; but when the matter came before [[B4-Ahasuerus]] the king, he ordered that Haman's evil plot should fall back on his own head, and [[B3-Haman]] Haman and his sons were hanged on [[TH_GALLOWS]] the gallows — therefore these days were called Purim, after the name of the *pur*, and the keeping was grounded in all the words of this letter, and in what the people had seen and what had come upon them.

**3F — Communicative Purpose**
Gives the festival's name and its reason — the lot, the plot, and its reversal — so the keeping is understood, not just observed.

**Significant Absence**
The reversal is told with no word of God turning it — the plot simply "returns on his own head." The book's signature silence holds even at the moment of explanation: the deliverance is named, dated, and named-after, but never credited to the One Israel would credit it to.

- What it is: a second written letter, over the names of Esther and Mordecai, confirming the festival
- Function in scene: gives the keeping a fuller, double authority — "words of peace and truth"
- Signals: the obligation re-issued and sealed; the festival made firm for every generation

[[I3-Purim]] — יְמֵי הַפּוּרִים / the days of Purim
- What it is: the festival now named and bound on the people and their seed
- Function in scene: the thing confirmed — to be remembered and kept in every generation, clan, province, and city
- Signals: a perpetual observance, never to lapse from among the Jews

[[TH_ANNALS]] — הַסֵּפֶר / the book
- What it is: the written record in which Esther's confirming word is set down
- Function in scene: "and it was written in the book" — the final fixing of the matter in writing
- Signals: permanence by record — the deliverance entered into the written memory of the people *(candidate: the "book" here may be the book of Esther itself or a community record)*

**3D — Times**
[[TM_ADAR]] — בִּזְמַנֵּיהֶם / in their appointed times
- What it is: the fixed yearly times of the two Purim days, in Adar
- Function: anchors the confirmed keeping to its recurring dates, "year by year"
- Signals: the calendar made permanent — the days fixed for every generation

**3E — What Happens**
[[B17-the-Jews]] The Jews take it firmly upon themselves and their descendants, and all who join them, never to fail to keep these two days as written and at their appointed time, year by year — days to be remembered and kept in every generation, clan, province, and city, never to pass from among the Jews nor their memory perish from their seed. Then [[B2-Esther]] Esther the queen, daughter of Abihail, and [[B1-Mordecai]] Mordecai the Jew write with full authority a second letter to confirm this letter of Purim, words of peace and truth, sent to all [[B17-the-Jews]] the Jews across the 127 provinces of [[B4-Ahasuerus]] Ahasuerus's kingdom — to fix the days of Purim at their appointed times, with their matters of fasting and lamentation. And the word of Esther confirms these matters of Purim, and it is written in [[TH_ANNALS]] the book.

**3F — Communicative Purpose**
Seals the festival with a second, doubly-authorized letter and a written record — making the observance permanent and binding on every generation.

**Significant Absence**
No oath to God, no blessing, no covenant-language of the LORD seals this binding — it is bound "upon themselves and their seed" by the people's own word and Esther's, recorded in a book, with no divine name invoked over the most solemn act of the chapter. And though "fasting and lamentation" are named, no prayer and no object of any prayer is.
