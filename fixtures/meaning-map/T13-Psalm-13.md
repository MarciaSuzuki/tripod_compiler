# T13 — Psalm 13:1–6

## 1. Metadata
- **Pericope title:** How long? — the lament that turns: from the hidden face to the vow of song
- **BCV:** Psalm 13:1–6
- **Genre Group:** POETIC_SUNG
- **Genre:** LAMENT
- **Register:** INTIMATE

**Versification:**
Verses are counted the English way here. The Hebrew text counts the heading as verse 1, so
English vv.1–4 are Hebrew vv.2–5 — and English vv.5–6 share ONE Hebrew verse (v.6): the
trust-turn and the vow of song are a single Hebrew verse, one breath.

**The heading (before the poem begins):**
לַמְנַצֵּחַ מִזְמוֹר לְדָוִד — "To the choirmaster. A psalm (*mizmor*) of David." A liturgical
heading, not part of the prayer itself — the poem's own voice begins after it. *Mizmor*
("psalm, a sung poem") fits what follows. The phrase לְדָוִד le-David ("of / for / belonging to
David") is genuinely ambiguous — author, dedicatee, or collection-marker — so the heading does
not settle who speaks. *La-menatseach* ("to the choirmaster") is a performance note whose
exact sense is no longer known.

**Multi-level register tagging:**
The whole poem sits in INTIMATE — a raw, one-to-one cry to God, spoken to him as "you"
without a break: "How long will YOU forget ME?" Through the complaint and the petition there
is no congregation in view and no public voice. At the close the voice lifts: the vow — "I
will sing to YHWH" — is worship speech, a song promised toward praise, and it is the poem's
one shift from speaking *to* God to naming him openly. Scene 3 (vv.5–6) carries that lift as
a scene-level rise to RELIGIOUS_WORSHIP; the poem's own register stays INTIMATE — the vow is
still one voice, and the promised song has not yet gathered an assembly. No COMMUNITY_MEMORY
framing appears anywhere in the poem.

## 2. Level 1 — Whole-Passage Movement

### 2.1 Prose Arc / Shape / Argument / Burden / Concern
Four times in two verses the same cry hammers out — "how long?… how long?… how long?… how
long?" — climbing a ladder of abandonment: forgotten by God, hidden from God's face, left
alone with churning thoughts and a daily sorrow, and beneath it all the enemy riding high. Then
the cry sharpens into petition: look at me, answer me, put the light back in my eyes — because
the alternative is named without flinching: the sleep that is death, the enemy's boast "I have
beaten him," the gloating of the foes when he falls. And then, with nothing in the text to
explain it, the poem pivots on a single word — "but as for me" — and everything after it stands
on ground the complaint denied: I have trusted in your loyal love; my heart will yet rejoice in
your rescue; I will sing to YHWH — because he has dealt fully with me. The burden of the poem
is that both halves are true at once and neither is retracted: the abandonment is spoken to
the very God it accuses, and the trust is vowed while the enemy still stands. The turn is the
poem's engine, and the text refuses to show its mechanism.

### 2.2 Context
This is an individual lament — the Psalter's largest class. God is named and spoken to
directly — as "you" — from the first line up to the closing vow, which turns to naming him
openly. A psalm
is a self-contained poem, not an episode of a running story: there is no book-cast to carry
forward, no before and after. What recurs across the Psalter recurs as *types* — the praying
"I" (anyone's voice; the heading's "of David" does not settle whose), the constant Addressee,
and the enemy — and as shared images and ideas: the hidden face, steadfast love (*ḥesed*),
salvation, trust. Psalm 13 is the compact classic of the lament form: address, complaint,
petition, motivation, turn, vow — six movements in six verses.

### 2.3 Emotion / Tone / Pace
The opening is anguish under pressure — not despair that has gone quiet, but protest that
still expects to be heard: the fourfold "how long" is the sound of endurance running out
while address continues. The text never labels the feeling; the distress is read from the
wording itself — the fourfold repetition, "forever," the daily sorrow — and what is at stake
beneath it is the lost presence, the hidden face. The middle tightens into urgency: the petition runs in three bare pleas, and the
fear under it is named by its objects — the sleep of death, the enemy's boast — rather than by
any word for fear. Then the pace breaks: "but as for me" (*va-ʾani*) swings the poem in one
step from plea to settled ground, and the closing line runs forward — rejoicing that is coming,
a song already promised — with the quiet, spacious confidence of a thing decided. Restraint
throughout: the poem never names its own emotions; it voices them.

### 2.4 Communicative Function
The poem gives a sufferer words for the unbearable middle of trouble — the time when God has
not yet answered and the enemy has not yet fallen — and it does so without softening either
side: it legitimizes protest spoken directly to God, and it binds that protest to trust
without narrating a rescue in between. To speak this poem is to make the turn oneself: to
carry a hearer from the fourfold "how long" through the petition and its named terrors, across
the unexplained pivot, into trust, coming joy, and the vow of song. The doubled sayings, the
fourfold repetition, and the bare "but as for me" are the performance's load-bearing parts —
the intensification lives in them.

## 3. Level 2 — Scenes / Episodes

*(The poem moves in three movements — complaint vv.1–2 · petition vv.3–4 · turn-and-vow
vv.5–6 — and each movement is a scene below. No Selah appears in Psalm 13.)*

### Scene 1 — The complaint: the fourfold "how long" (vv.1–2 · MT vv.2–3)

**3A — Beings**
[[B1-The-Psalmist]] — the psalmist (the praying I; the word "I" itself, אֲנִי, surfaces only at
the v.5 pivot)
- Role: the speaker; the one crying "how long" over being forgotten — sorrowing, and overtopped
- Relationship: addresses YHWH directly; the heading's "of David" does not settle who he is —
  the poem gives only "I" and "me," one single voice, never "we"
- Presence: PRESENT

[[B2-YHWH]] — יְהוָה / YHWH
- Role: the Addressee — accused, in lament, of forgetting and of hiding his face
- Relationship: spoken to as "you" throughout; PRESENT as the one spoken TO, even while
  the complaint's content is his felt absence — that tension IS the scene
- Presence: PRESENT

[[B3-The-Enemy]] — אֹיְבִי / the enemy ("my enemy")
- Role: the third axis of the classic complaint (the I, the Thou, the they): the adversary
  exalted over the speaker
- Relationship: unnamed adversary type; carries the global enemy/foe concept
- Presence: REFERENCED

**3B — Places**
None named. The poem has no geography — its whole space is the address itself: the speaker,
the hidden face, the enemy above.

**3C — Objects and Elements**
None concrete. The "face" of v.1 is God's own presence, not a thing in the room; the soul
and heart of v.2 are the speaker's own interior.

**3D — Times**
No registered time entities. Two duration words shape the scene from inside the complaint:
נֶצַח "forever" (the feared duration of being forgotten) and יוֹמָם "daily / all the day" (the
sorrow's relentless schedule).

**3E — What Happens**
The psalmist hurls the same question at [[B2-YHWH]] YHWH four times — "how long?" — twice over
God's withdrawal (will you forget me forever? / will you hide your face from me?), once over
the anguish carried alone (counsels in my soul, sorrow in my heart, daily), and once over
[[B3-The-Enemy]] the enemy's standing (how long will my enemy be exalted over me?).

**3F — Communicative Purpose**
One voice speaks throughout — "I" and "me," never "we." Opens the lament by naming the
distress along all three of its classic axes — the Thou (God withdrawn), the I (inner
anguish), the they (the enemy up) — while keeping the channel of address open: the accusation
of absence is spoken *to* the Absent One. The fourfold repetition is the intensification
itself, and the doubled sayings carry the mounting force. Under the protest — unnamed by the
text, read from the wording of vv.1–2 — is distress at lost presence; what is at stake is the
face itself.

**Significant Absence**
No answer comes, and none will come anywhere in this psalm: YHWH never speaks. The fourfold
"how long" stands unanswered — the question receives no oracle, no assurance, no voice from
the One addressed. The absence of the divine reply, where the address so insistently expects
one, is the scene's load-bearing silence.

### Scene 2 — The petition and its feared outcomes (vv.3–4 · MT vv.4–5)

**3A — Beings**
[[B1-The-Psalmist]] — the psalmist
- Role: petitioner — three bare pleas; then the one whose death and downfall are feared
- Relationship: petitions YHWH by name and bond — "YHWH *my God*" (יְהוָה אֱלֹהָי)
- Presence: PRESENT

[[B2-YHWH]] — YHWH ("my God")
- Role: the petitioned — asked to look, to answer, to relight the eyes
- Relationship: calling him "MY God" claims the bond the complaint feared broken
- Presence: PRESENT

[[B3-The-Enemy]] — the enemy
- Role: would-be victor of the feared future: the one who would say "I have prevailed over him"
- Relationship: the same unnamed adversary type as Scene 1; the would-be victor over the psalmist
- Presence: REFERENCED

[[B4-The-Foes]] — צָרַי / the foes ("my adversaries")
- Role: would-be celebrants of the feared future: the ones who would rejoice at the shaking
- Relationship: the adversary GROUP — a distinct scene role from the singular boaster
- Presence: REFERENCED

**3B — Places**
None named.

**3C — Objects and Elements**
None concrete. The "eyes" to be lighted are the speaker's own life-force, not a thing in
the room.

**3D — Times**
No registered time entities. The feared futures ("lest…") are possibilities held off, not dated events.

**3E — What Happens**
The psalmist petitions [[B2-YHWH]] YHWH his God — look at me, then answer me (the plea
advancing from noticing to responding), light up my eyes — and then names what the petitions
exist to prevent: lest I sleep the sleep of death; lest [[B3-The-Enemy]] my enemy say "I have
prevailed over him"; lest [[B4-The-Foes]] my foes rejoice because I am shaken.

**3F — Communicative Purpose**
Turns complaint into asking. The three pleas carry the psalm's only requests, and the
three "lest" sayings are their motivation — the feared outcomes are named so that the petition
has stakes: death (the lights going out), the enemy's spoken triumph, the foes' joy at a fall.
None of these has happened; each is named only as the thing the pleas exist to prevent.
The fear itself is carried in the poem's own triple "lest" (פֶּן); what the text leaves
unsaid — and the wording of vv.3–4 lets us name — is the thing at stake under it: the
speaker's life, the light of the eyes.

**Significant Absence**
Between the petition and the turn, nothing arrives: no oracle, no priestly word, no sign, no
narrated rescue. The text withholds whatever happened — if anything happened — between "answer
me" and "but I have trusted." Death is named at the door and no promise is recorded against
it. The mechanism of the coming turn is exactly what this scene refuses to supply.

### Scene 3 — The turn and the vow: trust, coming joy, promised song (vv.5–6 · MT v.6)

**3A — Beings**
[[B1-The-Psalmist]] — the psalmist ("but as for me…")
- Role: the truster, the one whose heart will rejoice, the vower of song
- Relationship: pivots against his own complaint — וַאֲנִי "but as for me" — while still
  addressing YHWH ("YOUR steadfast love… YOUR salvation")
- Presence: PRESENT

[[B2-YHWH]] — YHWH
- Role: the trusted one; the rescuer trusted-for (the rescue itself never shown); the
  recipient of the vowed song; the one who "has dealt bountifully with me"
- Relationship: still spoken to as "you" ("YOUR steadfast love… YOUR salvation") until the vow,
  which names him openly — the poem's one shift from speaking to him to naming him, at its most
  public-facing line
- Presence: PRESENT

**3B — Places**
None named.

**3C — Objects and Elements**
None concrete. Steadfast love and salvation are realities the poem names, not things in a
room.

**3D — Times**
No registered time entities. The poem's own timing does the work: trust already standing,
joy yet to come, song promised, bounty already dealt.

**3E — What Happens**
Against everything vv.1–4 said — "but as for me" — the psalmist declares his standing trust in
[[B2-YHWH]] YHWH's ḥesed; on that ground his heart will yet rejoice in YHWH's salvation; and he
vows to sing to YHWH, because YHWH has dealt bountifully with him.

**3F — Communicative Purpose**
Closes the lament with the mood-turn — the sudden swing from plea to trust that is the
lament form's signature hinge — and the vow of praise that ends the classic arc. The turn is a first-class structural
event: trust is not a report about feelings but the ground the last two verses stand on, and
the rejoicing is its consequence, not its restatement. The vow's grounds ("because he has
dealt bountifully") reach back to completed bounty even as the rejoicing is still future —
the poem ends standing between the two. The trust and the coming joy are the poem's own
words, not a reading between its lines; what the wording of v.5 lets us name — "in YOUR
ḥesed… in YOUR salvation" — is what the joy rests on: the covenant loyalty itself.

**Significant Absence**
The turn has no stated cause, and the rescue is never narrated: the enemy of vv.2–4 is not
reported fallen, the face is not reported unhidden, and no event stands between the fourth
"how long" and "but I have trusted." The psalm ends with the song promised, not performed, and
the deliverance trusted, not shown. That gap — trust vowed while the trouble still stands — is
the poem's meaning.
