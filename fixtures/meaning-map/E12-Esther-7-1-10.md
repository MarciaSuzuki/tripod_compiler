# E12 — Esther 7:1–10

## 1. Metadata
- **Pericope title:** The second banquet: Esther names the adversary, and Haman hangs on his own gallows
- **BCV:** Esther 7:1–10
- **Genre Group:** NARRATIVE
- **Genre:** HISTORICAL_NARRATIVE
- **Register:** INFORMAL_CASUAL

**Multi-level register tagging:**
The whole passage sits in INFORMAL_CASUAL — the plain voice of a storyteller telling how the trap closed. The voice is mostly carried in quoted speech: the king's repeated offer ("What is your request? … it shall be done"), Esther's plea ("let my life be given me … and my people"), the king's demand ("Who is he, and where is he?"), Esther's naming of the adversary, and the king's death-order ("Hang him on it"). None of this is a written decree being read out, so no FORMAL_OFFICIAL override applies — even the king's final command to hang Haman is a spoken order at the banquet, not a sealed edict. The register stays INFORMAL_CASUAL throughout.

## 2. Level 1 — Whole-Passage Movement
### 2.1 Prose Arc / Shape / Argument / Burden / Concern
This is the turning that the whole book has been bending toward, and it all happens in one room over one cup of wine. The king, for the second day running, offers Esther anything she asks — up to half the kingdom. This time she answers: she begs for her own life and the life of her people, who have been sold to be wiped out. The king, who does not yet know that his own decree is the death-warrant and that the woman before him is one of the condemned, demands to know who would dare such a thing. Esther names him — the man at the table: Haman. From there the scene collapses fast. The king storms out into the garden in fury; Haman, seeing his doom settled, throws himself on Esther's couch to beg for his life; the king walks back in, sees a man on the queen's couch, and reads it as assault. A servant points to the very gallows Haman had built for Mordecai, and the king has Haman hanged on it. The burden of the passage is reversal made complete: the accuser becomes the accused, the one who built the gallows dies on it, and the king's hot anger only settles once it has fallen on the right man.

### 2.2 Context
By now the reader knows what the king does not. Haman, the king's highest official, has bought from the king a decree to destroy all the Jews across the empire on a fixed day, and the lots (Pur) have been cast for it. Esther, the queen, is herself a Jew — a fact she has kept hidden — and Mordecai, her kinsman, is the man Haman most hates, the man for whom Haman has already built a tall gallows in his own house. The day before, the king could not sleep, heard read aloud how Mordecai had once saved his life, and made Haman parade Mordecai through the city in royal honour. So Haman comes to this second banquet already humiliated and warned by his own household that his fall has begun. The book never names God in any of this, but every piece is in place — the hidden queen, the doomed people, the unwitting king, the gallows already standing — for the single word that turns it all: a name.

### 2.3 Emotion / Tone / Pace
After two slow days of banqueting and deferral, the pace breaks into a run. The first verses are quiet and formal — the king's courteous, repeated offer, Esther's careful, building plea that holds back the name to the last possible moment. Then, at the naming, the tone snaps: the king's fury, his stalking out to the garden, Haman's panic, the fall on the couch, the king's return and his furious misreading, the swift order, the hanging. Dread gives way to a grim, almost ironic satisfaction as the very gallows Haman raised swallows him. The closing line is sudden and final — the king's wrath "subsided," like a fire going out the instant it has its fuel — and the room falls still.

### 2.4 Communicative Function
The passage delivers the book's central reversal: the threat against the Jews is turned back onto the man who designed it. It shows Esther moving at last from hiding to open speech — risking herself to name the danger to her people — and it shows the unwitting king becoming the instrument of rescue without ever knowing the deeper story. It closes two long-running threads: Haman's rise (begun when the king first lifted him above all the princes) ends here in his fall, and the gallows he built for Mordecai becomes his own death. To speak this passage is to hand on the moment the trap snaps shut — and to set up everything that follows: the saving of Mordecai, the undoing of the decree, and the deliverance the book will never call God's.
