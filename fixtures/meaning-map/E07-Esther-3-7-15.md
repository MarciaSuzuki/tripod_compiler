# E07 — Esther 3:7–15

## 1. Metadata
- **Pericope title:** The lot and the decree of destruction: Haman buys the death of a people
- **BCV:** Esther 3:7–15
- **Genre Group:** NARRATIVE
- **Genre:** HISTORICAL_NARRATIVE
- **Register:** INFORMAL_CASUAL

**Multi-level register tagging:**
The narrator's voice is INFORMAL_CASUAL throughout — the plain telling of how a death-sentence was set in motion. One moment takes a FORMAL_OFFICIAL override: the written decree itself at v.13, whose own words are spelled out — "to destroy, to kill, and to annihilate all the Jews … in one day," with their goods as plunder. That is the language of the edict itself, not the narrator's plain report, so the decree's own wording carries FORMAL_OFFICIAL. Haman's speech to the king at v.8–9 — the charge against "a certain people" and the price named in silver — is a spoken proposal made before the throne, not decree wording, so it stays INFORMAL_CASUAL (as with the courtiers' proposal in E03 and Esther's petition in E13). The surrounding narration — the casting of the lot, the handing over of the ring, the couriers going out, the city left bewildered — stays INFORMAL_CASUAL.

## 2. Level 1 — Whole-Passage Movement

### 2.1 Prose Arc / Shape / Argument / Burden / Concern
The passage moves from a single private act — a lot cast on a table before one man — to an empire-wide sentence of death, and it does so with terrible smoothness. First the lot is thrown, month by month, day by day, until it settles on a far-off month: the time is chosen. Then Haman goes to the king with a charge dressed as administration — there is a people, scattered everywhere, who keep their own laws and not the king's; it is not worth letting them be — and a sum of silver to make the problem disappear. The king does not even ask their name. He pulls the signet ring from his hand and gives it to Haman, and with it the people, to do with as he sees fit. From there the machinery runs on its own: scribes summoned, the decree written out in every script and tongue, sealed with the king's own ring, copied, and carried by couriers to every province — destroy, kill, annihilate, all of them, in one day, and take their goods. The couriers rush out; the king and Haman sit down to drink. The burden of the passage is how lightly the unspeakable is decided — a genocide arranged over a cup, by lot and by signet — and the last image holds the whole weight of it: the two men drinking while the city around them reels.

### 2.2 Context
This is the turning of the book's screw. In the pericopes before, Haman has been raised over all the princes, and Mordecai the Jew has refused to bow to him; Haman, learning Mordecai's people, has set his rage not on one man but on the whole nation. Here that rage becomes law. The reader already knows what the king and Haman do not weigh: that the queen herself is one of this "certain people." The Persian decree-machine the book built in its opening chapter — rule by writing, sealed by the ring, irreversible once issued — is now turned, for the first time, to killing. Everything the rest of the book will fight against is set in place in these few verses: a fixed day, a sealed word, a whole people marked. And the lot that names the day, falling far off in the last month of the year, is the slender gap of time the deliverance will later squeeze through.

### 2.3 Emotion / Tone / Pace
The tone is cold and administrative on its surface, and that is exactly where the horror lies — the narrator reports a death-sentence in the same flat voice he would use for any royal errand. The pace is deliberate at first, almost ritual: the lot falling month to month, day to day, the slow tally of an empire's months. Then, once the ring changes hands, it accelerates into pure procedure — scribes, every province, every people, every script and tongue, written, sealed, copied, sent — a piling-up of bureaucratic completeness that makes the killing sound like paperwork. The final verse snaps the rhythm: the couriers go out *driven, in haste*, and then a sudden, awful stillness — the two men *sat down to drink* — set against the one word the narrator allows himself to feel: the city was *bewildered*.

### 2.4 Communicative Function
The passage seals the threat that the whole rest of the book exists to undo. It shows how power in this empire works — by lot, by accusation, by silver, by a ring pressed into wax — and how easily it can be aimed at an entire people whose name the king never even hears. It fixes the deadline (the thirteenth of the twelfth month, Adar) and the terms (destroy, kill, annihilate, plunder) that every later scene will race against. And it leaves the reader holding the unbearable picture the narrator ends on: the decree gone out to every province, a people condemned, and at the center of the empire two men with their cups, while the capital itself does not understand what has just been done. To speak this passage is to set the clock running.

## 3. Level 2 — Scenes / Episodes

### Scene 1 — The casting of the lot (v.7)

**3A — Beings**
[[B3-Haman]] — הָמָן / Haman
- Role: the man before whom the lot is cast; the one choosing the day for his plan
- Relationship: the king's raised-up official, set on destroying Mordecai's people
- Presence: PRESENT

**3B — Places**
- None: the lot is cast "before Haman," with no place named. No setting is given for this moment.

**3C — Objects and Elements**
[[TH_PUR_LOT]] — פּוּר / the lot (Pur)
- What it is: the Persian lot — *pur* — here glossed in the Hebrew itself as *the goral*, the lot
- Function in scene: the means of choosing the day; cast month by month, day by day, until it falls on the twelfth month
- Signals: chance, or fate — the providence hinge of the book; the festival Purim will later take its name from this very word

**3D — Times**
[[TM_NISAN]] — נִיסָן / Nisan, the first month
- What it is: the month in which the lot is cast — the first month, in the twelfth year of the king's reign
- Function: dates the casting at the year's beginning
- Signals: a beginning — the plot set in motion at the turn of the year

[[TM_ADAR]] — אֲדָר / Adar, the twelfth month
- What it is: the month on which the lot falls — the twelfth month, the last of the year
- Function: fixes the far-off day the decree will name
- Signals: a long delay before the blow — the gap of nearly a year that the deliverance will use

**3E — What Happens**
In the first month — [[TM_NISAN]] Nisan — in the twelfth year of King [[B4-Ahasuerus]] Ahasuerus, the lot, [[TH_PUR_LOT]] Pur, was cast before [[B3-Haman]] Haman, going from day to day and from month to month, until it fell on the twelfth month — [[TM_ADAR]] the month of Adar.

**3F — Communicative Purpose**
Shows the day of destruction being chosen — by lot, far off at the end of the year.

**Significant Absence**
No God is named over the casting of the lot — the book's signature silence falls exactly where a reader might most expect a hand behind the chance. The text says only that the lot fell; it does not say who made it fall. Nothing is said either of why the far-off month was let stand — the long delay simply happens, unexplained.

### Scene 2 — Haman's charge and the king's ring (v.8–11)

**3A — Beings**
[[B3-Haman]] — הָמָן / Haman
- Role: the accuser; the one who proposes the destruction and pays for it
- Relationship: standing before the king; son of Hammedatha the Agagite, named here "the enemy of the Jews"
- Presence: PRESENT

[[B4-Ahasuerus]] — הַמֶּלֶךְ / the king (Ahasuerus)
- Role: the one who hears the charge and grants it — handing over both the ring and the people
- Relationship: king of the empire; here giving Haman a free hand
- Presence: PRESENT

[[B17-The-Jews]] — עַם־אֶחָד / a certain people (the Jews)
- Role: the people accused and condemned — never named to the king, called only "a certain people, scattered and dispersed"
- Relationship: the nation Haman means to destroy; the king's own subjects across the provinces
- Presence: REFERENCED

The doers of the work — עֹשֵׂי הַמְּלָאכָה / those who carry out the business *(scene content — the treasury officials, not a registry being)*
- Role: the officials into whose hands Haman offers to weigh out the silver
- Relationship: the king's administrators of the treasuries
- Presence: REFERENCED

**3B — Places**
The king's treasuries — גִּנְזֵי הַמֶּלֶךְ / the king's treasuries *(scene content — the royal stores, not a registered place)*
- Role: where Haman's silver is to be brought
- Type: the royal treasury
- Meaning: the price of the destruction, paid into the king's own coffers
- Effect on scene: makes the killing a transaction — a people's life set against a weight of silver

**3C — Objects and Elements**
[[I2-The-law-dat]] — דָּת / their laws / the king's laws (dat)
- What it is: law — here doubled: *their* laws, which the people keep, set against *the king's* laws, which Haman says they do not keep
- Function in scene: the whole charge turns on it — a people who live by a different law and so (Haman argues) are not worth leaving alone
- Signals: difference made into a crime; the same word that runs the empire now turned into an accusation

Silver — כֶּסֶף / silver *(scene content — Haman's payment)*
- What it is: ten thousand talents of silver, which Haman offers to weigh out
- Function in scene: the price Haman puts on the destruction, to be paid into the treasuries
- Signals: a people's death bought and budgeted

[[TH_SIGNET_RING]] — טַבַּעַת / the signet ring
- What it is: the king's signet ring, pulled from his own hand
- Function in scene: handed to Haman — and with it the authority to seal anything as the king's own will
- Signals: the transfer of absolute, irreversible power into Haman's hand; the king's word now Haman's to write

**3D — Times**
- None: no separate time-setting; the audience with the king follows on from the casting of the lot, with no new date given.

**3E — What Happens**
[[B3-Haman]] Haman said to King [[B4-Ahasuerus]] Ahasuerus that there was [[B17-The-Jews]] a certain people, scattered and dispersed among the peoples throughout the provinces of his kingdom, whose laws were different from every other people's and who did not keep the king's [[I2-The-law-dat]] laws — so it was not worth the king's while to leave them be. If it pleased the king, let it be written that they be destroyed; and Haman would weigh out ten thousand talents of silver into the hands of those who did the work, to bring into the king's treasuries. Then the king took [[TH_SIGNET_RING]] his signet ring from his hand and gave it to Haman — son of Hammedatha the Agagite, the enemy of the Jews — and told him the silver was his to keep, and the people his to do with as he saw fit.

**3F — Communicative Purpose**
Shows the destruction proposed, bought, and granted — the king handing Haman the ring and the people without even asking their name.

**Significant Absence**
The king never asks who the people are. He does not learn their name, their nation, or their God — and the narrator lets that ignorance stand, unbroken: the reader knows it is the people of the queen, but the king condemns them blind. No protest is voiced, no counsel sought, no god invoked over a sentence of death. And Haman, naming the price in silver, never names the people he means to wipe out.

### Scene 3 — The decree written, sealed, and sent (v.12–15)

**3A — Beings**
[[B3-Haman]] — הָמָן / Haman
- Role: the one whose command the decree carries — written "according to all that Haman commanded"

[[B4-Ahasuerus]] — הַמֶּלֶךְ / the king (Ahasuerus)

[[B17-The-Jews]] — הַיְּהוּדִים / all the Jews

- Presence: PRESENT

- Role: the empire's officials to whom the letters are addressed
- Relationship: the administration of every province and every people
- Presence: REFERENCED

**3B — Places**
[[PL1-Susa-the-citadel]] — שׁוּשַׁן הַבִּירָה / Susa the citadel
- Role: the capital where the decree is issued — and the city left bewildered at the close
- Type: royal capital / fortress-city
- Meaning: the seat from which the sentence goes out, and the place that does not understand what has been done
- Effect on scene: fixes the issuing of the decree at the empire's center, and ends the pericope there, on the city's confusion

[[I5-The-127-provinces]] — מְדִינוֹת הַמֶּלֶךְ / every province of the king
- Role: the reach of the decree — "all the provinces of the king"
- Type: the empire, province by province
- Meaning: the killing is empire-wide; nowhere the Jews live is left out
- Effect on scene: stretches the sentence across the whole realm, in every province's own script and tongue

**3C — Objects and Elements**
[[TH_EDICT]] — סְפָרִים / the letters (the decree)
- What it is: the written decree — letters copied and sent to every province
- Function in scene: the carrier of the command to destroy; written in every script and language, addressed to every province's officials
- Signals: the irreversible Persian word, here the instrument of genocide; the same letter-machinery that opened the book now condemning a people

The text of the decree — דָּת … לְהַשְׁמִיד לַהֲרֹג וּלְאַבֵּד / the law … to destroy, to kill, and to annihilate *(voiced decree content — FORMAL_OFFICIAL)*
- What it is: the decree's own words — to destroy, to kill, and to annihilate all the Jews, young and old, children and women, in one day, the thirteenth of the twelfth month, and to plunder their goods
- Function in scene: the actual sentence, spelled out; a copy is to be published to every people, that they be ready for that day
- Signals: the unspeakable said plainly, in the cold language of law — the register lifts here into FORMAL_OFFICIAL

[[TH_SIGNET_RING]] — טַבַּעַת הַמֶּלֶךְ / the king's signet ring
- What it is: the king's ring, used to seal the decree
- Function in scene: the decree is "written in the name of King Ahasuerus and sealed with the king's ring" — made irreversible
- Signals: the seal that turns Haman's command into the king's unbreakable word

[[I2-The-law-dat]] — דָּת / the law (dat)
- What it is: the decree given the force of law in every province, the published edict
- Function in scene: the writing is "to be given as law in every province," made binding everywhere
- Signals: difference-made-crime now made statute across the empire

**3D — Times**
[[TM_NISAN]] — חֹדֶשׁ הָרִאשׁוֹן / the first month
- What it is: the day the decree is written — the thirteenth day of the first month, Nisan
- Function: dates the issuing of the sentence at the very start of the year
- Signals: nearly the whole year stands between the decree and the day it names — the gap the rescue will need

[[TM_ADAR]] — חֹדֶשׁ אֲדָר / the month of Adar
- What it is: the day set for the killing — the thirteenth of the twelfth month, Adar
- Function: fixes the deadline the rest of the book races against
- Signals: the appointed day of destruction, named in the decree itself

**3E — What Happens**
On the thirteenth day of the first month the king's scribes were summoned, and the decree was written out — exactly as [[B3-Haman]] Haman commanded — to the king's satraps, to the governors over every province, and to the princes of every people, each province in its own script and each people in its own tongue; it was written in the name of King [[B4-Ahasuerus]] Ahasuerus and sealed with [[TH_SIGNET_RING]] the king's ring. The [[TH_EDICT]] letters were sent by couriers to all the king's [[I5-The-127-provinces]] provinces: to destroy, to kill, and to annihilate all [[B17-The-Jews]] the Jews — young and old, children and women — in one day, the thirteenth of [[TM_ADAR]] the twelfth month, and to take their goods as plunder. A copy of the writing was to be given as [[I2-The-law-dat]] law in every province and published to every people, that they be ready for that day. The couriers went out, driven in haste by the king's word, and the decree was issued in [[PL1-Susa-the-citadel]] Susa the citadel. And the king and Haman sat down to drink — while the city of Susa was bewildered.

**3F — Communicative Purpose**
Shows the sentence made unbreakable and sent across the empire — and the chilling close: the two men drinking while the capital reels.

**Significant Absence**
Even here, where a whole people is condemned by name in writing, no God is named — not in the decree, not in the narration, not over the bewildered city. No one in the scene speaks for the condemned; no Jew, no Mordecai, no Esther appears yet to answer it. And the narrator withholds all comment on the killing itself, reporting it in the same flat voice as the couriers' errand — the only crack of feeling is the last word, that the city was *bewildered*, set against the two men calmly at their cups.
