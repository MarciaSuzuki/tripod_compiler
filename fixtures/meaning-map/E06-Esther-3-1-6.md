# E06 — Esther 3:1–6

## 1. Metadata
- **Pericope title:** Haman raised, Mordecai unbowed: one man's refusal becomes a death-sentence for a people
- **BCV:** Esther 3:1–6
- **Genre Group:** NARRATIVE
- **Genre:** HISTORICAL_NARRATIVE
- **Register:** INFORMAL_CASUAL

**Multi-level register tagging:**
The whole passage sits in INFORMAL_CASUAL — the storyteller's plain voice, reporting a promotion, a standoff at a gate, and a man's hidden rage from the outside. There is no decree voiced here in its own words: the king's order to bow (v.2, "for so the king had commanded concerning him") is *reported* by the narrator, not quoted as an edict, so it draws no FORMAL_OFFICIAL override. The one stretch of direct speech is the servants' nagging question to Mordecai in v.3 ("Why do you transgress the king's commandment?") — ordinary speech among courtiers, which stays INFORMAL_CASUAL. The pericope's weight is interior: Haman's fury and his silent resolve to wipe out a whole people. That interior turn is reported plainly, not lifted into a grander register.

## 2. Level 1 — Whole-Passage Movement
### 2.1 Prose Arc / Shape / Argument / Burden / Concern
The passage turns on a single refusal and the monstrous thing it grows into. It opens with a sudden elevation: the king promotes Haman the Agagite and sets his seat above all the other officials, so that everyone at the king's gate kneels and bows to him by royal order. Against this universal bowing stands one still figure — Mordecai, who will not kneel and will not bow. The other servants press him about it day after day; he will not listen, and tells them only that he is a Jew. They take the matter to Haman to see whether Mordecai's stand will hold. When Haman sees for himself that this one man will not bow, he is filled with fury — and then the passage makes its terrible leap: Haman scorns the idea of striking at Mordecai alone, and because he now knows the man's people, he sets out to destroy not one Jew but every Jew in the whole empire. The burden of the passage is how a small, stubborn act of conscience collides with a great man's pride, and how that wounded pride reaches at once past the offender to his entire nation.

### 2.2 Context
This is the hinge on which the danger of the book swings shut. Earlier pericopes built the court, raised Esther quietly to the throne, and let Mordecai save the king's life unrewarded. Now a rival is lifted instead, and the long shadow of an old enmity falls across the story: Haman is named "the Agagite," tying him to Agag, king of the Amalekites — Israel's ancient enemy — while Mordecai stands in for his people. The bowing Mordecai withholds is the spark, but the fire it lights is the book's central threat: a decreed extermination of the Jews throughout the Persian empire. Everything the rest of the book races to undo begins here, in a gate, with a man who would not bend. And still, as throughout this book, the name of God is nowhere — the deliverance to come is being set up entirely off-stage.

### 2.3 Emotion / Tone / Pace
The tone tightens from ceremony to menace. It begins almost formally — a promotion, a universal homage — then narrows to a single point of friction: one man, standing while everyone else bows. The pace is patient through the middle, marked by the repeated "day after day" of the servants' questioning, a slow grinding that draws the reader toward the breaking point. Then it accelerates and darkens fast: Haman *sees*, Haman is *filled with fury*, Haman *scorns* the small revenge and *seeks* the large one. The last verse is the coldest in the passage — the leap from one despised man to a whole people marked for destruction is made almost matter-of-factly, which is what makes it chilling.

### 2.4 Communicative Function
The passage introduces the book's antagonist at full height and immediately sets him against the book's quiet hero, then shows the disproportion of his response — the move from a private slight to a national death-sentence. It establishes Haman's character (proud, easily enraged, vindictive past all measure), Mordecai's (immovable, defined here by his Jewishness), and the stakes (the survival of an entire people). To speak this passage is to light the fuse: everything tense and urgent in the chapters ahead is already burning by its final line.

**3E — What Happens**
After these things, [[B4-Ahasuerus]] King Ahasuerus promoted [[B3-Haman]] Haman son of Hammedatha the Agagite — he raised him up, and set his seat above all the princes who were with him.

**3F — Communicative Purpose**
Lifts Haman to the top of the court, establishing the height from which the conflict will fall.

**Significant Absence**
No reason is given for the promotion — no service rendered, no merit named (and pointedly, just before this, it was *Mordecai* who saved the king's life and went unrewarded). And no god is named over this raising-up; the elevation is the king's bare choice. The "Agagite" tag is dropped without explanation, left for the reader who knows the old story to feel its weight.

- Role: the place where the king's servants gather, bow, and confront Mordecai
- Type: the palace gate — the seat of official business and access to the king
- Meaning: the public arena of the court, where rank and homage are on display
- Effect on scene: stages the standoff in the open, before everyone — Mordecai's refusal cannot be hidden

**3C — Objects and Elements**

- Function in scene: the rule Mordecai breaks; the grounds the servants cite against him
- Signals: homage made compulsory by royal authority — to refuse is to "transgress"

- What it is: the single explanation Mordecai offers for his refusal — that he is a Jew
- Function in scene: turns a personal standoff into a matter of peoplehood and identity
- Signals: the refusal is rooted in who he is, not mere stubbornness; it foreshadows the threat to a whole people

**3D — Times**
Day after day — יֹום וָיֹום / day by day *(scene content — the repeated, grinding interval; not a dated time)*
- What it is: the stretch over which the servants press Mordecai, again and again
- Function: shows the standoff hardening over time, not flaring once
- Signals: a slow build toward the breaking point; Mordecai's settled, repeated refusal

**3E — What Happens**
All the king's servants who were in the king's gate were kneeling and bowing to [[B3-Haman]] Haman, for so the king had commanded concerning him; but [[B1-Mordecai]] Mordecai would not kneel and would not bow. The king's servants who were in the gate said to him, "Why do you transgress the king's commandment?" And it came about, as they spoke to him day after day and he did not listen to them, that they told [[B3-Haman]] Haman, to see whether [[B1-Mordecai]] Mordecai's stand would hold — for he had told them that he was a Jew.

**3F — Communicative Purpose**
Sets one man's refusal against the whole court's homage, ties the refusal to his Jewish identity, and hands the matter to Haman.

**Significant Absence**
Mordecai gives no reason but "I am a Jew" — the text withholds any further explanation (no appeal to the commandment against bowing, no theology stated). The book's signature silence holds: God is never named as the ground of the refusal, even though the refusal is the book's first act of faithful defiance. And Mordecai never answers the servants' "why" — he simply does not listen, leaving the gap they then carry to Haman.

### Scene 3 — Haman's fury and the plot against a whole people (v.5–6)

**3A — Beings**
[[B3-Haman]] — הָמָן / Haman
- Role: the enraged favourite who scorns a narrow revenge and reaches for a total one
- Relationship: the man Mordecai will not honour; now bent on annihilation
- Presence: PRESENT

[[B1-Mordecai]] — מָרְדֳּכַי / Mordecai (the Jew)
- Role: the man who will not bow, whose refusal Haman now sees for himself
- Relationship: the single offender — and, through his people, the occasion for a far larger target
- Presence: REFERENCED

[[B17-The-Jews]] — הַיְּהוּדִים / the Jews (Mordecai's people) *(here first entering the book as the threatened nation)*
- Role: the whole people Haman now sets out to destroy
- Relationship: Mordecai's people (עַם מָרְדֳּכָי), scattered through all the empire
- Presence: REFERENCED

**3B — Places**
The whole kingdom of Ahasuerus — כָּל־מַלְכוּת אֲחַשְׁוֵרֹושׁ / all the kingdom of Ahasuerus *(scene content — the empire-wide reach of the intended destruction)*
- Role: the full extent over which Haman aims his plot
- Type: the entire Persian empire
- Meaning: names how far the danger reaches — every province, every Jew in it
- Effect on scene: stretches a private grudge to the scale of an empire-wide extermination

**3C — Objects and Elements**
Striking with the hand — לִשְׁלֹחַ יָד / to lay a hand (on Mordecai alone) *(scene content — the narrow revenge Haman scorns)*
- What it is: the idea of killing Mordecai by himself
- Function in scene: the small vengeance Haman rejects as beneath him — too little to satisfy the slight
- Signals: pride that disdains a proportionate answer and reaches for the monstrous instead

**3D — Times**
- None: no dated time-setting; the fury and the plot follow directly on the servants' report.

**3E — What Happens**
When [[B3-Haman]] Haman saw that [[B1-Mordecai]] Mordecai would not kneel or bow to him, Haman was filled with fury. But he scorned to lay a hand on Mordecai alone — for they had told him Mordecai's people — and so Haman sought to destroy all [[B17-The-Jews]] the Jews, the people of Mordecai, throughout the whole kingdom of [[B4-Ahasuerus]] Ahasuerus.

**3F — Communicative Purpose**
Shows Haman's rage breaking out, and the catastrophic widening of his aim from one man to an entire people across the empire.

**Significant Absence**
Haman's leap from Mordecai to "all the Jews" is made with no stated deliberation, no counsel, no pause — the disproportion is left bare and uncommented. And the people now marked for death are named only as "Mordecai's people," with no hint yet of the deliverance to come and, once more, no mention of God — the threat stands alone, unanswered, at the pericope's close.
