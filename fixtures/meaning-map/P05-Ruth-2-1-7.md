# P05 — Ruth 2:1–7

## 1. Metadata
- **Pericope title:** Boaz introduced as kinsman; Ruth's gleaning initiative; her chance arrival in Boaz's portion; the foreman's report
- **BCV:** Ruth 2:1–7
- **Genre Group:** NARRATIVE
- **Genre:** HISTORICAL_NARRATIVE
- **Register:** INFORMAL_CASUAL

**Multi-level register tagging:**
Pericope-level default is INFORMAL_CASUAL, the unmarked default register for biblical narrator voice. Scene 2 (Ruth's gleaning initiative at v.2) carries scene-level INTIMATE — daughter-in-law and mother-in-law speaking face-to-face inside their dwelling, no community audience and no institutional venue; the relational-close register fits the moment better than INFORMAL_CASUAL even though the speech contains a request-and-release exchange. Scenes 1, 3, and 4 stay at the INFORMAL_CASUAL narrator default. The v.4 greeting exchange holds covenant-blessing weight at the form level (the YHWH-greeting and the return-blessing), but this institutional weight is carried by figure flags and downstream encoding, not by elevated register. CONSULTATIVE is not used for narrator voice (hard rule).

## 2. Level 1 — Whole-Passage Movement

### 2.1 Prose Arc / Shape / Argument / Burden / Concern
The pericope moves from a narrator-parenthetical to a field-community exchange. The narrator pauses at v.1 to introduce Boaz before he acts; Ruth proposes the gleaning to Naomi and gets her release; she goes, gleans, and lands in Boaz's portion through a chance-construction the narrator holds open between coincidence and providence; Boaz arrives and exchanges a YHWH-greeting with his harvesters; he asks his foreman whose young woman she is, and the foreman reports her identity and her work-pattern. The burden is the audience-and-character gap: the audience knows the kinsman who could redeem Elimelech's line has just been named, while Ruth and Boaz have not yet met. The concern is whether the chance-arrival in Boaz's portion will become recognition.

### 2.2 Context
The harvest frame opened at the close of the previous pericope is now the live setting. Naomi and Ruth have arrived in Bethlehem; Naomi has declared herself empty; the barley harvest is beginning. The pericope opens chapter 2 as the first field-scene of the book. Boaz is being introduced before he enters the storyline because he is the kinsman the audience needs in order to read what follows. The clan-of-Elimelech frame the narrator establishes at v.1 and restates at v.3 is the legal-relational basis for the kinsman-redeemer pattern the book will work out.

### 2.3 Emotion / Tone / Pace
Quiet and economical. The pace narrows at v.1 around the narrator-parenthetical, holds at v.2 for the interior dialogue, widens at v.3 with the chance-providence framing, and settles into chronicle-pace at v.4-7 with the greeting and the foreman's report. The dominant mood is anticipation under restraint — the narrator names what the characters do not yet see, and lets the audience hold the gap.

### 2.4 Communicative Function
Opens chapter 2 by introducing Boaz as kinsman of Elimelech before he acts; positions Ruth and Boaz in the same field through a chance-construction that holds providence open; reactivates the Moabite epithet on Ruth, now in a third-party voice for the first time in Bethlehem; opens the chayil cross-pair that brackets Boaz at the field and Ruth at the threshing floor; stages the gleaning institution as the legal-customary frame the rest of the chapter assumes; advances the line-and-inheritance question at the audience level even before the two protagonists meet face-to-face.

[[B2-Elimelech]] — אֱלִימֶלֶך / Elimelech
- Role: the ancestor-frame through whom Boaz is positioned; named twice (once as Naomi's husband, once in the clan-phrase)

- Relationship: includes [[B2-Elimelech]], [[B13-Boaz]]; later includes the nearer redeemer and the dead sons

**3C — Objects and Elements**

- Signals: the narrator anchors the introduction to Naomi, not to Boaz himself; structurally significant

- What it is: the compound chayil-formula by which the narrator introduces Boaz; carries connotations of substance, capability, and worth in the community
- Function in scene: marks Boaz as someone of standing before he speaks; the chayil lexeme opens a cross-pericope pair that will close when the same lexeme is used of Ruth
- Signals: introduction-formula for a person of standing; cross-pericope pair with eshet chayil at 3:11
- Cross-ref: [[CB_0032-Chayil]], [[FIG_0090-Ish-Gibbor-Chayil]], [[FIG_0134-Chayil-Pair-Completed]]

מִמִּשְׁפַּחַת אֱלִימֶלֶך / "of the clan of Elimelech" — clan-locator phrase
- What it is: the clan-frame that places Boaz inside the kin-group of Elimelech
- Function in scene: grounds the kinsman-redeemer possibility in legal-relational kin-structure before the storyline reaches it
- Signals: the clan-frame will be restated at v.3 when Ruth lands in Boaz's portion

וּשְׁמוֹ בֹּעַז / "and his name was Boaz" — narrator-naming form
- What it is: the explicit naming of Boaz at the end of the parenthetical
- Function in scene: completes the introduction; name placed last, after relation, attribute, and clan
- Signals: chronicle-narrator pattern — relation first, then attribute, then clan, then name

**3D — Times**
- None: no distinct temporal frame for this scene; the narrator-parenthetical is given outside the storyline's time-flow.

**3E — What Happens**
The narrator pauses the storyline to introduce [[B13-Boaz]] Boaz. He is a kinsman of [[B3-Naomi]] Naomi's husband [[B2-Elimelech]] Elimelech, a man of standing, and a member of [[B29-The-Clan-of-Elimelech]] the clan of Elimelech.

**3F — Communicative Purpose**
Introduces Boaz to the audience before he enters the storyline. Anchors him in the kin-network through Naomi's relation to her dead husband and through the clan-frame.

**Significant Absence**
The narrator does not say where Boaz is or what he is doing at the moment of introduction. The narrator gives no interior state — no motive, no inclination toward the women, no awareness of their return. The narrator-parenthetical names him, attributes him, places him in the clan, and stops.

### Scene 2 — Ruth's gleaning initiative (v.2)

**3A — Beings**
[[B9-Ruth]] — רוּת הַמּוֹאֲבִיָּה / Ruth the Moabitess
- Role: speaking subject; proposes the gleaning to Naomi
- Relationship: daughter-in-law of [[B3-Naomi]] Naomi; the narrator names her with the Moabite epithet at this speech-event
- Presence: PRESENT

[[B3-Naomi]] — נָעֳמִי / Naomi
- Role: addressee of Ruth's request; grants permission
- Relationship: mother-in-law of [[B9-Ruth]] Ruth
- Presence: PRESENT

**3B — Places**
[[PL_NAOMIS_DWELLING-Naomis-Dwelling-in-Bethlehem]] — Naomi's dwelling (implicit)
- Role: setting of the interior dialogue between Ruth and Naomi
- Type: private household space
- Meaning: not named in the text; structurally implicit at every interior scene between the two women
- Effect on scene: holds the speech inside a private relational space, distinct from the public arrival-scene at the close of the previous pericope

הַשָּׂדֶה / "the field" — referenced destination
- Role: where Ruth proposes to go
- Type: agricultural field (no specific owner named at this point in Ruth's speech)
- Meaning: the harvest-locus generally available for gleaning; not yet identified with Boaz's portion
- Effect on scene: identifies the locus toward which Ruth is moving by her own initiative

**3C — Objects and Elements**
אֵלְכָה־נָּא / "let me go, please" — request-permission cohortative
- What it is: the first-person cohortative with the *na*-particle; the request form by which Ruth proposes the action to her mother-in-law
- Function in scene: marks Ruth's speech as request-asking-permission, not as directing or as informing
- Signals: source-text discipline — Ruth proposes; Naomi does not propose

וַאֲלַקֳטָה בַשִּׁבֳּלִים / "let me glean among the ears of grain"
- What it is: Ruth's proposed action; the gleaning-among-ears formulation
- Function in scene: names the action Ruth proposes — the institutional gleaning-right
- Signals: assumes the gleaning institution as the legal-customary frame for the poor and the foreigner
- Cross-ref: [[CB_0034-Leket-Gleaning]]

אַחַר אֲשֶׁר אֶמְצָא־חֵן בְּעֵינָיו / "after one in whose eyes I find favor"
- What it is: the favor-in-eyes formulation; Ruth's qualification of who she will glean behind
- Function in scene: names the kind of person Ruth will follow — one who shows favor; carries the *hen* concept that will recur across the book
- Signals: first occurrence of the favor-in-eyes formulation on Ruth's lips
- Cross-ref: [[CB_0033-Hen-Favor]], [[FIG_0018-Favor-in-Eyes]]

לְכִי בִתִּי / "go, my daughter" — grants-permission form
- What it is: Naomi's release; brief permission-granting reply with the daughter-form of address
- Function in scene: grants Ruth permission to act on her initiative; brief and immediate
- Signals: Naomi does not amend or qualify; her release matches Ruth's request in form (go-imperative with daughter-form of address)

**3D — Times**
- None: no distinct temporal frame for this scene; time is continuous with the previous pericope's arrival.

**3E — What Happens**
[[B9-Ruth]] Ruth said to [[B3-Naomi]] Naomi: "Let me go, please, to the field and glean among the ears of grain after one in whose eyes I find favor." Naomi said to her: "Go, my daughter."

**3F — Communicative Purpose**
Establishes that the gleaning move comes from Ruth's own initiative, not Naomi's. Naomi releases her without amending the proposal. The exchange is short, intimate, and consequential — it places Ruth in the field.

**Significant Absence**
Naomi does not name a specific field. Naomi does not propose the gleaning herself. Naomi does not bless Ruth, does not warn her of any danger, does not suggest a kinsman, does not name Boaz — even though the narrator has just named Boaz to the audience in the previous verse. The audience knows what Naomi does not yet say.

- Function in scene: lands Ruth in Boaz's portion through a construction the narrator deliberately does not resolve; YHWH is not named as agent

- Signals: chronicle-narrator attention-marker

- What it is: Boaz's greeting to the harvesters; brief invocation-blessing form
- Function in scene: names the divine name in greeting; standard Israelite greeting carrying covenant weight
- Signals: first dialogic invocation of YHWH in the book in pure blessing form (not lament, not vow)
- Cross-ref: [[FIG_0092-YHWH-Be-with-You-Greeting]], [[CB_0008-Blessing]]

יְבָרֶכְךָ יְהוָה / "YHWH bless you" — return-blessing form
- What it is: the harvesters' return-blessing; matches Boaz's greeting in form
- Function in scene: completes the greeting exchange; standard Israelite return-blessing
- Signals: structurally pairs with the opening greeting
- Cross-ref: [[FIG_0093-YHWH-Bless-You-Return-Greeting]], [[CB_0008-Blessing]]

**3D — Times**

**3E — What Happens**
[[B9-Ruth]] Ruth went, came, and gleaned in [[PL5-Field]] the field behind [[B14-Harvesters]] the reapers. Her chance chanced upon [[PL5_BOAZ_PORTION-Boazs-Portion-of-the-Field]] the portion of the field belonging to [[B13-Boaz]] Boaz, who was of [[B29-The-Clan-of-Elimelech]] the clan of Elimelech. **Significant Absence**

- Function in scene: gives the audience Ruth's words to the foreman through the foreman's recall; the request-form matches her v.2 request to Naomi in cohortative-with-*na* shape
- Signals: quoted-speech-within-speech; the foreman is the relay channel

וַתָּבוֹא וַתַּעֲמוֹד מֵאָז הַבֹּקֶר וְעַד־עַתָּה / "she came and has continued from morning until now"
- What it is: the foreman's statement of Ruth's work-pattern through the morning
- Function in scene: reports persistence and continuity of effort; the *vata'amod* verb carries "standing / persevering"
- Signals: the work-pattern is part of what the foreman tells Boaz about Ruth; gives Boaz a basis for the favor he will show in the next pericope

זֶה שִׁבְתָּהּ הַבַּיִת מְעָט / "her resting in the shelter has been only a little" — textually disputed
- What it is: the closing clause of the foreman's report on Ruth; the Hebrew syntax and the precise referent are textually disputed across standard commentaries; readings range from "she has rested only a little in the shelter" to "she has not rested even for a moment"
- Function in scene: closes the foreman's report with a comment on Ruth's brief rest (or lack of it); the textual uncertainty is held open
- Signals: textual-clarity flag; do not resolve

**3D — Times**
מֵאָז הַבֹּקֶר וְעַד־עַתָּה / "from morning until now" — within-day duration in the foreman's report
- What it is: a duration named inside the foreman's quoted report; not a scene-setting temporal frame
- Function: marks Ruth's continued work through the morning to the present moment in the foreman's voice
- Signals: within-day duration; content element carried in 3C rather than a 3D scene-setting frame, following the P01 precedent for approximate-duration phrases

**3E — What Happens**
[[B13-Boaz]] Boaz said to [[B15-The-Foreman]] his young man standing over [[B14-Harvesters]] the reapers: "Whose young woman is this?" The young man standing over the reapers answered and said: "She is a Moabite young woman, the one who returned with [[B3-Naomi]] Naomi from [[PL2-Fields-of-Moab]] the fields of Moab. And she said: 'Let me glean, please, and gather among the sheaves after the reapers.' So she came and has continued from morning until now; her resting in [[PL_HA_BAYIT_FIELD_SHELTER-The-Shelter-at-the-Field]] the shelter has been only a little."

**3F — Communicative Purpose**
Stages the first public identification of Ruth in the field-community: Boaz asks under whose cover this young woman is, and the foreman reports her ethnic identity, her relation to Naomi, her quoted gleaning-request, and her work-pattern from morning until now. The foreman's report grounds Ruth in the home-community's knowledge of the women's return.

**Significant Absence**
The foreman does not give Ruth's personal name. Identification is by ethnic identity, by relation to Naomi, and by work-pattern; the name "Ruth" does not appear in the foreman's voice. The narrator does not give Boaz's interior state at the question — no surprise, no recognition, no inclination is attributed. Boaz's question carries no marked emotional weight in the narrator's framing.
