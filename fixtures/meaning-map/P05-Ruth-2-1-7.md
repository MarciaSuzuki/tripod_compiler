# P05 — Ruth 2:1–7

## 1. Metadata
- **Pericope title:** Boaz introduced as kinsman; Ruth's gleaning initiative; her chance arrival in Boaz's portion; the foreman's report
- **BCV:** Ruth 2:1–7
- **Genre Group:** NARRATIVE
- **Genre:** HISTORICAL_NARRATIVE
- **Register:** INFORMAL_CASUAL

**Multi-level register tagging:**
The whole passage sits in INFORMAL_CASUAL. Scene 2 — Ruth's plan to go gleaning at v.2 — shifts to INTIMATE: a daughter-in-law and her mother-in-law speaking face to face at home, with no crowd and no formal setting; that close key fits the moment better than the plain default, even though what passes between them is a request and Naomi's go-ahead. Scenes 1, 3, and 4 stay in the plain narrator key. The greeting at v.4 carries the weight of a blessing (Boaz greets the reapers in YHWH's name, and they bless him back), but that weight is carried by the figures and the deeper encoding, not by a grander way of speaking.

## 2. Level 1 — Whole-Passage Movement

### 2.1 Prose Arc / Shape / Argument / Burden / Concern
The passage moves from a quiet aside by the narrator into a scene out in the fields. The narrator stops at v.1 to introduce Boaz before he does anything. Ruth asks Naomi if she may go and glean, and Naomi lets her. She goes out, gleans, and happens to end up in Boaz's field — and the narrator tells it so you can't quite tell whether it was luck or God's quiet hand. Boaz comes out and greets his reapers in the LORD's name. He asks the man over them whose young woman this is, and the man reports who she is and how she has worked. What weighs on the passage is the gap between what the audience knows and what the people in the story know: the audience has just been told that here is the kinsman who could rescue Elimelech's line, while Ruth and Boaz have not yet so much as met. The question it leaves hanging is whether this chance landing in Boaz's field will turn into his noticing her.

### 2.2 Context
The harvest named at the end of the last pericope is now where the story is happening. Naomi and Ruth have reached Bethlehem; Naomi has called herself empty; the barley harvest is just starting. This passage opens chapter 2, the first scene out in the fields. Boaz is introduced before he steps into the story because he is the kinsman the audience needs to know about to follow what comes next. The clan of Elimelech, which the narrator names at v.1 and says again at v.3, is the family-and-legal tie that makes the kinsman-redeemer pattern of the book possible — a near relative who can step in to rescue a dead man's family and land.

### 2.3 Emotion / Tone / Pace
Quiet and spare. The telling slows at v.1 for the narrator's aside, holds at v.2 for the private talk between the two women, opens out at v.3 where Ruth lands in Boaz's field by what may be luck or may be God's hand, and settles into plain reporting at v.4-7 with the greeting and the man's report. The mood all through is a held-back waiting — the narrator names what the people in the story cannot yet see, and lets the audience sit with knowing more than they do.

### 2.4 Communicative Function
This passage opens chapter 2 by introducing Boaz, a kinsman of Elimelech, before he does anything. It brings Ruth and Boaz into the same field by a turn the narrator tells so you can't tell if it is chance or God's doing. It lets someone call Ruth "the Moabite" again — now on a stranger's lips, the first time in Bethlehem. It opens the "worth" thread (the word used of Boaz here comes back when Ruth is called a worthy woman at the threshing floor). It sets up gleaning — the custom that let the poor and the foreigner gather leftover grain — as the legal-and-customary frame the rest of the chapter takes for granted. And it pushes forward the question of the family line and inheritance for the audience, even before the two of them have met.

**3B — Places**

- Role: where Ruth asks to go
- Type: a grain field (Ruth names no owner yet)

**3C — Objects and Elements**
[[CB_0033-Hen-Favor]] — חֵן / favor
- What it is: kindness shown by someone who has the say over how things turn out
- Function in scene: the kindness Ruth hopes to find with whoever owns the field she gleans in
- Signals: first named here at 2:2; comes back in Ruth's words with Boaz at 2:10 and 2:13

[[CB_0034-Leket-Gleaning]] — לֶקֶט / gleaning
- What it is: the custom, backed by law, that let the poor and the resident foreigner gather the grain the harvesters leave behind
- Function in scene: the practice Ruth proposes to take up on her own
- Signals: first named here at 2:2; the custom the rest of the chapter takes for granted

**3D — Times**
- None: no distinct temporal frame for this scene; time is continuous with the previous pericope's arrival.

**3E — What Happens**
[[B9-Ruth]] Ruth said to [[B3-Naomi]] Naomi: "Let me go, please, to the field and glean among the ears of grain after one in whose eyes I find favor." Naomi said to her: "Go, my daughter."

**3F — Communicative Purpose**
Shows that the gleaning is Ruth's own idea, not Naomi's. Naomi lets her go without changing a word of it. The exchange is short and close, and it matters — it puts Ruth in the field.

**Significant Absence**
Naomi names no particular field. She does not suggest the gleaning herself. She does not bless Ruth, does not warn her of any danger, does not point her to a kinsman, does not name Boaz — even though the narrator has just named Boaz to the audience one verse before. The audience knows the very thing Naomi does not yet say.

- Type: a shelter at the field; just what it is, the text leaves unclear
- Meaning: a shelter or shaded spot at the field where Ruth has rested — or, by another reading, has hardly rested at all; the Hebrew at v.7b is read different ways across the major commentaries
- Effect on scene: ties the foreman's report to a real place out in the field; the unclear wording is left open

**3C — Objects and Elements**
מֵאָז הַבֹּקֶר וְעַד־עַתָּה / "from morning until now" — how long she has worked
- What it is: a stretch of time named inside the foreman's report; a piece of what he says, not the time-setting for the scene
- Function in scene: marks how Ruth has kept at it from morning to the present moment, in the foreman's own words
- Signals: a span within the one day; carried in §3C as content (the same way P01 handles "about ten years"), not as a §3D time-setting

**3D — Times**
- None: no separate time-setting for this scene; "from morning until now" is content held in §3C, not a time-setting for the scene — the same way P01 handles "about ten years."

**3E — What Happens**
[[B13-Boaz]] Boaz said to [[B15-The-Foreman]] his young man standing over [[B14-Harvesters]] the reapers: "Whose young woman is this?" The young man standing over the reapers answered and said: "She is a Moabite young woman, the one who returned with [[B3-Naomi]] Naomi from [[PL2-Fields-of-Moab]] the fields of Moab. And she said: 'Let me glean, please, and gather among the sheaves after the reapers.' So she came and has continued from morning until now; her resting in [[PL_HA_BAYIT_FIELD_SHELTER-The-Shelter-at-the-Field]] the shelter has been only a little."

**3F — Communicative Purpose**
Shows the first time Ruth is named out loud among the field-workers: Boaz asks whose young woman this is, and the foreman tells him where she is from, how she is tied to Naomi, what she asked when she came to glean, and how she has worked from morning until now. The foreman's report ties Ruth to what the home-community already knows about the women's return.

**Significant Absence**
The foreman never gives Ruth's own name. He places her by where she is from, by her tie to Naomi, and by how she has worked; the name "Ruth" is not on his lips. And the narrator tells us nothing of what is in Boaz as he asks — no surprise, no recognition, no leaning toward her. In the way the narrator frames it, Boaz's question carries no particular feeling.
