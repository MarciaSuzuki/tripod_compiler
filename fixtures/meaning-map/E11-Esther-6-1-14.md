# E11 — Esther 6:1–14

## 1. Metadata
- **Pericope title:** The sleepless night and the reversal: Haman honours Mordecai
- **BCV:** Esther 6:1–14
- **Genre Group:** NARRATIVE
- **Genre:** HISTORICAL_NARRATIVE
- **Register:** INFORMAL_CASUAL

**Multi-level register tagging:**
The whole passage sits in INFORMAL_CASUAL — the plain, fast voice of a storyteller telling the night the whole book turns over. There is a great deal of quoted speech here (the king's questions, Haman's misjudged answer, the king's command, the wife's omen), but quoted dialogue inside a narrative is still narration — it does not lift the register. No royal decree or written edict is *issued or read out word-for-word* in this pericope; the chronicle is summarised, not quoted, and Haman's prescription for honour is private court-advice spoken to the king, not a published law. So there is no FORMAL_OFFICIAL moment-override anywhere in E11. The COMMUNITY_MEMORY framing-lift is reserved for the book's open and close and does not apply here.

## 2. Level 1 — Whole-Passage Movement
### 2.1 Prose Arc / Shape / Argument / Burden / Concern
This is the hinge of the whole book, and it turns on a sleepless night. The king cannot sleep, so the royal chronicle is read aloud to him — and there, by chance, is the record that Mordecai once exposed a plot against the king's life and was never rewarded. At that exact moment Haman arrives at the palace, come to ask permission to hang Mordecai on the gallows he has built. Before he can speak his errand, the king asks him how to honour a man the king delights to honour. Haman, sure it must be himself, prescribes the most extravagant thing he can imagine — the king's own robe, the king's own horse, and a herald crying the man's worth through the city. Then the trap shuts: the king tells Haman to do all of it, every word, for Mordecai the Jew. Haman must lead his enemy through the streets, proclaiming his glory with his own mouth. He hurries home in mourning, his head covered, and his wife and friends — who days ago urged him to build the gallows — now speak the book's verdict over him: if Mordecai is of Jewish stock, Haman will not prevail; he will surely fall. The movement is a single, exact inversion — the man who came to destroy is made to exalt — and it lands without one event being credited to anyone the book will name.

### 2.2 Context
This sits at the centre of Esther, the turning-point everything before has been bending toward and everything after will unwind from. Haman, the king's highest official, has already cast lots and bought a decree to destroy all the Jews of the empire; the night before this, he has had a gallows built to hang Mordecai in particular. Mordecai is the Jew who sits in the king's gate, who once uncovered an assassination plot — recorded but unrewarded — and whose refusal to bow set Haman's hatred in motion. Queen Esther, secretly a Jew and Mordecai's kin, has just begun the two banquets at which she will spring her counter-plea. The reader knows what the characters do not, and what the book never says aloud: that the "chance" timing of the sleepless night, the reading, and Haman's arrival are the quiet hand the whole story turns on — the deliverance no one in the book ever names as God's.

### 2.3 Emotion / Tone / Pace
The tone is taut and quietly delighted — a tightening trap, savoured. After the slow banquets, the pace here is quick and overlapping: the night, the reading, Haman's arrival, the cross-purposed questions all crowd together. The dramatic irony is the engine — we know Haman is describing his own humiliation while he gloats, and the scene lets the dread build in him as the king's command lands word by word. The mood breaks open at the end into open reversal: the long street-procession, then Haman fleeing home in mourning with his head covered, then the cold finality of his own wife's omen. Underneath the comedy of the trap runs something graver — a sense of a verdict being handed down — but the narrator never raises his voice to say so.

### 2.4 Communicative Function
The passage executes the great reversal of the book and shows it executed by the villain's own hand and mouth. It pays off the planted record of Mordecai's loyalty (the chronicle), it springs the irony built up across the banquets, and it turns Haman from hunter to herald in a single scene. It also installs the book's verdict in the mouths of Haman's own household — "you will surely fall before him" — naming the pattern (a Jew rising, his enemy falling) that the rest of the book will carry out. To speak this passage is to deliver the turn: the night the unrewarded loyalty is remembered, the enemy made to honour his target, and the first word spoken of his coming fall.

**3E — What Happens**
The king asked who was in the court — and [[B3-Haman]] Haman had just come into the outer court of the king's house, to ask the king to hang [[B1-Mordecai]] Mordecai on [[TH_GALLOWS]] the gallows he had prepared for him. The attendants said Haman was standing in the court, and the king said: let him come in. So Haman came in, and the king asked him: what should be done for the man the king delights to honour? So Haman said: for such a man, let them bring [[TH_ROYAL_ROBE]] a royal robe the king has worn, and [[TH_ROYAL_HORSE]] a horse the king has ridden, with a royal crown on its head; and let the robe and the horse be put in the hand of one of the king's noblest princes, and let them clothe the man and lead him on the horse through the open square of the city, crying before him, "Thus it is done for the man the king delights to honour."

**3F — Communicative Purpose**
Springs the irony: Haman, come to ask for Mordecai's death, mistakes himself for the man to be honoured and prescribes the most lavish honours he can imagine.

**Significant Absence**
Haman never gets to speak his errand — the request to hang Mordecai stays unspoken, swallowed by the king's question; the narrator lets us know it only in passing. And the king never says *whom* he means; the name is withheld, so Haman convicts himself in the gap. No one — not the king, not Haman — invokes any higher will; the perfectly-timed cross-purpose simply happens, with no hand named over it.

**3F — Communicative Purpose**

**Significant Absence**
No protest from Haman is recorded — between the king's command and Haman's obeying it, the narrator gives not one word of his fury or his pleading; the silence is the humiliation. And no one names the justice on display: the perfect symmetry of the man made to exalt the man he meant to hang is left to stand bare, uncredited to any hand.
