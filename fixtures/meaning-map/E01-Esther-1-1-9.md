# E01 — Esther 1:1–9

## 1. Metadata
- **Pericope title:** The empire on display: the king's two banquets and the queen's feast
- **BCV:** Esther 1:1–9
- **Genre Group:** NARRATIVE
- **Genre:** HISTORICAL_NARRATIVE
- **Register:** INFORMAL_CASUAL

**Multi-level register tagging:**
The whole passage sits in INFORMAL_CASUAL — the plain voice of a storyteller recalling a famous court. One spot lifts at v.1: the set opening phrase *vayhi bimei Achashverosh* ("and it happened in the days of Ahasuerus") raises the voice into the formal key of an old, remembered tale — COMMUNITY_MEMORY (this marks the framing, not the register). The register itself stays INFORMAL_CASUAL throughout, even where the content is grand: the narrator reports the empire's reach, the wealth, and the wine-protocol of v.8 plainly, from the outside, never adopting the voice of a decree.

## 2. Level 1 — Whole-Passage Movement
### 2.1 Prose Arc / Shape / Argument / Burden / Concern
The passage opens at the widest possible angle — a single king ruling 127 provinces from India to Cush — and then spends itself almost entirely on display: two enormous banquets, one after the other. First a half-year-long showing of royal wealth to the empire's nobility, then a seven-day feast thrown open to everyone in the capital, drowning in gold, fine cloth, and wine poured without limit. The movement is not really a sequence of events but a swelling of scale and splendour, building and building — until the last verse turns aside, almost in passing, to a second, separate feast: the queen's, held for the women, in the king's own house. What weighs on the passage is the sheer size and self-display of the power on show, and the one small seam the closing line opens up — that the king's world and the queen's are, for now, two feasts apart.

### 2.2 Context
This is where the book of Esther begins, in the reign of Ahasuerus (Xerxes), king of Persia — the great empire that ruled the Jewish people in their exile, generations after Babylon. No Israelite, no Jew, and no mention of God has entered yet; the reader is set down inside a foreign court at the height of its glory. The questions the whole book will turn on — power and who really holds it, the irreversible royal decree, a hidden people in danger, and a deliverance no one names as God's — are nowhere stated here, but the world they will play out in is being built: an empire run by law and display, where a king's word becomes the law of the Medes and Persians, and where even the drinking is regulated by decree.

### 2.3 Emotion / Tone / Pace
The tone is admiring on its surface and faintly ironic underneath — the narrator lays out the riches with a straight face and a slightly overflowing list, letting the sheer excess speak. The pace is unhurried and accumulating: long stretches of time (a hundred and eighty days, then seven more) and long inventories of cloth, stone, metal, and wine, piling detail on detail. Nothing is hurried because nothing yet is at stake; the passage luxuriates. Only the final verse changes the rhythm — short, set apart by "also," naming the queen and her separate feast — and with it a first small note of something held back enters the otherwise wall-to-wall splendour.

### 2.4 Communicative Function
The passage opens the book by building its world: the scale of the empire, the absoluteness and theatricality of the king's power, and the centrality of the banquet — the table where, again and again in this story, fates will be decided. It establishes Ahasuerus as a king who rules by display and by law, and the court as the arena. And it plants, in its last line, the hinge the next pericope will swing on: Vashti the queen, and a women's feast held apart from the king's. To speak this passage is to set the stage — the empire, the capital, the throne, the wine, and the queen — for the collision that is about to come.

**3D — Times**

**3F — Communicative Purpose**
Shows the king's lavish public feast for the whole capital, where even the wine runs by royal law.

**Significant Absence**
For all the gold and wine, nothing is eaten, said, or done that the story will turn on — the scene is pure display. And no god is thanked, invoked, or named over any of it.

**Significant Absence**
