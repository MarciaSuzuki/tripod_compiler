# J05 — Jonah 4:1–11

## 1. Metadata
- **Pericope title:** The qiqayon and the last question
- **BCV:** Jonah 4:1–11
- **Genre Group:** NARRATIVE
- **Genre:** HISTORICAL_NARRATIVE
- **Register:** INFORMAL_CASUAL

*(Versification note: chapter 4 is numbered the same in English and Hebrew — no divergence in this pericope.)*

**Multi-level register tagging:**
The whole passage sits in INFORMAL_CASUAL. One moment-level shift falls inside the speech: Jonah's prayer in 4:2–3 ("I beg, YHWH — was this not my word…") lifts into RELIGIOUS_WORSHIP — it is prayer, addressed to YHWH by name, even though it is furious prayer; the register carries the address, not the mood. The exchanges that follow — YHWH's probing question (4:4), the question over the qiqayon and Jonah's retort (4:9), and YHWH's closing argument (4:10–11) — are left in the plain register: question and answer between two speakers, neither command nor liturgy.

## 2. Level 1 — Whole-Passage Movement

### 2.1 Prose Arc / Shape / Argument / Burden / Concern
The mercy that saved Nineveh lands on Jonah as a great evil, and he burns. His prayer finally says what the story has withheld since the flight: this is why I ran — I knew you were gracious and compassionate, slow to anger, abounding in hesed, relenting of evil. He asks to die, gets a question instead — "is it good that it burns to you?" — and walks out east of the city to watch what will become of it. Then God's appointings come one a verse: a qiqayon plant to shade his head, and Jonah's one joy in the book; a worm at dawn that kills the plant; a cutting east wind and a hammering sun, until he faints and asks again to die. The question returns, sharpened to the plant — and Jonah answers it: yes, angry enough to die. The book ends with YHWH's argument from the lesser to the greater: you pitied a plant you never grew, a child of a night — and I, should I not pity Nineveh, a city of more than a hundred and twenty thousand who cannot tell their right hand from their left, and many animals? No one answers. The book ends inside the question.

### 2.2 Context
This is the payoff of everything held back: the missing reason for the flight (J01's silence) is supplied at last by Jonah himself, as an accusation — his creed is Israel's oldest description of YHWH (gracious, compassionate, slow to anger, abounding in hesed), and he hurls it as a complaint. The qiqayon is a fast-growing plant, never further identified; what matters is that it is given, loved, and lost in a day. A booth is the shelter a watcher builds himself — Jonah's own shade, against which God's shade is set. "Appointed" (vayman) is the book's quiet signature: God appointed the great fish, and now the plant, the worm, and the wind — the whole creation runs errands in this story. To "pity" (chus) is to let the eye spare a thing; the book's last argument hangs on who deserves it more, a plant or a city.

### 2.3 Emotion / Tone / Pace
The chapter runs on heat. Jonah burns (the narrator's word for his anger), the sun blazes, the east wind cuts dry — and against all of it, one verse of shade and the book's only joy, "a great joy," over a plant. The dialogue is spare and edged: two questions from God built on the same words, one defiant answer, and a closing argument that is gentle, vast, and unanswered. The pace alternates stillness and strike — Jonah sitting, watching, sheltered; then plant, worm, wind, sun in quick blows. The ending is the stillest moment in the book: a question hanging over a silent prophet and a spared city, with the animals — of all things — given the last word.

### 2.4 Communicative Function
The passage turns the book from a story about Nineveh into a question about Jonah — and about everyone who hears it. It completes the confession the flight withheld, sets the prophet's pity for a plant against God's pity for a city, and refuses to close: the last word is a question YHWH asks and no one answers, so the hearer is left holding it. It binds the book's threads — the relenting Jonah feared (J04) becomes the creed he quotes; the appointed fish finds its mates in plant, worm, and wind; the animals of the decree stand inside the final mercy. To speak this passage is to keep the heat, the smallness of the joy, the symmetry of the two questions, and above all the open end — the question must land on the hearer and stay unanswered.

## 3. Level 2 — Scenes / Episodes

### Scene 1 — The anger and the reason for the flight (4:1–3)

**3A — Beings**
[[B1-Jonah]] — יוֹנָה / Jonah
- Role: the one to whom the mercy is a great evil; the angry pray-er; the one who asks to die
- Relationship: the prophet whose preaching worked — and who cannot bear that it did
- Presence: PRESENT

[[B2-YHWH]] — יְהוָה / YHWH
- Role: the one prayed at — accused with his own creed
- Relationship: the God whose relenting Jonah fled
- Presence: PRESENT
- Referential form: addressed by name, "I beg, YHWH"

**3B — Places**
[[PL2-Tarshish]] — תַּרְשִׁישׁ / Tarshish
- Role: the flight's destination, named again in the confession
- Type: the far place of J01
- Effect on scene: the old run is finally explained — "that is why I fled toward Tarshish"

**3C — Objects and Concepts**

[[CB_0057-God-Relents]] — נִחָם עַל־הָרָעָה / relenting of the evil
- What it is: God turning back from a course he had set
- Function in scene: the last line of Jonah's creed — the very thing he says he fled, now done for Nineveh

[[CB_0011-Hesed]] — חֶסֶד / hesed
- What it is: hesed — loyal, covenant kindness
- Function in scene: inside the creed — the God Jonah quotes is "abounding in hesed," and that abundance is his complaint

[[CB_0058-Compassion-Creed]] — אֵל־חַנּוּן וְרַחוּם / a God gracious and compassionate
- What it is: the old confession of who YHWH is — gracious, compassionate, slow to anger, abounding in hesed, relenting of evil
- Function in scene: the creed Jonah quotes as his accusation — the reason he ran, in Israel's oldest words of praise

**3D — Times**
- None: no time-setting; the anger follows the relenting directly.

**3E — What Happens**
The relenting is a great evil to [[B1-Jonah]] Jonah, and he burns with anger. He prays to [[B2-YHWH]] YHWH: I beg, YHWH — was this not my word while I was still on my own soil? That is why I fled toward [[PL2-Tarshish]] Tarshish before: for I knew that you are a God gracious and compassionate, slow to anger, abounding in hesed, and relenting of evil. And now, YHWH, take my life from me — for my death is better than my life.

**3F — Communicative Purpose**
Supplies the reason the whole book withheld: Jonah ran because he knew the creed — and the scene turns Israel's oldest praise into a prophet's accusation. It sets the question the rest of the chapter answers with a plant.

**Significant Absence**
The missing motive of J01 is given here — and the narrator lets Jonah's own mouth give it, with no comment, no rebuke, and no defense of him. What exactly Jonah feared — Nineveh spared as such, or his word undone, or mercy reaching Israel's enemy — is left folded inside the creed; the text never unpacks it.

### Scene 2 — The question, and the watcher east of the city (4:4–5)

**3A — Beings**
[[B2-YHWH]] — יְהוָה / YHWH
- Role: the questioner — "is it good that it burns to you?"
- Relationship: answering a death-wish with a question
- Presence: PRESENT

[[B1-Jonah]] — יוֹנָה / Jonah
- Role: the unanswering one — he walks out and sits down to watch
- Relationship: the prophet waiting on the city's fate as if it were still open
- Presence: PRESENT

**3B — Places**
[[PL1-Nineveh]] — הָעִיר / the city
- Role: what Jonah leaves and watches
- Type: the great city, spared
- Effect on scene: he sits east of it, eyes on it — the spared city is the thing he cannot look away from

**3C — Objects and Concepts**

[[O13-Booth]] — סֻכָּה / a booth
- What it is: the watcher's shelter Jonah builds himself
- Function in scene: his own shade — the self-made comfort against which God's plant is about to be set

**3D — Times**
- None: no distinct time-setting; the watch is open-ended — "until he should see what would happen in the city."

**3E — What Happens**
[[B2-YHWH]] YHWH says: is it good that it burns to you? [[B1-Jonah]] Jonah does not answer. He goes out of the [[PL1-Nineveh]] city and sits down east of it; there he makes himself a [[O13-Booth]] booth and sits under it in the shade, until he should see what becomes of the city.

**3F — Communicative Purpose**
Sets the question of the chapter and lets it hang: Jonah answers with his feet, not his mouth. The watcher's post east of the city stages the lesson — a man, his anger, his own little shade, and the city below.

**Significant Absence**
Jonah gives no answer to the question — none at all. And the narrator never explains why he still watches the city after God has already relented — whether he hopes the doom will yet fall is left entirely unsaid.

### Scene 3 — Appointed: the plant, the worm, the wind (4:6–8)

**3A — Beings**
[[B2-YHWH]] — יְהוָה־אֱלֹהִים / YHWH-God
- Role: the appointer — plant, then worm, then wind
- Relationship: the same hand that appointed the fish
- Presence: PRESENT
- Referential form: the doubled name "YHWH-God" at the plant (4:6) — the hinge between the names; then "God" (Elohim) alone for the worm and the wind

[[B1-Jonah]] — יוֹנָה / Jonah
- Role: the shaded one, the rejoicing one, then the struck and fainting one who asks to die
- Relationship: pitying his own comfort more than the city
- Presence: PRESENT

[[B13-Worm]] — תּוֹלַעַת / a worm
- Role: the appointed striker — it kills the qiqayon at dawn
- Relationship: God's smallest creature on God's errand, like the fish before it
- Presence: PRESENT

**3B — Places**

None: no place is named in this scene; it stays at the watcher's post.

**3C — Objects and Concepts**

[[O11-Qiqayon]] — קִיקָיוֹן / the qiqayon plant
- What it is: a fast-growing plant, child of a night
- Function in scene: God's shade over Jonah's head, "to deliver him from his evil" — given in a verse, loved greatly, killed at dawn

[[O12-East-Wind]] — רוּחַ קָדִים חֲרִישִׁית / a cutting east wind
- What it is: the dry scorching wind from the east
- Function in scene: the third appointing — it strips the comfort so the sun can strike

**3D — Times**
- None as the scene's frame: "at the rising of dawn the next day" and "when the sun rose" sequence the strikes inside the scene rather than setting it.

**3E — What Happens**
[[B2-YHWH]] YHWH-God appoints a [[O11-Qiqayon]] qiqayon plant, and it rises up over [[B1-Jonah]] Jonah to be shade over his head, to deliver him from his evil — and Jonah rejoices over the qiqayon, a great joy. Then God appoints a [[B13-Worm]] worm at the rising of dawn the next day, and it strikes the qiqayon, and it withers. And when the sun rises, God appoints a cutting [[O12-East-Wind]] east wind; the sun strikes Jonah's head, and he faints. He asks for his life to die: my death is better than my life.

**3F — Communicative Purpose**
Builds the lesson out of appointings: the comfort given and loved, then taken blow by blow — plant, worm, wind, sun — until the prophet's death-wish returns over a plant. The book's one "great joy" stands here, and it is for shade.

**Significant Absence**
Jonah's joy is for the qiqayon — the spared city stirs nothing in him; the narrator sets the great joy beside the great evil of 4:1 without a word of comment. Why the plant is killed is never explained inside the scene — the meaning waits for YHWH's argument.

### Scene 4 — "Is it good?" — and the question that ends the book (4:9–11)

**3A — Beings**
[[B2-YHWH]] — אֱלֹהִים / God
- Role: the questioner again, then the arguer — from the plant to the city
- Relationship: pressing the prophet's pity toward his own
- Presence: PRESENT
- Referential form: "God" (Elohim) asks over the qiqayon (4:9); "YHWH" speaks the closing argument (4:10–11)

[[B1-Jonah]] — יוֹנָה / Jonah
- Role: the defiant answerer — "angry enough to die" — then the silent one
- Relationship: the man whose own pity convicts him
- Presence: PRESENT

[[B5-People-of-Nineveh]] — אָדָם / the people in her
- Role: the argument's weight — more than a hundred and twenty thousand who cannot tell right hand from left
- Relationship: the spared, described only by their number and their unknowing
- Presence: REFERENCED

[[B12-Animals]] — בְּהֵמָה רַבָּה / many animals
- Role: the book's last word — the animals stand inside the final mercy
- Relationship: the same beasts the decree once dressed in sackcloth
- Presence: REFERENCED

**3B — Places**
[[PL1-Nineveh]] — נִינְוֵה / Nineveh
- Role: the city of the closing question — "should I not pity Nineveh, the great city?"
- Type: the great city, spared
- Effect on scene: the whole argument lands on her — the plant against the city

**3C — Objects and Concepts**

[[O11-Qiqayon]] — הַקִּיקָיוֹן / the qiqayon
- What it is: the dead plant, now the argument's small term
- Function in scene: what Jonah pitied — no labor, no growing, a child of a night that perished in a night

**3D — Times**
- None: no time-setting; the argument follows the fainting directly.

**3E — What Happens**
[[B2-YHWH]] God says to [[B1-Jonah]] Jonah: is it good that it burns to you — over the qiqayon? He answers: it is good that it burns to me, even to death. And YHWH says: you — you pitied the qiqayon, which you did not labor over and did not grow, which was a child of a night and perished a child of a night. And I — should I not pity [[PL1-Nineveh]] Nineveh, the great city, in which there are more than a hundred and twenty thousand people who do not know their right hand from their left — and many animals?

**3F — Communicative Purpose**
Closes the book by refusing to close it: the two pities are set side by side — a plant against a city — and the question is left standing in the air. The hearer, not Jonah, is the one who must answer it.

**Significant Absence**
The book ends on an unanswered question — Jonah says nothing, the narrator adds nothing, and no verdict is passed on the prophet. What became of Jonah, and of Nineveh after, is never told. The last word of the book belongs to the animals, and the text leaves it there.
