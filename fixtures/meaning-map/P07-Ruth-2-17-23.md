# P07 — Ruth 2:17-23

## 1. Metadata
- **Pericope title:** The gleaning brought home and the redeemer named
- **BCV:** Ruth 2:17-23
- **Genre Group:** NARRATIVE
- **Genre:** HISTORICAL_NARRATIVE
- **Register:** INFORMAL_CASUAL

*(Versification note: Ruth's chapter and verse numbers are the same in English and Hebrew — no divergence in this pericope.)*

**Multi-level register tagging:**
The whole passage sits in INFORMAL_CASUAL. Scene 3 (vv. 19-22) shifts to INTIMATE: a mother-in-law and her daughter-in-law alone at the end of the day, the day's surprise spilling out — quick questions, the report, the naming of the man, and counsel. One moment inside that talk lifts to CEREMONIAL: Naomi's blessing at v.20 takes a set, weighty shape and invokes YHWH by name. The narrator's framing at v.17, v.18, and v.23 stays in the plain, everyday telling.

## 2. Level 1 — Whole-Passage Movement

### 2.1 Prose Arc / Shape / Argument / Burden / Concern
The passage moves from the field to home, and then out across a whole season. Ruth gleans until evening, beats out what she gathered, and it comes to about an ephah of barley — a heavy load for one day. She carries it into town, her mother-in-law sees how much there is, and Ruth hands her the food left over from her own meal. Naomi asks where she gleaned and blesses the man who took notice of her — not yet knowing his name. When Ruth names him — Boaz — Naomi blesses him by YHWH, whose hesed (loyal, covenant kindness) has not let go of the living or the dead, and tells Ruth the man is near to them, one of their redeemers (a near relative who can step in for a dead man's family, land, and name). Ruth adds what Boaz said about staying close to his workers; Naomi counsels her to go out with his young women. Ruth does, gleaning to the end of the barley and wheat harvests, and stays living with her mother-in-law. What weighs on the passage is the turn from food to hope: a day's gleaning becomes the first sign that the family's rescue may have a name. The question it leaves hanging is what will come of this kinship now that Naomi has seen it.

### 2.2 Context
This follows straight on the day in Boaz's field: his welcome, his blessing, the shared meal, and his order for generous gleaning. The audience has known since 2:1 what Ruth and Naomi have not yet put together — that Boaz is a kinsman of the dead Elimelech; here Naomi makes the connection at the sound of his name. The hesed word last fell at 1:8, in Naomi's blessing-wish that YHWH show the daughters-in-law hesed as they had shown the dead and her; at 2:20 that wish comes back with both halves — hesed, the living, the dead — now spoken over a named man. The redeemer-role is named here for the first time in the book. One small ripple in the reports: Boaz's word at 2:8 was "stay close to my young women"; Ruth's report at 2:21 says "the young men who are mine"; Naomi's counsel at 2:22 returns to "his young women." The narrator lets the difference stand without comment.

### 2.3 Emotion / Tone / Pace
The tone moves from steady work to quickened feeling and back to a long, calm close. The field line at v.17 is plain and busy — gleaning, beating out, measuring. The pace lifts at home: Naomi sees the heap, fires two short questions, and breaks into blessing before any name is known. The high point is v.20, where the second blessing reaches past the man to YHWH's unbroken hesed and gathers in the living and the dead. Then it settles: Naomi's counsel is calm and practical, and v.23 stretches weeks of harvest into one quiet line. The mood at the end is settled hope — the household no longer empty-handed, a kinsman now in view.

### 2.4 Communicative Function
This passage closes the chapter's provision strand: a hungry household now holds an ephah of barley and weeks of gleaning ahead. It opens the redeemer thread the rest of the book turns on, by letting Naomi name the role at the sound of Boaz's name. It brings the hesed word back — last heard as Naomi's wish at 1:8, now her blessing at 2:20 — and stretches it over the living and the dead, so the dead husband and sons stay inside the family's accounting. It names Ruth "the Moabite" again at the very moment her place in the household grows. It closes Ruth's clinging — the word of her first holding-fast to Naomi at 1:14 — by her staying close to Boaz's young women through both harvests. And its quiet last line, Ruth at home with Naomi as the season ends, is the floor the next chapter's plan is built on.

- What it is: the kinsman-redeemer — a near relative bound to step in for a dead clansman's family, land, and name

**3E — What Happens**
[[B3-Naomi]] Naomi asks [[B9-Ruth]] Ruth where she gleaned today and where she worked, and blesses the man who took notice of her. Naomi blesses him by [[B10-YHWH]] YHWH, whose hesed has not let go of the living or the dead, and tells Ruth: the man is near to us — one of our redeemers. **3F — Communicative Purpose**
The turn of the passage: at the name "Boaz," Naomi recognizes a redeemer, and the day's food becomes the first sign of rescue — sealed in a blessing that holds the living and the dead together under unbroken hesed.

**Significant Absence**
Naomi does not explain to Ruth what a redeemer is or what the nearness could mean for them; she names the role and stops, and the narrator does not say what Ruth understands by it. The dead are named only as "the dead," not as Elimelech, Mahlon, and Chilion. The blessing never says whose hesed has not been forsaken — YHWH's or the man's; the words leave it open.
