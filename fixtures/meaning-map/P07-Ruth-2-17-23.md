# P07 — Ruth 2:17-23

## 1. Metadata
- **Pericope title:** Ruth brings home the gleaning; Naomi recognizes Boaz as a redeemer; Ruth stays through the harvest
- **BCV:** Ruth 2:17-23
- **Genre Group:** NARRATIVE
- **Genre:** HISTORICAL_NARRATIVE
- **Register:** INFORMAL_CASUAL

**Multi-level register tagging:**
The whole passage sits in INFORMAL_CASUAL, the plain default for the biblical narrator's voice. The talk between the two women back home (vv. 19-22) shifts to INTIMATE: a mother-in-law and her daughter-in-law alone at the end of the day, with the day's surprise spilling out — Naomi's quick questions, Ruth's report, the naming of the man. One line inside that talk lifts higher, to CEREMONIAL: Naomi's blessing on Boaz at v.20 ("Blessed be he of the LORD") takes a formal, weighty shape and names YHWH. The narrator's own voice is never put in a consultative key; the framing at v.17, v.18a, and v.23 stays in the plain, everyday telling.

## 2. Level 1 — Whole-Passage Movement

### 2.1 Prose Arc / Shape / Argument / Burden / Concern
The passage moves from the field to home and then out to the long harvest. Ruth finishes her gleaning, beats out what she gathered, and it comes to a full ephah of barley — a heavy load for one day. She carries it into town, shows her mother-in-law how much she gathered, and hands her the food left over from her own meal. Naomi, struck by the amount, asks where she gleaned and blesses the man who took notice of her. When Ruth names him — Boaz — Naomi recognizes the name: he is a near kinsman, one of their redeemers (the kinsman-redeemer: a near relative who can step in to rescue a dead man's family, land, and name). She blesses the LORD, whose hesed (loyal, covenant kindness) has not let go of the living or the dead. Naomi tells Ruth to keep close to Boaz's young women, and Ruth does, gleaning with them to the end of the barley and the wheat harvests. What weighs on the passage is the turn from bare provision to hope: a day's food becomes the first sign that the family's rescue may have a name. The question it leaves hanging is what will come of this kinship now that Naomi has seen it.

### 2.2 Context
This follows straight on the day in Boaz's field: his welcome, his blessing, the shared meal, and his order to let Ruth glean freely. The audience already knows what Ruth and Naomi do not fully know — that Boaz is a kinsman of the dead Elimelech (told at 2:1), the very man who could redeem the line. Naomi's household is empty; the barley harvest is the season the women are living through. Here the two strands meet: Ruth comes home with proof of the day's favor, and at the name "Boaz" Naomi makes the connection the audience has been holding. The hesed word, kept back through the field scenes, is spoken here for the first time in chapter 2; the redeemer-role is named here for the first time in the book.

### 2.3 Emotion / Tone / Pace
The tone moves from steady work to quickened feeling and back to a long, calm close. The field lines at v.17 are plain and busy — gleaning, beating out, measuring. The pace lifts the moment Ruth gets home: Naomi sees the heap, then fires two short questions in a rush, then breaks into blessing. The high point is her recognition at v.20, where her words turn from the man to the LORD and reach back to name both the living and the dead. Then the passage settles: Naomi's counsel at v.22 is calm and practical, and v.23 stretches out over weeks of harvest in a single quiet summary. The mood by the end is settled hope — no longer empty, with a kinsman now in view.

### 2.4 Communicative Function
This passage closes the harvest-provision strand the chapter has carried: a hungry household now has an ephah of barley and weeks of gleaning ahead. It turns the day's favor into something larger by letting Naomi name Boaz as a redeemer — the first time the rescuing role is spoken in the book. It brings back the hesed word, now on Naomi's lips, and stretches it over the living and the dead, so the dead husband and sons are not dropped from the family's accounting. It reasserts Ruth as the Moabite at the very moment she repeats Boaz's welcome, keeping her outsider standing in view as her place in the household grows. And it closes Ruth's clinging — the same word used when she first held fast to Naomi — by having her stay close to Boaz's young women through both harvests.

- What it is: the day Naomi asks about — "Where did you glean today?"

**3F — Communicative Purpose**

**Significant Absence**
Naomi does not explain to Ruth what a redeemer is or what it could mean for them; she names the role and stops. The narrator does not say what Ruth understands by it. The dead are named only as "the dead," not as Elimelech, Mahlon, and Chilion.
