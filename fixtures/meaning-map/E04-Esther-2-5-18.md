# E04 — Esther 2:5–18

## 1. Metadata
- **Pericope title:** Mordecai and Hadassah: the orphan cousin is taken into the harem, wins favor, hides her people, and is crowned queen
- **BCV:** Esther 2:5–18
- **Genre Group:** NARRATIVE
- **Genre:** HISTORICAL_NARRATIVE
- **Register:** INFORMAL_CASUAL

**Multi-level register tagging:**
The whole passage sits in INFORMAL_CASUAL — the plain reporting voice of a storyteller who introduces two new people, follows a young woman through a year of waiting, and arrives at a crowning. There is no decree voiced and no edict written out in this pericope, so no FORMAL_OFFICIAL override is taken anywhere. The closest the text comes is v.8's "word of the king and his law" and v.12's "the regulation for the women" — but in both cases the narrator is *reporting* that a rule existed and was heard, not quoting the rule's own words, so the register stays INFORMAL_CASUAL. This pericope is mid-book, not the opening or close, so no COMMUNITY_MEMORY framing lift is applied.

## 2. Level 1 — Whole-Passage Movement
### 2.1 Prose Arc / Shape / Argument / Burden / Concern
The passage narrows the camera from the empire-wide search of the previous scene down onto two people and then onto one. First it stops to introduce a man — Mordecai, a Jew of the capital, a Benjaminite whose family had been carried off from Jerusalem in the great exile — and then his orphaned younger cousin, the girl Hadassah, who is also called Esther, whom he had taken as his own daughter. When the king's order sweeps young women into the citadel, Esther is taken too, into the keeping of Hegai who holds the women. There she quietly wins favor: Hegai hurries her ointments and her portions and her seven maids to her, and moves her to the best place in the house. All the while she keeps one thing hidden — her people and her kindred — because Mordecai had charged her to, and Mordecai paces outside the women's court day after day to learn how she fares. The text then pauses to describe the slow machinery she is inside: a full year of beautifying before any girl goes in to the king, each going in at evening and returning at morning to a second house, never to return unless the king wants her by name. When Esther's own turn comes she asks for nothing beyond what Hegai advises, and she carries favor in every eye that sees her. She is taken in to the king in the tenth month, Tebeth, in his seventh year; the king loves her above all the women, sets the royal crown on her head, and makes her queen in Vashti's place — and throws a great banquet in her name. The arc moves from exile and orphanhood to crown, by a path of favor quietly won and an identity quietly kept.

### 2.2 Context
This continues the book of Esther, inside the Persian court of Ahasuerus, after the queen Vashti has been removed and the king's servants have begun gathering the empire's young women so the king may choose a new queen. Now the two people the rest of the book will turn on are named for the first time: Mordecai, whose Benjaminite line and exile from Jerusalem tie this Persian story back to the deported people of Judah, and Esther, the hidden Jewish girl who will become queen. The book's signature is already at work here: a Jewish identity that is deliberately concealed inside a foreign palace, on the order of the older relative who is watching from outside. Everything that the later crisis will require — a Jew near the throne, a queen whose people are unknown to the king — is being quietly put in place in this pericope, and, as everywhere in this book, without a single mention of God.

### 2.3 Emotion / Tone / Pace
The tone is restrained and watchful. The narrator reports favor and beauty plainly, without lingering, and there is a held-breath quality to the concealment — a secret kept on purpose, a guardian pacing outside a wall he cannot enter. The pace is uneven by design: it moves briskly through the introductions and the taking, then slows almost to a standstill for the long catalogue of the beautifying regimen — twelve months, six of myrrh-oil and six of perfumes — letting the reader feel the length of the waiting, before quickening again into the crowning and the feast. The overall feeling is of a quiet ascent: nothing forced, favor accumulating, an identity held in reserve.

### 2.4 Communicative Function
The passage introduces the book's two human protagonists and binds them together — guardian and ward, the watching Jew outside and the hidden Jewish queen inside. It establishes the concealment of Esther's people as a deliberate, charged act, not an accident, and shows it holding. It plants the love of the king for Esther and her elevation over Vashti as the turn that puts a Jew on the throne. To speak this passage is to set in place the two people, the kept secret, and the crown on which the whole deliverance will later hang.

### Proposition 1 — Esther 2:5 [Scene 1]
