# E05 — Esther 2:19–23

## 1. Metadata
- **Pericope title:** At the king's gate: the hidden kindred and the foiled assassination
- **BCV:** Esther 2:19–23
- **Genre Group:** NARRATIVE
- **Genre:** HISTORICAL_NARRATIVE
- **Register:** INFORMAL_CASUAL

**Multi-level register tagging:**
The whole passage sits in INFORMAL_CASUAL — the plain, quick voice of a storyteller reporting court business after the fact. There is no decree spoken or written-out here, so no FORMAL_OFFICIAL override is taken: even the closing note that the affair "was written in the book of the chronicles" is the narrator *describing* an entry, not quoting it, so the register stays INFORMAL_CASUAL throughout.

## 2. Level 1 — Whole-Passage Movement
### 2.1 Prose Arc / Shape / Argument / Burden / Concern
The passage moves in three quiet steps that the rest of the book will lean on. First it sets Mordecai down at the king's gate and reminds us, in a single held-back line, that Esther is still keeping her people and her kindred hidden, just as Mordecai told her to. Then, in those same days, two of the king's own gatekeepers — royal eunuchs — turn against him and plot to kill him; Mordecai learns of it, passes it to Esther, and Esther warns the king in Mordecai's name. Finally the matter is checked, found true, the two conspirators hanged on a tree, and the whole thing written down in the royal chronicles before the king. What weighs on the passage is the planting of two seeds that will not flower until much later: a loyal service the king does not yet repay, and a written record waiting in a book. The burden is hiddenness and delay — a kindred concealed, a good deed filed away and forgotten, until the day the book is read aloud.

### 2.2 Context
This sits early in the book of Esther, just after Esther has been made queen in the Persian court of Ahasuerus (Xerxes), and just before the rise of Haman, who will set the whole crisis in motion. Mordecai, a Jew of the exile, sits "in the king's gate" — the place where the king's business and justice were done, so this marks him as a man now attached to the court. Two things are laid down here that the story will reach back for: Esther's still-hidden identity, which Mordecai has charged her to keep, and Mordecai's unrewarded rescue of the king, recorded in the chronicles. Both will return at the hinge of the book — the night the king cannot sleep and has the chronicles read to him (the reversal this entry seeds). As everywhere in Esther, God is never named; the rescue, the timing, and the writing all happen in the plain machinery of the court.

### 2.3 Emotion / Tone / Pace
The tone is level and matter-of-fact, the narrator reporting court events without raising his voice — even an assassination plot and a double execution are told in a few tight words. The pace is brisk and clipped: a conspiracy, a discovery, a relay of warning, an investigation, a hanging, a record — six turns in five verses, each given barely a clause. Under the flat surface runs a deliberate withholding: the one line about Esther's hidden kindred is set down almost in passing, and Mordecai's loyalty is recorded but pointedly not rewarded. The feeling is of things being noted and shelved — the calm of a court that does its business and moves on, not yet knowing what it has filed away.

### 2.4 Communicative Function
The passage does two pieces of structural work for the book. It confirms that Esther's identity stays hidden under Mordecai's instruction — keeping the secret loaded for the moment she will choose to reveal it. And it plants the unrewarded-loyalty thread: Mordecai saves the king's life, the deed goes into the chronicles, and the king does nothing — a debt left open on purpose. To speak this passage is to lay two charges that the later story will set off: the queen who is not yet known for who she is, and the record in the book that the king will one day have read back to him. It is groundwork that looks like a passing aside.

**3E — What Happens**
At the time when the young women were gathered a second time, [[B1-Mordecai]] Mordecai was sitting in the king's gate. [[B2-Esther]] Esther had still not made known her kindred or her people, just as [[B1-Mordecai]] Mordecai had charged her — for Esther kept doing what Mordecai said, the same as when she was under his care.

**3F — Communicative Purpose**
Places Mordecai at the court's threshold and confirms that Esther's identity stays hidden under his standing instruction — keeping the secret loaded for later.

**Significant Absence**
No God is named over Esther's concealment, and no reason for it is restated — the narrator only notes that the secret still holds. The "second gathering" is mentioned but never explained. And nothing yet says why a hidden kindred should matter; the verse withholds its own stakes.

- Function: binds Scene 2 to Scene 1's setting

**3E — What Happens**
In those days, while [[B1-Mordecai]] Mordecai was sitting in the king's gate, two of the king's eunuchs, [[B11-Bigthana]] Bigthana and [[B12-Teresh]] Teresh — keepers of the threshold — grew angry and plotted to lay hands on King [[B4-Ahasuerus]] Ahasuerus. The matter became known to [[B1-Mordecai]] Mordecai, and he told it to [[B2-Esther]] Esther the queen, and Esther told the king, in Mordecai's name.

**3F — Communicative Purpose**
Shows the assassination plot uncovered by Mordecai and relayed through Esther to the king, with Mordecai named as the source — planting the unrewarded-loyalty thread.

**Significant Absence**
No motive is given for the eunuchs' anger — they "grew angry," and the cause is left blank. No God intervenes; the rescue runs entirely through court channels — an overheard word, a queen's message. And the king's reaction is withheld: he is warned, but nothing is said of any thanks or reward, an omission the story will later exploit.

- What it is: the royal annals — the running record of court affairs
- Function in scene: where Mordecai's deed is written down before the king
- Signals: a deed filed and forgotten — the record that will be read out at the book's turning-point (seeds the E11 reversal)

The plot, investigated and confirmed — הַדָּבָר … וַיִּמָּצֵא / the matter … and it was found (true) *(scene content — the inquiry's result; not a registry object)*
- What it is: the investigation that checks the report and finds it true
- Function in scene: the verification that justifies the execution and the record
- Signals: court procedure run to its end — checked, confirmed, acted on, written

**3D — Times**
- None: no separate time-setting; the investigation, hanging, and record run on directly from the warning in Scene 2.

**3E — What Happens**
The matter was investigated and found to be true, and the two of them were hanged on a tree. And it was written in the book of the chronicles, before the king.

**3F — Communicative Purpose**
Closes the affair — the plot confirmed, the conspirators executed, the deed entered in the royal chronicles before the king — leaving Mordecai's loyalty recorded but unrewarded.

**Significant Absence**
Mordecai's reward is conspicuously absent — the deed is written down, but nothing is given to him; the omission is the point, held back for the reversal to come. No God is named in the rescue or the justice. And the entry is recorded plainly, with no hint that anyone will ever look at it again.
