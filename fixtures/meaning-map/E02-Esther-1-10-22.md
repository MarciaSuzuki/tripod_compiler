# E02 — Esther 1:10–22

## 1. Metadata
- **Pericope title:** The queen's refusal and the empire's answer: Vashti deposed by decree
- **BCV:** Esther 1:10–22
- **Genre Group:** NARRATIVE
- **Genre:** HISTORICAL_NARRATIVE
- **Register:** INFORMAL_CASUAL

**Multi-level register tagging:**
The narrator's voice stays INFORMAL_CASUAL throughout — the plain, slightly amused storytelling that carried the banquets of the opening pericope: a king merry with wine, a queen who simply will not come, a panicked counsellor. One stretch lifts out of that voice: in Scene 3 (v.16–20) Memucan, of the king's seven inner princes, stops giving advice and begins to *voice the decree itself* — "Let a royal word go out, and let it be written in the laws of Persia and Media, that it not pass away… that Vashti come no more before the king." That is an edict being spoken aloud in its own official cadence, so a **FORMAL_OFFICIAL** register override applies at the moment-level across v.19–20 (the voiced edict), nested inside Memucan's otherwise persuasive speech. The frame around it — the king's question to his wise men, the sending of letters in v.22 — is the narrator reporting from the outside and stays INFORMAL_CASUAL.

## 2. Level 1 — Whole-Passage Movement
### 2.1 Prose Arc / Shape / Argument / Burden / Concern
The passage turns the splendour of the opening feast into a crisis in a single stroke. On the seventh day, with the king's heart "good with wine," he sends his seven personal eunuchs to bring the queen out before the gathered men, crowned, so that her beauty can be put on display like the gold and the wine before it. She refuses. With that one refusal — given without a stated reason — the whole machinery of the empire swings into motion: the king's pleasure becomes rage, the rage becomes a question of law put to his wise men, and the wise man Memucan answers by lifting the private slight into an empire-wide emergency — if the queen may defy the king and go unpunished, every wife in 127 provinces will despise her husband. The remedy he proposes is itself a decree: depose Vashti, give her place to another, and publish it everywhere so that "every man rule in his own house." The king is pleased, and letters go out to every province in its own script and every people in its own tongue. The burden of the passage is how fast and how far a single woman's "no" is made to travel — and how the empire answers a private refusal with a public, irreversible law.

### 2.2 Context
This continues directly from the opening banquets (E01): the same seven-day feast, the same capital, the same king now drunk on the seventh day. Vashti, named in the last line of the previous pericope as holding her own feast for the women, is now summoned from it. The scene introduces the book's recurring engine — the unchangeable law of the Medes and Persians, which a later pericope will turn against the Jews and which cannot, by its own nature, be revoked. It also introduces, in passing, the machinery the whole plot will run on: a king who decides by the counsel of those nearest him, a class of "wise men who know the times," and letters carried to every province in every language. The deposing of Vashti clears the throne that Esther will be raised to — though neither Esther nor any Jew, and no word of God, has yet appeared.

### 2.3 Emotion / Tone / Pace
The tone shifts hard from the leisurely opulence of the feast to something tense and faintly absurd. The king's mood is named twice and runs from pleasure ("good with wine") to fury ("the king grew very angry, and his rage burned in him") in two verses. Then the pace slows and inflates: a private flash of anger is met with solemn deliberation — the wise men, the laws and judgments, the long courtroom-style speech — out of all proportion to its cause. The narrator lets the disproportion show: an emperor and his seven highest princes convened in council because a queen would not come when called. There is dread under the comedy, though — the same disproportion, the same irreversible law, will later threaten a whole people. The pace closes fast and wide: the decree written, approved, and dispatched to the ends of the empire in a single sweep.

### 2.4 Communicative Function
The passage moves the book from stage-setting into its first conflict and plants the engine of the entire plot. It shows power as something that cannot absorb a refusal without converting it into law — and shows that the conversion is done by counsel, by precedent-fear, and by writing. It establishes the irrevocable Persian decree, the council of the king's nearest men, and the empire-wide letter as the instruments by which everything later (Haman's edict, Mordecai's counter-edict) will be done. And functionally it empties the throne: by the end, there is a vacancy where the queen was, and a published law that she shall never return — the gap the next pericope will fill with the search for a new queen.

- What it is: the royal command, carried by the eunuchs, that the queen would not obey
- Function in scene: the thing refused — "she did not do the word of King Ahasuerus that was by the hand of the eunuchs"
- Signals: that what was disobeyed was not a husband's wish but a *royal command* — which is what makes it a matter of law

**3D — Times**
- None: the rage and the consultation follow immediately on the refusal; no separate time-setting.

**3E — What Happens**
But [[B5-Vashti]] Queen Vashti refused to come at the king's word that was brought by the eunuchs; and [[B4-Ahasuerus]] the king grew very angry, and his rage burned in him. Then the king turned to the wise men who knew the times — for so was the king's manner, to lay matters before all who knew law and judgment, the nearest to him — and asked them, according to law, what should be done with Queen Vashti, because she had not obeyed the command of King Ahasuerus carried by the eunuchs.

**3F — Communicative Purpose**
Shows the refusal, the king's instant fury, and his choice to convert a private slight into a question of law before his counsellors — opening the door for the decree.

**Significant Absence**
Vashti gives no reason for her refusal — the narrator records the "no" and not a word of why, leaving the gap that has occupied readers ever since. God is again unnamed; the king appeals not to any divine law but to Persian law and precedent. And the queen, having spoken her one act of refusal, is now spoken about only in the third person — judged in her absence, never summoned to answer.

And when the king's decree is heard throughout all his realm — vast as it is — all the wives will give honour to their husbands, from the greatest to the least."

**3F — Communicative Purpose**
Shows the king's nearest counsellor magnifying a private refusal into an empire-wide threat and answering it with a voiced, irrevocable decree — the engine of the whole book set running.

**Significant Absence**
No one speaks for Vashti; no defence, no inquiry, no hearing — judgment is passed on her in her absence and she is never named again after the decree. God is not invoked to sanction the law. And there is no proportion: an empire's machinery of irrevocable law is turned on a single act of a single woman, with no voice raised against the scale of the response.
