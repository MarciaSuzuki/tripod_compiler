# J04 — Jonah 2:10–3:10

## 1. Metadata
- **Pericope title:** Nineveh believes, and God relents
- **BCV:** Jonah 2:10–3:10
- **Genre Group:** NARRATIVE
- **Genre:** HISTORICAL_NARRATIVE
- **Register:** INFORMAL_CASUAL

*(Versification note: English 2:10 is Hebrew 2:11; chapter 3 is numbered the same in both. The verse references below are English.)*

**Multi-level register tagging:**
The whole passage sits in INFORMAL_CASUAL, the plain default for the biblical narrator's voice. Three moment-level shifts fall inside the speech: YHWH's renewed charge in 3:2 ("get up, go to Nineveh, call out") lifts into ELDER_AUTHORITY — the same commanding voice as 1:2, giving the same command. Jonah's oracle in 3:4 ("forty more days and Nineveh is overthrown") lifts into FORMAL_OFFICIAL — a herald's public proclamation to a city. The king's decree in 3:7–9 is FORMAL_OFFICIAL as well — a royal edict issued in Nineveh by order of the king and his nobles, running through to his "who knows?" hope. The narrator's own voice stays INFORMAL_CASUAL throughout; CONSULTATIVE is not used.

## 2. Level 1 — Whole-Passage Movement

### 2.1 Prose Arc / Shape / Argument / Burden / Concern
The fish gives Jonah back to the dry land at a word, and the word of YHWH comes a second time — the same charge, as if the flight had never happened. This time Jonah gets up and goes. Into the vast city he carries five words: forty more days and Nineveh is overthrown. What follows is the largest repentance in the book told at the fastest speed: the Ninevites believe God, fast, and put on sackcloth from the greatest to the least; the news reaches the king and he comes down from throne to ashes; his decree pushes the fast out to the herds and flocks and calls every man to turn from his evil way — on the bare hope, "who knows? God may relent." And God sees what they did, and relents of the evil he had spoken, and does not do it. The burden of the passage is that one word, turning: the city turns from its evil, and God turns from his.

### 2.2 Context
This resumes the errand of J01 from the beach where the fish left Jonah: the word comes "a second time," the same get-up-go-and-call, with no mention of the running in between. Nineveh is the great city of J01's charge — so large the narrator gives it three days just to walk. Sackcloth and ashes are the dress of grief and repentance; a fast called for beasts as well as people is repentance pushed past all custom — even the herds wear mourning. "Overthrown" is the word used of Sodom, and it cuts two ways: a city can be overthrown by destruction, or turned over into something new — the oracle does not say which. To "relent" (nicham) is to turn back from an intended course; the king stakes the city on the chance that God does it, and God does.

### 2.3 Emotion / Tone / Pace
The pace is headlong and the wonder rides on how little resistance there is. The recall takes two verses; the obedience, one; the sermon, five words — and then the city falls over itself: believe, fast, sackcloth, in a single verse, before the king has even heard. The king's response is a staircase down — off the throne, robe off, sackcloth on, into the ashes — and his decree climbs the other way, out over man and beast, herd and flock. Under it all runs a quiet astonishment the narrator never names: the pagans of J02 prayed and were spared, and now the pagan city of the oracle repents at five words from a runaway prophet. The close is still and large: God saw, God relented, and the disaster did not happen.

### 2.4 Communicative Function
The passage answers J01's broken errand with the errand completed, and answers Nineveh's evil — named since 1:2 — with Nineveh's turning. It hands the book its theology in one verse: the God whose word threatens a city is the same God who relents when the city turns; salvation, as the psalm said, belongs to YHWH. It also lays the fuse for J05: everything here that looks like a happy ending — the belief, the ashes, the relenting — is exactly what Jonah will be angry about, and the narrator holds the prophet's silence shut until then. To speak this passage is to keep the speed of the city's turning, the smallness of the sermon against the size of the response, and the last verse's weight: God saw, God relented, God did not do it.

**3E — What Happens**

**3F — Communicative Purpose**

**Significant Absence**
The king is never named, and no word of his is spoken to Jonah — prophet and king never meet. The decree commands turning from "his evil way," yet no one in the story ever says what Nineveh's evil was. The hope is bare — "who knows?" — with no promise to stand on; the king decrees repentance without any assurance it will work.

- What it is: the wrongdoing of Nineveh — the same evil that rose before YHWH in J01

[[CB_0057-God-Relents]] — וַיִּנָּחֶם / and he relented
- What it is: God turning back from a course he had set
- Function in scene: the hope of the decree happening — God relents of the evil and does not do it

**3D — Times**
- None: no time-setting; whether the forty days ran out is never said.

**3E — What Happens**
[[B9-God]] God sees their deeds — that they turn from their evil way. And God relents of the evil he had said he would do to them, and he does not do it.

**3F — Communicative Purpose**
Lands the book's theology in one verse, on one repeated word: they turn from their evil, and God turns from his. The oracle's "overthrown" comes true the second way — the city is turned over, not torn down.

**Significant Absence**
God says nothing — the relenting is reported, not spoken; God's last words in this pericope were the charge of 3:2. Whether the forty days passed is never told. And Jonah is wholly absent from the ending — what the prophet thought of the mercy he announced is the held breath the next pericope opens on.
