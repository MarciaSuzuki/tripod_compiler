# E15 — Esther 9:1–10

## 1. Metadata
- **Pericope title:** The day turned over: the Jews stand, strike, and take no plunder
- **BCV:** Esther 9:1–10
- **Genre Group:** NARRATIVE
- **Genre:** HISTORICAL_NARRATIVE
- **Register:** INFORMAL_CASUAL

**Multi-level register tagging:**
The whole passage sits in INFORMAL_CASUAL — the storyteller's plain voice, reporting the long-feared day and how it came out. There is no decree voiced here (the edict that set this day was written out earlier, in E14), so no FORMAL_OFFICIAL override applies: the narrator only *refers* to "the king's word and his decree" (דְּבַר הַמֶּלֶךְ וְדָתֹו) as the appointed thing now coming due, rather than quoting it. The one verbal lift is the narrator's own comment at v.1 — "and it was turned over" (נַהֲפֹוךְ הוּא) — a flat, almost stunned aside marking the great reversal; it stays inside the casual register. *(Meaning call for Marcia: confirm no FORMAL_OFFICIAL moment-override — the decree is named, not spoken, so I have kept the whole pericope INFORMAL_CASUAL.)*

## 2. Level 1 — Whole-Passage Movement
### 2.1 Prose Arc / Shape / Argument / Burden / Concern
The passage turns on a single hinge-word: *reversed*. It opens on a date the reader has been waiting for since the edict was issued — the thirteenth of Adar, the day the enemies of the Jews had counted on to overpower them — and then, in one breath, flips it: on the very day meant for their ruin, it was the Jews who held the upper hand. From there the movement is all consolidation and outcome: the Jews gather in their cities across the empire, and no one can stand against them, because a dread of them has fallen on every people; the empire's own officials prop them up, out of fear of Mordecai, who is growing greater and greater. Then the striking itself — the enemies cut down "as they pleased" — and a close-up on the capital: five hundred men killed in Susa the citadel, and the ten sons of Haman named one by one. The burden of the passage is the overturning of a doom into a deliverance; and its final, pointed note is a restraint — on the plunder they did not lay a hand.

### 2.2 Context
This is the payoff of the book's central machinery. Earlier, Haman cast lots and bought a decree to destroy every Jew in the empire on the thirteenth of Adar; that decree, by the law of the Medes and Persians, could not be revoked, so a second decree was issued instead — letting the Jews assemble and defend themselves on that same day. This pericope is that day arriving. Everything turns on the irreversibility the book has been building: the original death-date cannot be cancelled, so it must be *survived* and turned inside out. The reader already knows Mordecai has risen to power in the king's house (he wears royal robes, the signet is his); here that rise becomes the very reason the empire's machinery now leans the Jews' way. The hidden hand the book never names is felt all through the "reversal," but no deliverer is credited and no prayer is offered.

### 2.3 Emotion / Tone / Pace
The tone is taut and almost ledger-like — a reporter recording an outcome, not savoring it. The pace is fast at the open (the date, the reversal, the assembling, the dread, all stacked quickly) and then slows for two deliberate close-ups: the body-count in the capital, and the roll of ten names. The naming is the passage's one ceremonial slowing — ten sons of the great enemy listed in full, each one a stone laid on Haman's line. Under the restraint there is grim relief and a kind of vindication, but the narrator keeps it cold; the loudest emotional statement is made by an *omission* — three times in this stretch and the next, the text insists the Jews took no plunder, refusing the spoil that the decree had expressly allowed them.

### 2.4 Communicative Function
The passage delivers the great reversal the whole book has been driving toward, and it does the bookkeeping of that reversal: it fixes the date, states the overturning plainly, shows the empire itself now siding with the Jews out of fear of Mordecai, tallies the dead in the capital, and seals the fall of Haman's house by naming his ten sons. It also plants, by threefold refusal, the moral signature of the Jews' self-defense — that this was deliverance, not looting. To speak this passage is to announce that the doomed day became the day of rescue, to feel the weight of an empire tilting, and to mark — by what is *not* taken — the restraint that sets this victory apart.

**Significant Absence**

**3E — What Happens**
[[B17-The-Jews]] The Jews struck all their enemies with the stroke of the sword — killing and destroying — and dealt with those who hated them as they pleased. In [[PL1-Susa-the-citadel]] Susa the citadel the Jews killed five hundred men, and also the ten sons of [[B3-Haman]] Haman: Parshandatha, Dalphon, Aspatha, Poratha, Adalia, Aridatha, Parmashta, Arisai, Aridai, and Vaizatha — the ten sons of Haman son of Hammedatha, the foe of [[B17-The-Jews]] the Jews. But on the plunder they did not lay their hand.

**3F — Communicative Purpose**
Reports the striking of the enemies, tallies the five hundred dead in the capital, names Haman's ten slain sons in full, and seals the scene with the refusal of the spoil.

**Significant Absence**
Again no god is named over the victory, and no battle-cry, no song, no triumph-speech is recorded. And the loudest absence is an action *withheld*: the plunder the decree had granted them is expressly not taken — the "did not lay their hand" that the next pericope will repeat, marking the Jews' self-restraint as the moral center of the deliverance.
