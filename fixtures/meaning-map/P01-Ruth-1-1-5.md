# P01 — Ruth 1:1–5

## 1. Metadata
- **Pericope title:** The famine, the family's sojourn, and the emptying of the household
- **BCV:** Ruth 1:1–5
- **Genre Group:** NARRATIVE
- **Genre:** HISTORICAL_NARRATIVE
- **Register:** INFORMAL_CASUAL

**Multi-level register tagging:**
Pericope-level default is INFORMAL_CASUAL, the unmarked default register for biblical narrator voice. The narrator chronicles community memory in relaxed storytelling form. One local shift occurs at v.1: the stylized formula vayhi bi-yemei ("and it happened in the days of") carries an elevated narrative framing — COMMUNITY_MEMORY (a NARRATIVE_FRAMING value, not a register). The register itself stays INFORMAL_CASUAL throughout; v.3 and v.5 are tightly compressed chronicle reports of deaths.

## 2. Level 1 — Whole-Passage Movement
### 2.1 Prose Arc / Shape / Argument / Burden / Concern
The passage moves from a wide historical frame into a single family's compressed exile and emptying. The arc traces famine in the homeland, departure to a foreign land, the naming of a household of four, the death of the father, marriages of the two sons to foreign women, ten years passing in foreign soil, and finally the deaths of both sons. The burden is the steady contraction from a complete Israelite household to one bereaved woman alive among foreigners. The concern is whether anything of this family — its name, its line, its future — can survive past the woman who remains.

### 2.2 Context
This is the opening of the book of Ruth, set in the era of the judges, an era the audience associates with disorder and fragmented leadership. A famine drives an Ephrathite family from Bethlehem of Judah, whose name means "house of bread," into the foreign country of Moab, a people whose history with Israel is fraught. No characters have been established before this passage; the audience enters the book here. The whole-book questions of redemption, lineage preservation, hesed, and outsider incorporation hang over the passage but are not yet engaged.

### 2.3 Emotion / Tone / Pace
The dominant tone is restrained and chronicle-like, with weight gathering through compression rather than expression. The narrator does not pause for grief, does not describe inner states, and does not name divine cause for any event. Each verse adds loss to loss, and the deaths are reported in verbs as terse as the marriages were brief. The closing mood is one of quiet desolation — three deaths in five verses, with the final clause leaving one woman bereft on every side at once, and any feeling carried by what the line does not say.

### 2.4 Communicative Function
The passage opens the book by naming the situation that the rest of the book must answer: a household emptied of its men, a widow emptied of her future, an Israelite family stranded in foreign exile. It transmits the founding loss of the narrative and plants the questions of lineage, inheritance, and survival that will drive every later scene. Speaking the passage establishes the people, the place, the era, and the absence around which the story will turn. Its function is foundational: a community memory of how the story's emptying began.

**3C — Objects and Elements**
מוֹת שְׁנֵיהֶם / death of both sons
- What it is: the dying of [[B4-Mahlon]] Mahlon and [[B5-Chilion]] Chilion together
- Function in scene: removes both sons from the household
- Signals: the apparent end of the family line

שְׁנֵיהֶם / "both of them" — totality marker
- What it is: the totality-marking phrase that signals nothing remains of the sons
- Function in scene: completes the subtraction of the male line in a single phrase
- Signals: catastrophic completion; nothing partial about the loss

תִּשָּאֵר הָאִשָּה / "the woman remained"
- What it is: the linguistic frame marking the survivor after a loss
- Function in scene: marks [[B3-Naomi]] Naomi as the sole survivor after the cumulative subtractions
- Signals: image-rhyme completion with v.3; this time the remaining is total

הָאִשָּה / "the woman" — stripped reference for Naomi
- What it is: the narrator's reduction of Naomi's reference to a bare common noun at the moment of complete bereavement
- Function in scene: defamiliarizes the protagonist at the height of loss; reduces her to a category before restoring her name at 1:6
- Signals: structural naming-down marker; the protagonist becomes anonymous at her emptiest moment

יְלָדֶיהָ / "her boys" — tender child-form
- What it is: the diminutive child-form (yeladim) used instead of the adult-son form (banim)
- Function in scene: reframes the loss as the loss of children, not of adult heirs
- Signals: tonal shift toward maternal grief without naming it; tender form preserved against the chronicle register

מִשְּׁנֵי יְלָדֶיהָ וּמֵאִישָּהּ / "bereft of her two children and her husband" — cumulative loss listing
- What it is: the terse summative phrase for what [[B3-Naomi]] Naomi has lost, in a single compressed listing
- Function in scene: counts every loss together in one breath; reverses the natural order of children outliving parents
- Signals: catastrophic, complete emptying; single-phrase compression must be preserved at reconstruction

מָוֶת אֱלִימֶלֶך (referenced) / death of Elimelech (referenced from v.3)
- What it is: the prior loss of [[B3-Naomi]] Naomi's husband, summed into v.5's bereavement phrase
- Function in scene: included in v.5's count of what [[B3-Naomi]] Naomi has lost
- Signals: cumulative weight of three deaths

**3D — Times**
- None: no distinct temporal frame for this scene; time is continuous within the Moabite sojourn.

**3E — What Happens**
[[B4-Mahlon]] Mahlon and [[B5-Chilion]] Chilion both die, and [[B3-Naomi]] Naomi is left without her two children and her husband.

**3F — Communicative Purpose**
Records the catastrophic emptying that closes the opening pericope.

**Significant Absence**
The narrator records no grief, no mourning rituals, no successor, and no divine action. The losses are reported and the line stops there.
