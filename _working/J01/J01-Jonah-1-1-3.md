# J01 — Jonah 1:1–3

## 1. Metadata
- **Pericope title:** The word comes to Jonah, and Jonah flees
- **BCV:** Jonah 1:1–3
- **Genre Group:** NARRATIVE
- **Genre:** HISTORICAL_NARRATIVE
- **Register:** INFORMAL_CASUAL

**Multi-level register tagging:**
The whole passage sits in INFORMAL_CASUAL, the plain default for the biblical narrator's voice — the relaxed, everyday way an old story gets told. One scene-level override falls in v.2: YHWH's direct command ("Arise, go… and call out") lifts into ELDER_AUTHORITY — the authoritative voice of a superior giving an order. The narrator's voice around it stays INFORMAL_CASUAL; v.3 reports the flight in a tight run of plain verbs.

## 2. Level 1 — Whole-Passage Movement
### 2.1 Prose Arc / Shape / Argument / Burden / Concern
The passage opens with a command and answers it with a flight. The word of YHWH comes to Jonah: get up, go east to Nineveh, the great city, and cry out against it. Jonah gets up — but to run the other way. He heads west for Tarshish, the far edge of the world, away from before YHWH; he goes down to the port at Joppa, finds a ship bound for Tarshish, pays his way, and goes down into it to sail off with the crew, away from before YHWH. What weighs on the passage is a prophet running from his God in the very breath he is called. The question it leaves wide open is why he runs — the narrator never says — and whether anyone can get away from before YHWH at all.

### 2.2 Context
This is where the book of Jonah begins. Jonah son of Amittai is the one the word comes to; the story names him and his father and nothing more of who he is. Nineveh is "the great city" he is sent to — a far-off, foreign city to the east. Tarshish is the opposite direction, the distant west, the way a man goes to put as much world as he can between himself and the place he was sent. The questions the whole book will turn on — God's reach over the nations, mercy for an enemy city, and whether a called man can refuse — are already in the air here, in the gap between the command and the run. *(The fuller weight of Nineveh as the great enemy city, and of Tarshish as the world's far end, is book-context the BCD/Agent 1 supplies; the bare text gives "the great city" and the two directions.)*

### 2.3 Emotion / Tone / Pace
The tone is plain and fast, and the force of it is in the turn. The command is four quick verbs; the flight is a tumble of them — got up, to flee, went down, found, paid, went down — all in one breath, all moving the wrong way. The narrator never tells us what Jonah feels, never lets Jonah say a word, and never explains the running. The pace drops like the verbs do: down to the port, down into the ship, down and away. By the end the mood is a quiet alarm — a man in full motion, fleeing the one place a prophet cannot flee — and whatever feeling there is rides on what the line refuses to say.

### 2.4 Communicative Function
The passage opens the book by setting the command against the refusal: a clear charge from YHWH, and a prophet who answers it with his heels. It hands on the first turn the whole story is built on — the flight — and plants the questions the rest of the book has to answer: why a called man runs, whether he can get away, and what a God does with a prophet who will not go. To speak this passage is to set in place the caller, the called, the city he is sent to, the far place he runs to, and the silence where his reason should be. This is the groundwork: how the running began. The form is plain narrative, but its mode is already ironic and teaching: the one man called to carry God's word is the one who runs from it, and the book makes its point through that reversal.

## 3. Level 2 — Scenes / Episodes

### Scene 1 — The word of YHWH comes to Jonah (v.1–2)

**3A — Beings**
[[B1-Jonah]] — יוֹנָה / Jonah
- Role: the one the word comes to; the prophet who is sent
- Relationship: son of [[B3-Amittai]] Amittai
- Presence: PRESENT
- Referential form: named plainly as "Jonah son of Amittai" — given, with no title, office, or background

[[B2-YHWH]] — יְהוָה / YHWH
- Role: the one whose word comes; the one who sends
- Relationship: the God who calls Jonah and names the errand
- Presence: PRESENT

[[B3-Amittai]] — אֲמִתַּי / Amittai
- Role: fixes who Jonah is by naming his father
- Relationship: father of [[B1-Jonah]] Jonah
- Presence: REFERENCED

[[B5-People-of-Nineveh]] — (רָעָתָם) / "their" (the people of Nineveh)
- Role: the ones whose evil is the reason for the errand
- Relationship: the inhabitants of [[PL1-Nineveh]] Nineveh
- Presence: REFERENCED

**3B — Places**
[[PL1-Nineveh]] — נִינְוֵה / Nineveh
- Role: the place Jonah is sent to
- Type: foreign city
- Effect on scene: it is the errand — the great city Jonah is told to go to and cry against, and the city he runs from going to

**3C — Objects and Concepts**

[[CB_0051-Evil-of-Nineveh]] — רָעָתָם / their evil
- What it is: the wrongdoing of the people of Nineveh
- Function in scene: it is the reason the errand is given — it has "come up before" YHWH
- Cross-ref: [[CB_0052-Before-the-Face-of-YHWH]] (it comes up before YHWH's face)

[[CB_0052-Before-the-Face-of-YHWH]] — לְפָנַי / before my face
- What it is: the presence of YHWH — being in front of him, in his sight
- Function in scene: where Nineveh's evil has "come up to"; the same presence Jonah will run from in Scene 2

**3D — Times**
- None: no separate time-setting for this scene. The opening word comes with no date or era named.

**3E — What Happens**
The word of [[B2-YHWH]] YHWH comes to [[B1-Jonah]] Jonah son of [[B3-Amittai]] Amittai, telling him to get up, go to [[PL1-Nineveh]] Nineveh the great city, and cry out against it, because the evil of its people has come up before YHWH.

**3F — Communicative Purpose**
Gives the command that the rest of the pericope answers: the caller, the called, the city, and the reason.

**Significant Absence**
The narrator names "their evil" but never says what it is — the wrongdoing is asserted, not described. Nothing is told of who Jonah is or why he is the one sent.

### Scene 2 — Jonah flees toward Tarshish (v.3)

**3A — Beings**
[[B1-Jonah]] — יוֹנָה / Jonah
- Role: the called one who flees instead of going
- Relationship: son of [[B3-Amittai]] Amittai; the one [[B2-YHWH]] YHWH sent
- Presence: PRESENT

[[B2-YHWH]] — (מִלִּפְנֵי יְהוָה) / YHWH (the presence fled from)
- Role: the one Jonah flees away from
- Relationship: the God who sent Jonah
- Presence: REFERENCED

[[B4-Ship-Crew]] — (עִמָּהֶם) / "them" (the ship's crew)
- Role: the ones Jonah means to sail away with
- Relationship: the people aboard the [[O1-Ship]] ship bound for Tarshish
- Presence: REFERENCED

**3B — Places**
[[PL2-Tarshish]] — תַּרְשִׁישׁ / Tarshish
- Role: the place Jonah flees toward
- Type: distant foreign place (the far west; the opposite way from Nineveh)
- Effect on scene: it is the run — named three times, the destination of the flight away from the errand

[[PL3-Joppa]] — יָפוֹ / Joppa
- Role: the port Jonah goes down to
- Type: coastal town
- Effect on scene: the way out by sea — where Jonah finds the ship

**3C — Objects and Concepts**

[[O1-Ship]] — אָנִיָּה / ship
- What it is: a seagoing vessel bound for Tarshish
- Function in scene: the means of the flight — Jonah finds it, pays for it, and goes down into it

[[O2-Fare]] — שְׂכָרָהּ / its fare
- What it is: the price of passage on the ship
- Function in scene: Jonah pays it — he buys his way onto the flight

[[CB_0052-Before-the-Face-of-YHWH]] — מִלִּפְנֵי יְהוָה / from before YHWH
- What it is: the presence of YHWH — the same presence Nineveh's evil came up to in Scene 1
- Function in scene: what Jonah flees away from — stated twice, opening and closing the verse

**3D — Times**
- None: no separate time-setting; the flight runs straight on from the command.

**3E — What Happens**
[[B1-Jonah]] Jonah gets up to flee toward [[PL2-Tarshish]] Tarshish, away from before [[B2-YHWH]] YHWH. He goes down to [[PL3-Joppa]] Joppa, finds an [[O1-Ship]] ship bound for Tarshish, pays its [[O2-Fare]] fare, and goes down into it to sail with the [[B4-Ship-Crew]] crew toward Tarshish, away from before YHWH.

**3F — Communicative Purpose**
Records the flight: the prophet's refusal, carried out as a journey the opposite way.

**Significant Absence**
The narrator gives no reason for the flight. Jonah says nothing — no refusal, no argument, no prayer. Why he runs is left entirely unsaid.
