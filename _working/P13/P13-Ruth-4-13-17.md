# P13 — Ruth 4:13-17

## 1. Metadata
- **Pericope title:** Obed: the conception YHWH gives, the women's blessing, the child on Naomi's lap
- **BCV:** Ruth 4:13-17
- **Genre Group:** NARRATIVE
- **Genre:** HISTORICAL_NARRATIVE
- **Register:** INFORMAL_CASUAL

*(Versification note: Ruth's chapter and verse numbers are the same in English and Hebrew — no divergence in this pericope.)*

**Multi-level register tagging:**
Pericope-level INFORMAL_CASUAL — the narrator's telling of the marriage, the birth, and the naming. Scene 2, the women's blessing to Naomi (4:14–15), lifts to CEREMONIAL: a set "blessed be YHWH" benediction invoking the divine name, the women's counterpart to the men's blessing at the gate. Scene 3, Naomi taking the child and the neighbor-women naming him (4:16–17), settles to INTIMATE: the close domestic moment of a grandmother and a child, and the neighborhood women speaking over the cradle.

## 2. Level 1 — Whole-Passage Movement

### 2.1 Prose Arc / Shape / Argument / Burden / Concern
The passage is the book's emptiness reversed. Boaz takes Ruth, she becomes his wife, he comes to her — and YHWH gives her conception, and she bears a son. After a book of human acts — gleaning, plans, a night, a gate — the second of the story's only two direct acts of God falls here: the LORD gives the child. The neighbor-women turn to Naomi, not to the parents, and bless: blessed be YHWH, who has not left you without a redeemer today; may his name be called out in Israel. He will be a restorer of your life and a sustainer of your old age — for your daughter-in-law, who loves you, who is better to you than seven sons, has borne him. Naomi takes the child, sets him on her bosom, and becomes his nurse. And the women of the neighborhood call out a name, saying a son has been born to Naomi — and they name him Obed; he is the father of Jesse, the father of David. What weighs on the passage is the turn from death to a living name and from empty to full: the woman who came back saying YHWH had emptied her now holds a child the women call hers, and the line everyone feared was cut off has its name spoken into Israel. The question the book leaves is the one the last line answers past the story's own horizon: where this small, redeemed line is going.

### 2.2 Context
This closes the book by answering its beginning. Naomi left full and came back empty (1:21); now the women fill her arms. The redeemer-word, which traveled from Naomi's first recognition (2:20) through the night (3:9) and the gate (4:1–10), lands on the child himself — he is the redeemer the LORD has not left her without. The blessing the men spoke at the gate (4:11–12) is answered by the women's blessing here, the two framing the resolution; and the divine name, returned at the gate's blessing, sounds again in the women's. The "better than seven sons" measure overturns the book's losses — Ruth, the foreign daughter-in-law, outweighs the fullness of a complete house. The women name the child for Naomi, as they failed to place her at her bitter return (1:19); the naming-by-the-women that opened in grief closes in joy. And the last line lifts the whole book past itself: Obed, father of Jesse, father of David — the small Bethlehem household stands at the head of the king's line.

### 2.3 Emotion / Tone / Pace
Swift, then full, then still and lifting. The opening verse moves fast — take, become, come, give, bear — the agents shifting in a breath, Boaz to YHWH to Ruth, the years of waiting resolved in one line. The women's blessing opens the passage wide and warm: redeemer, restorer, sustainer, a love better than seven sons. Then it slows to its tenderest image — an old woman with a child against her chest. And the naming closes it quietly and then suddenly vast: a son for Naomi, his name Obed — and then, in the narrator's own later voice, the names that turn a family story into the prologue of a kingdom. The mood is restoration: grief named at the start is answered, fullness returned, and the horizon opened.

### 2.4 Communicative Function
This passage resolves every thread the book has carried. It delivers the child as the gift of YHWH — the second of the story's two direct divine acts — and lays the line's whole future on a single birth. It lands the redeemer-word on the child, answering the legal redemption with a living one. It answers the men's gate-blessing with the women's domestic blessing, and returns Ruth and Naomi to the scene the gate kept them out of. It overturns the book's emptiness — empty to full, dead line to living name, foreign widow to better-than-seven-sons. It closes the communal-naming the book opened in Naomi's bitter homecoming. And in its last line it reaches past the story to David, making the inclusio with the prologue and opening onto the genealogy. To speak this passage is to keep the swiftness of the gift, the warmth of the blessing, the tenderness of the lap, and the sudden reach of the closing names.

- Relationship: the Moabite widow of Mahlon, now Boaz's wife and a mother

**3C — Objects and Concepts**

- None: no distinct temporal frame; the marriage, the conception, and the birth run as one swift sequence.

**3E — What Happens**
[[B13-Boaz]] Boaz takes [[B9-Ruth]] Ruth, and she becomes his wife, and he comes to her; and [[B10-YHWH]] YHWH gives her conception, and she bears a son.

**3F — Communicative Purpose**
Resolves the marriage and the waiting in a single swift line, with YHWH's gift of conception at its center — the book's second direct act of God.

**Significant Absence**
The narrator gives no wedding, no span of time, no description of the birth — only the bare swift verbs. And the child is not yet named or claimed; the women's word in the next verses does that, and not by the parents.

- What it is: the genealogy's terminal name — the king the line runs to
- Function in scene: the narrator's reach past the story; the household stands at the head of the royal line

**3D — Times**
- None as the scene's frame: the naming follows the birth; the closing "father of Jesse, father of David" is the narrator's reach to a later time, not a setting within the scene.

**3E — What Happens**
[[B3-Naomi]] Naomi takes the child, sets him on her bosom, and becomes his nurse. [[B24-Women-of-Bethlehem]] The neighbor-women call out a name for him, saying a son has been born to Naomi; and they call his name [[B25-Obed]] Obed. He is the father of [[B26-Jesse-and-David]] Jesse, the father of David.

**3F — Communicative Purpose**
Closes the book's reversal at its tenderest and then its widest: the child against Naomi's chest, named for her by the women, and the names that open onto David.

**Significant Absence**
Ruth is not in this last scene — the child is on Naomi's lap and reckoned a son to Naomi, the mother stepped out of the closing frame. Boaz, too, is gone from the telling after 4:13. And the narrator names David without a word of who he is or will be — the weight of the name is left for the audience to carry.
